     <p><b>Concordia University</b> (commonly referred to as <b>Concordia</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Canadian" title="Canadian" class="mw-redirect">Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public research university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Founded in 1974 following the merger of <a href="/wiki/Loyola_College_(Montreal)" title="Loyola College (Montreal)">Loyola College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Williams_University" title="Sir George Williams University" class="mw-redirect">Sir George Williams University</a>, Concordia is one of the two universities in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> where English is the primary language of instruction. As of the 2011-2012 academic year, there were 45,954 students enrolled at Concordia,<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> making the university among the largest in Canada by enrollment. The university has two campuses, set approximately seven&nbsp;km apart: Sir George Williams Campus in the downtown core of <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, in an area known as <a href="/wiki/Quartier_Concordia" title="Quartier Concordia">Quartier Concordia</a> and Loyola Campus in the residential district of <a href="/wiki/Notre-Dame-de-Gr%C3%A2ce" title="Notre-Dame-de-Gr&acirc;ce">Notre-Dame-de-Gr&acirc;ce</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tour-our-campuses_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tour-our-campuses-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> With four faculties, a school of <a href="/wiki/Graduate_studies" title="Graduate studies" class="mw-redirect">graduate studies</a> and numerous colleges, centres and institutes, Concordia offers over 300 undergraduate and 100 graduate programs and courses.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Concordia was ranked 7th among Canadian universities in the International Professional Classification of Higher Education Institutions, a worldwide ranking compiled by the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Mines_de_Paris" title="&Eacute;cole des Mines de Paris" class="mw-redirect">&Eacute;cole des Mines de Paris</a> taking into account the number of graduates occupying the rank of <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer" class="mw-redirect">Chief Executive Officer</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> companies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup>The university was ranked 13th among Canada's comprehensive universities in the <a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a> 22nd annual rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Internationally, Concordia was ranked 501-550th overall in the 2012 <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a>. Nationally, the 2012 Higher Education Strategy Associates' university rankings placed Concordia 9th in the field of <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> and 20th in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The university's <a href="/wiki/John_Molson_School_of_Business" title="John Molson School of Business">John Molson School of Business</a> is consistently ranked within the top ten Canadian <a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">business schools</a>, and within the top 100 worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Aspen_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aspen-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BGP_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGP-16"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CK2008_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CK2008-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CK2009_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CK2009-18"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Concordia is a <a href="/wiki/Non-sectarian" title="Non-sectarian" class="mw-redirect">non-sectarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coeducational" title="Coeducational" class="mw-redirect">coeducational</a> institution, with more than 25,000 alumni living outside of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup>The university is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Universities_and_Colleges_of_Canada" title="Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada">Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Universities" title="International Association of Universities">International Association of Universities</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Commonwealth_Universities" title="Association of Commonwealth Universities">Association of Commonwealth Universities</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Theological_Schools_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada">Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_University_Press" title="Canadian University Press">Canadian University Press</a>. The university's varsity teams, known as the <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Stingers" title="Concordia Stingers">Stingers</a>, compete in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Although the roots of its founding institutions go back more than 160 years, Concordia University was formed on August 24, 1974 through the merger of Loyola College (1896) and Sir George Williams University (1926). Since its inception, Concordia has changed its logo 4 times.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loyola_College">Loyola College</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Loyola College">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Loyola_College_(Montreal)" title="Loyola College (Montreal)">Loyola College (Montreal)</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Loyola_college_1937.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Loyola_college_1937.jpg/220px-Loyola_college_1937.jpg" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Loyola_college_1937.jpg/330px-Loyola_college_1937.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Loyola_college_1937.jpg/440px-Loyola_college_1937.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Loyola_college_1937.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Loyola College in 1937.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Loyola College was founded on <a href="/wiki/Sherbrooke_Street" title="Sherbrooke Street">Sherbrooke Street West</a> in 1896 as an English-language program of the Jesuit <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_Sainte-Marie_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Coll&egrave;ge Sainte-Marie de Montr&eacute;al">Coll&egrave;ge Sainte-Marie de Montr&eacute;al</a> (since merged into <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_du_Qu%C3%A9bec_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Universit&eacute; du Qu&eacute;bec &agrave; Montr&eacute;al">Universit&eacute; du Qu&eacute;bec &agrave; Montr&eacute;al</a>). It was originally located at the Sacred Heart Convent in downtown Montreal. The college moved into the present west-end campus in 1916. Although founded as a <i>coll&egrave;ge classique</i> (the forerunners of Quebec's <a href="/wiki/College_education_in_Quebec" title="College education in Quebec">college system</a>), Loyola began granting university degrees through <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Laval" title="Universit&eacute; Laval" class="mw-redirect">Universit&eacute; Laval</a> as early as 1906. By 1940, <i>coll&egrave;ge classique</i> programs were gone and Loyola became a four-year university, although it never obtained its own charter, granting its degrees through Laval or, after 1920, the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Universit&eacute; de Montr&eacute;al">Universit&eacute; de Montr&eacute;al</a>.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Loyola_Chapel">Loyola Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Loyola Chapel">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Loyola_College_(Montreal)#Loyola_Chapel" title="Loyola College (Montreal)">Loyola College Chapel</a> chapel is a place of Catholic denomination worship for students, personnel as well as their families. The chapel is a classic example of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_revival_architecture" title="Gothic revival architecture" class="mw-redirect">gothic revival architecture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Kelsey" title="Charles William Kelsey">Charles William Kelsey</a> created a twelve light stained glass war memorial window (1933) in the Loyola chapel. The windows depict the torture of the priests by North American Indians. In the dedication panel is written: &quot;To the Greater Glory of God and In Memory of the Officers and Men of the 55th Irish Canadian Rangers.&quot; The Loyola Chapel has been converted into a theatre named the F.C. Smith Auditorium.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sir_George_Williams_University">Sir George Williams University</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sir George Williams University">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Sir_george_william_1970.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sir_george_william_1970.jpg/220px-Sir_george_william_1970.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sir_george_william_1970.jpg/330px-Sir_george_william_1970.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sir_george_william_1970.jpg/440px-Sir_george_william_1970.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Sir_george_william_1970.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Sir George Williams University's Henry F. Hall Building in 1970.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In 1851, the first <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a> in North America was established<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> on Ste-Helene street in Old Montreal. Sixty years later, it relocated to its current location on <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Street_(Montreal)" title="Stanley Street (Montreal)">Stanley Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Montreal" title="Downtown Montreal">Downtown Montreal</a>, in a district now known as <a href="/wiki/Quartier_Concordia" title="Quartier Concordia">Quartier Concordia</a>. From its early years, the YMCA offered evening classes to allow working people in the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_Quebecer" title="English-speaking Quebecer">English-speaking community</a> to pursue their education while working during the day. In 1926, the education program was re-organized as Sir George Williams College, named after <a href="/wiki/George_Williams_(YMCA)" title="George Williams (YMCA)">George Williams</a>, founder of the YMCA. In 1934, Sir George Williams College offered the first undergraduate credit course in adult education in Canada.</p> 
     <p>The College became Sir George Williams University (SGWU) in 1948, when it received a university charter from the provincial government, though it remained the education arm of the Montreal YMCA. SGWU expanded into its first standalone building, the Norris Building, in 1956. It established a Centre for Human Relations and Community Studies in 1963. SGWU continued to hold classes in the YMCA building until the construction of the Hall Building in 1966. The university gained international attention in 1969, when a group of students occupied the Hall Building's 9th floor computer lab (see <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Williams_Computer_Riot" title="Sir George Williams Computer Riot">Sir George Williams Computer Riot</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1974.E2.80.93present">1974?present</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1974?present">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The merger of Loyola College and Sir George Williams University was recommended in 1969 by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_commission" title="Royal commission" class="mw-redirect">Parent Commission</a>, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularization</a> of Quebec's educational system (see <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a>). In August 1974, the two schools would follow the commission's recommendations and merge, thus creating Concordia University.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The name was taken from the motto of the city of Montreal, <i>Concordia salus</i> (meaning 'well-being through harmony').<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1968, in the wake of the Parent Commission Report, the Quebec government asked Loyola and Sir George Williams to consider some form of union. Negotiations began in 1969 and continued on and off over the next four years.</p> 
     <p>While a number of possible models were considered, including that of a loose federation, the solution finally adopted was that of an integrated institution, Concordia University, operating under the existing Sir George Williams charter. The legal existence of Concordia dates from August 24, 1974.</p> 
     <p>Today, the school is one of three English-language universities in the province of Quebec. The other two institutions are <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_University" title="Bishop's University">Bishop's University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sherbrooke" title="Sherbrooke">Sherbrooke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill University</a>, also in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>.</p> 
     <p>Concordia University is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Universities_and_Colleges_of_Canada" title="Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada">Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Universities" title="International Association of Universities">International Association of Universities</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Commonwealth_Universities" title="Association of Commonwealth Universities">Association of Commonwealth Universities</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Theological_Schools_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada">Association of Theological Schools in the United States and Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Association_of_Research_Libraries" title="Canadian Association of Research Libraries">Canadian Association of Research Libraries</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Student_Sport_Federation" title="Quebec Student Sport Federation">Quebec Student Sport Federation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_University_Society_for_Intercollegiate_Debate" title="Canadian University Society for Intercollegiate Debate">Canadian University Society for Intercollegiate Debate</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Bureau_for_International_Education" title="Canadian Bureau for International Education">Canadian Bureau for International Education</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_University_Press" title="Canadian University Press">Canadian University Press</a>.</p>

They are connected by free shuttle-bus service for students, faculty and staff.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries.2C_Archives_and_Galleries">Libraries, Archives and Galleries</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Libraries, Archives and Galleries">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Concordia_University_Libraries" title="Concordia University Libraries">Concordia University Libraries</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Concordia University has two library locations, Webster Library located in the McConnell Building of the Sir George Williams Campus and Vanier Library on the Loyola Campus. Concordia Libraries house several special and unique collections including the Azrieli Holocaust Collection and the Irving Layton Collection.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Most Special Collections are located in the Vanier Library. The Libraries also maintains the University's institutional repository, Spectrum.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The Concordia Libraries are members of the Canadian Association of Research Libraries.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Corcordian University's Hall Building houses The Leonard &amp; Bina Ellen Art Gallery. Samuel Schecter, an art enthusiast and businessman, set up two funds in 1962 to be used for the purchase of Canadian art at <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Williams_University" title="Sir George Williams University" class="mw-redirect">Sir George Williams University</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Loyola_College_(Montreal)" title="Loyola College (Montreal)">Loyola College (Montreal)</a>. When Sir George Williams University and Loyola College merged under the name Concordia in 1974 their respective art collections were also combined. The Collection of the Leonard &amp; Bina Ellen Gallery consists of 1700 paintings, sculptures, prints, photographs and videos, many of the works by 20th-century Canadian artists. <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><br /></p> 
     <p>The Concordia University Archives house official records of, or relating to, or people/activities connected with Concordia University and its two founding institutions. The collection consists of manuscripts, texts, photographs, audio-visual material and artifacts. <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_buildings">New buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New buildings">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Completed in 2009, the MB Building houses the <a href="/wiki/John_Molson_School_of_Business" title="John Molson School of Business">John Molson School of Business</a>. In 2001, Concordia embarked on a mission to develop and expand the quality of the downtown campus, and to revive the west end in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>.</p> 
     <p>The university has also acquired the historic <a href="/wiki/Grey_Nuns" title="Grey Nuns">Grey Nuns</a> <a href="/wiki/Motherhouse" title="Motherhouse">motherhouse</a> near its Sir George Williams Campus,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> for $18 million. Built in 1871, it would alone double the size of the current downtown campus. From 2007 to 2022, the university will begin occupying the building in 4 separate phases. The large property will house the faculty of Fine Arts and possibly the Mel Hoppenheim School of Cinema, and other departments. Currently the Grey Nuns building is only partially owned by Concordia (about 1/3 of the building on Saint-Mathieu Road), however full control of the building will be given to Concordia University in 2011. Concordia Residence Life currently houses nearly 250 students each year in the Grey Nuns building. The dorm-rooms are among the largest in the country, as many of the rooms have been transformed from when the section of the Grey Nuns building was occupied by the Grey Nuns. The site was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Sites_of_Canada" title="National Historic Sites of Canada">National Historic Site of Canada</a> in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>The Integrated Engineering, Computer Science and Visual Arts Complex at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Catherine_Street" title="Saint Catherine Street">Saint Catherine Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guy_Street" title="Guy Street">Guy Street</a> was opened in September 2005. The building is directly connected to the <a href="/wiki/Guy-Concordia_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Guy-Concordia (Montreal Metro)">Guy-Concordia metro station</a> and also houses Le Gym, a facility of Athletics and Recreation. Across the street, the 100-year-old <a href="/wiki/TD_Canada_Trust" title="TD Canada Trust">TD Canada Trust</a> building was donated to Concordia in 2005 by the <a href="/wiki/Toronto-Dominion_Bank" title="Toronto-Dominion Bank">Toronto-Dominion Bank</a>. The university had planned to begin using this space in 2006.</p> 
     <p>Construction of the new <a href="/wiki/John_Molson_School_of_Business" title="John Molson School of Business">John Molson School of Business</a> Building that is located on the corner of Guy and de Maisonneuve streets began in February 2007. The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education,_Recreation_and_Sports_(Quebec)" title="Ministry of Education, Recreation and Sports (Quebec)">Quebec Minister of Education, Recreation and Sports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Fournier" title="Jean-Marc Fournier">Jean-Marc Fournier</a>, on October 30, 2006 announced an investment of $60 million towards the construction of the new building. The minister made the announcement during a ceremony at Concordia. The government's $60 million represents about half of the total construction costs. Construction started on January 22, 2006 and the building was completed and opened in September 2009. The fifteen-story building now houses the JMSB's 6,000 full and part-time students under the same roof for the very first time. The dance and theatre departments at Concordia have also moved into the new JMSB building. It is connected to the EV building by a tunnel under Guy Street.</p> 
     <p>In April 2010, a 120-metre tunnel completed the underground connections of the Guy-Concordia metro station with the Hall Building and the McConnell Library building.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quartier_Concordia">Quartier Concordia</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Quartier Concordia">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Quartier Concordia is a neighbourhood redevelopment project centred around <a href="/wiki/Concordia_University_(Montreal)" title="Concordia University (Montreal)" class="mw-redirect">Concordia University</a>'s Sir George Williams campus in downtown <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, Canada. Bordered by <a href="/wiki/Sherbrooke_Street" title="Sherbrooke Street">Sherbrooke Street</a>, Saint-Mathieu Street, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_L%C3%A9vesque_Boulevard" title="Ren&eacute; L&eacute;vesque Boulevard">Ren&eacute; L&eacute;vesque Boulevard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Street" title="Bishop Street">Bishop Street</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> the district is designed to be a green urban campus that will improve the use and quality of public places and spaces, student life on campus and transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>As part of the redesign, the small <a href="/wiki/Norman_Bethune_Square" title="Norman Bethune Square">Norman Bethune Square</a> has been redesigned and enlarged. Sidewalks in the area will also be widened, with additional trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Bethune_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bethune-36"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>As of September 2010, an underground tunnel links the university's Hall and J.W. McConnell buildings with the <a href="/wiki/Guy-Concordia_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Guy-Concordia (Montreal Metro)">Guy-Concordia metro station</a>. The hallway was completed in Spring 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Riga_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Riga-37"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> However, a project to create a green space on <a href="/wiki/Mackay_Street" title="Mackay Street">Mackay Street</a> has been put on hold.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackay_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackay-38"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>Students enter the university in September, or, in some cases, in January or May. An undergraduate degree normally takes three or four years studying full-time to complete, a Master's takes from a year and a half to three years, and a Ph.D. is at least four years long. Certificates and diplomas usually take no longer than a year and a half to complete.</p> 
     <p>Concordia has more than 285 <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect">undergraduate</a> programs, divided into four faculties.<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The faculties are the Faculty of Arts and Science, the Faculty of Engineering and Computer Science, the Faculty of Fine Arts and the John Molson School of Business Students are normally enrolled in one of these Faculties, but they may take courses from any of the others as part of their studies. Class sizes vary from 85-400 students.</p> 
     <p>The School of Graduate Studies offers about 70 programs leading to Master's and doctoral degrees, as well as graduate diplomas and certificates for professionals seeking to upgrade their knowledge and skills.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The School of Extended Learning offers programs and services designed to make it easier for students to attend the university and be successful at their studies.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://co-op.concordia.ca">Institute for Co-operative Education</a> administers more than 30 bachelor's and master's programs in an alternating co-op work study format. Concordia's co-op programs enable students to enrich their learning by participating in career-relevant 12-17 week paid work terms.Depending on their faculty and major, co-op students will usually graduate with a minimum of 12 months of academically relevant work experience. Concordia is a member of the Canadian Association for Co-operative Education <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://cafce.ca">(CAFCE)</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculty_of_Arts_and_Science">Faculty of Arts and Science</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Faculty of Arts and Science">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Concordia University's Faculty of Arts and Science contains 21 departments in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a> at the undergraduate and graduate levels. There are over 293 programs,<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> offering more than 2,400 courses. There are 500 full-time and 400 part-time faculty members.<sup id="cite_ref-FASDepartments_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FASDepartments-41"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> During the 2010-2011 academic year, there were 15,767 undergraduate and 2,103 graduate students enrolled in the faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In addition to regular academic programs, the Faculty of Arts and Science also includes three colleges, two schools and one institute. These are the Liberal Arts College, the Loyola College for Diversity and Sustainability, the School of Community and Public Affairs, the School of Canadian Irish Studies, the Science College and the <a href="/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir" title="Simone de Beauvoir">Simone de Beauvoir</a> Institute.<sup id="cite_ref-CollegesSchoolsInstitutes_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CollegesSchoolsInstitutes-42"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Loyola College for Diversity and Sustainability (formerly Loyola International College) is an <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect">interdisciplinary</a> college of Concordia University on the Loyola campus, the original site of <a href="/wiki/Loyola_College_(Montreal)" title="Loyola College (Montreal)">Loyola College</a>. It offers minor programs in &quot;Diversity and the Contemporary World&quot; and &quot;Sustainability Studies&quot;.</p> 
     <p>At the undergraduate level, the Faculty of Arts and Science offers both <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> (B.A.) and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (BSc) programs with majors ranging from <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> to actuarial mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculty_of_Engineering_and_Computer_Science">Faculty of Engineering and Computer Science</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Faculty of Engineering and Computer Science">edit</a>]</span></h3>

offers 86 undergraduate and graduate-level programs<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> in the following href="/wiki/Industrial_Engineering" title="Industrial Engineering" class="mw-redirect">Industrial Engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_Systems_Security" title="Information Systems Security" class="mw-redirect">Information Systems Security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_Engineering" title="Mechanical Engineering" class="mw-redirect">Mechanical Engineering</a>, <a      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculty_of_Fine_Arts">Faculty of Fine Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Faculty of Fine Arts">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Fine_Arts" title="Fine Arts" class="mw-redirect">Fine Arts</a> offers 76 programs at the undergraduate and graduate levels. It includes nine departments and three research institutes.<sup id="cite_ref-FoFA_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FoFA-46"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> During the 2010-2011 academic year, there were 3,153 undergraduate and 555 graduate students enrolled in the faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Among the departments is the <a href="/wiki/The_Mel_Hoppenheim_School_of_Cinema" title="The Mel Hoppenheim School of Cinema" class="mw-redirect">The Mel Hoppenheim School of Cinema</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> It is informally identified as MHSoC, and accepts 200 students a year, for study in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_production" title="Film production" class="mw-redirect">film production</a> and film studies. It is the largest, university-based centre for the study of film animation, film production and film studies in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Molson_School_of_Business">John Molson School of Business</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: John Molson School of Business">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>The John Molson School of Business (JMSB) (formerly the Faculty of Commerce and Administration) offers 48 different programs<sup id="cite_ref-fastfact_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastfact-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> at the undergraduate and graduate levels from six different departments. The departments are Accountancy, Decision Sciences and MIS, Finance, International Business, Management and Marketing. During the 2010-2011 academic year, there were 7,508 undergraduate students and 1,470 graduate students enrolled as well as 37,788 alumni.<sup id="cite_ref-at-a-glance_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-at-a-glance-48"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> The JMSB is accredited by the <a href="/wiki/Association_to_Advance_Collegiate_Schools_of_Business" title="Association to Advance Collegiate Schools of Business">Association to Advance Collegiate Schools of Business</a> (AACSB).<sup id="cite_ref-JMSBAACSB_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMSBAACSB-49"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_media">Campus media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Campus media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Concordia University has student-run media outlets, including newspapers <i><a href="/wiki/The_Link_(newspaper)" title="The Link (newspaper)">The Link</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Concordian_(Montreal)" title="The Concordian (Montreal)">The Concordian</a></i> and <i>L'Organe</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> radio (<a href="/wiki/CJLO" title="CJLO">CJLO</a>); and TV (<a href="/wiki/Concordia_University_Television" title="Concordia University Television">CUTV</a>) stations.</p> 
     <p>The university presses, <i>The Concordian</i> and <i>L'Organe</i> are members of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_University_Press" title="Canadian University Press">CUP</a>. <i>The Link</i> left the university press network in 2012.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/The_Link_(newspaper)" title="The Link (newspaper)">The Link</a> is the longest-running independent, non-profit, student-run newspaper. Its revenue comes from a student fee levy and advertising. It was founded in 1980 as a merger between The Georgian, representing Sir George Williams University, and The Loyola News, representing Loyola College, when they merged to form Concordia University. The Link was so called because it was meant to link both campuses.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/The_Concordian_(Montreal)" title="The Concordian (Montreal)">The Concordian</a>, founded in 1983, is an independent non-profit <a href="/wiki/Student_newspaper" title="Student newspaper">student newspaper</a> published by and for the students of the university. Its funding is via a fee levy as well as advertising. The paper covers news, arts, sports, music, life and opinions at Concordia University and around Montreal.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Stingers" title="Concordia Stingers">Concordia Stingers</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Concordia University's athletic teams are called the <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Stingers" title="Concordia Stingers">Concordia Stingers</a>. They compete with other schools in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>, and more specifically, in the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Student_Sports_Federation" title="Quebec Student Sports Federation" class="mw-redirect">Quebec Student Sports Federation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_University_Football_League" title="Quebec University Football League">Quebec University Football League</a>. The university has ten varsity teams. In the fall, teams compete in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a>, men's and women's <a href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer" class="mw-redirect">soccer</a>, men's and women's <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sport_wrestling" title="Sport wrestling" class="mw-redirect">sport wrestling</a>. There are female and male wrestlers on the team from year to year, however they compete as one team. In the winter, teams compete in men's and women's <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> and men's and women's <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>.</p> 
     <p>Concordia won a national championship in 1999, when the women's hockey team beat the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">University of Alberta</a> in the final game of the season. Recently, the Stingers beat Cape Breton University Capers 12-2 and won the 2009 National Baseball Crown.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridge_Building_Competition">Bridge Building Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Bridge Building Competition">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Troitsky_Bridge_Building_Competition" title="Troitsky Bridge Building Competition">Troitsky Bridge Building Competition</a> brings together engineering students from across Canada and parts of the United States. Teams of students representing their universities must build a 1-metre-long bridge using only regular popsicle sticks, <a href="/wiki/Toothpick" title="Toothpick">toothpicks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dental_floss" title="Dental floss">dental floss</a>, and white <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate" title="Polyvinyl acetate">glue</a>. A panel of judges grades the bridges based on originality and presentation while a hydraulic loading device is used to determine the maximum load and performance.</p>

     <p>Concordia University is home to local and international fraternities and sororities. These organizations exist under, and intercommunicate through the Interfraternal Council, known as the &quot;IFC&quot;. In Greek alphabetical order, they are:</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Delta_Phi_Epsilon_(social)" title="Delta Phi Epsilon (social)">Delta Phi Epsilon</a> Sorority Beta Pi chapter was founded in 1994 at Concordia University, it is the only sorority at Concordia that is International, i.e., with chapters in both the United States and Canada. The continuously growing group of ladies, commonly referred to as Deephers, pride themselves for incorporating the Five S's into their daily lives that make the sisterhood unique and well-rounded: Sisterhood, Scholarship, Service, Social and Self.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Mu Omicron Zeta fraternity, commonly referred to as MOZ (pronounced like &quot;moes&quot;), was founded in 1992. In addition to Concordia, MOZ fraternity has members from McGill University|McGill and the Universit&eacute; de Montr&eacute;al.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Brotherhood of Omicron is another locally based fraternity at Concordia.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Their name is based on the Greek letter Omicron (?). They accept members from Montreal Universities.</p>

Based mainly in Concordia, the sorority was founded in 1968 by six women of Loyola College.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> It now has a large network of sisters, commonly referred to as ZETs (pronounced as zaytes). As a local sorority, they govern themselves, with a Board of Control outside of their active chapter.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

TKE has the most active chapters of any other fraternity in North America and is the only international fraternity at Concordia. Founded in 1965 at Loyola College,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> TKE is the oldest fraternity at Concordia. The KX chapter is famous for its annual &quot;Teke-in-a-Box&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> and &quot;Bachelor/Bachelorette Auction&quot; charity fundraisers. TKE also has its own scholarship, for which all active Undergraduate members are eligible.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_Union">Student Union</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Student Union">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Student_Union" title="Concordia Student Union">Concordia Student Union</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The Concordia Student Union (usually referred to as the CSU) is the organization representing undergraduate students at Concordia University in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Its membership totals more than 33,000 students.</p> 
     <p>The CSU was founded in 1979 from the merger of the Sir George Williams Day Students? Association (DSA), the SGW Part-Time Students? Association (PTSA), the Loyola Evening Students? Association Inc. (LESA) and the Loyola Students? Association Inc. (LSA). The impetus for the merger was the previous merger of Sir George William University with Loyola College, which had taken place at the initiative of the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Government" title="Quebec Government" class="mw-redirect">Quebec Government</a> in 1974, that had resulted in the creation of Concordia University. The CSU was originally named the Concordia University Students? Association (CUSA). It was incorporated in 1982 as the Concordia University Students? Association Inc. The name was changed to Concordia Student Union Inc. in 1994 and the ?Inc.? was dropped completely from the name in 2002.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_activism">Student activism</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Student activism">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Concordia has made media headlines for issues involving politically active students. The first major incident was <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Williams_Computer_Riot" title="Sir George Williams Computer Riot">SGW Computer Riots</a> in 1969, before the merger that created today's university. Concordia supports a vibrant student and community level <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> including well over 60 <a href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic" class="mw-redirect">academic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist" class="mw-redirect">socialist</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anarchist" title="Anarchist" class="mw-redirect">anarchist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist" class="mw-redirect">feminist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious" title="Religious" class="mw-redirect">religious</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gay_rights" title="Gay rights" class="mw-redirect">gay rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hacktivist" title="Hacktivist" class="mw-redirect">hacktivist</a> organizations, as well as cultural clubs and federal political parties. The level of politicization and activity of student groups is dependent on the group and its purpose, and varies according to changing membership from year to year.</p> 
     <p>In 1989, Concordia students voted in a referendum to directly fund their <a href="/wiki/PIRG" title="PIRG" class="mw-redirect">PIRG</a> with a fee-levy. With the support of this social-justice organization, which now had stable funding and a staff of paid workers, student activism flourished in the 1990s. A number of organizations that are now based at the university have their origins as QPIRG Concordia Working Groups. These include The People's Potato, a vegan soup-kitchen; Le Frigo Vert, a non-profit natural food co-op; and Right To Move/La Voie Libre, a fully equipped, volunteer-run bicycle repair workshop. All of these organizations are open to the general public and have strong representations of non-student community members.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Concordia students took an active role in the province-wide student strikes of 1996, which resulted in the renewal of a tuition freeze which was later revoked in 2007.</p> 
     <p>As the 1990s progressed, student activism became more militant, coming to a head in 1999 with the election of the first in a series of radical slates to the Concordia Student Union. Under the presidency of Rob Green, a referendum regarding of another strike garnered 2,284 votes of support. This was an unusually strong show of support, as student governments at Concordia are often elected on the basis of less than 1000 votes in their favor. The strike lasted from November 3 to 5th and targeted a range of issues, including student representation in the university senate, corporate presence and advertising on campus, and government cuts to education. There were several demonstrations in which both protesters and police were reported to be injured.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Concordia students voted in favor of accreditation of their student union in a referendum in December 2000. As a result, the CSU is now legally accountable only to its student constituents.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Many incidents over the last several years have had their roots in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. The conflict has been largely represented as a one between two student groups: the pro-Palestinian <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_for_Palestinian_Human_Rights" title="Solidarity for Palestinian Human Rights">Solidarity for Palestinian Human Rights</a> and pro-Israeli <a href="/wiki/Hillel:_The_Foundation_for_Jewish_Campus_Life" title="Hillel: The Foundation for Jewish Campus Life">Hillel</a>. In general most student activism is conducted at a lower profile level.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netanyahu_protest">Netanyahu protest</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Netanyahu protest">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/2002_Netanyahu_protest_at_Concordia_University" title="2002 Netanyahu protest at Concordia University" class="mw-redirect">2002 Netanyahu protest at Concordia University</a>
     </div> 
     <p>On September 9, 2002, a scheduled visit from the then former (and now current) <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Netanyahu" title="Benjamin Netanyahu">Benjamin Netanyahu</a> was cancelled after <a href="/wiki/Service_de_police_de_la_Ville_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Service de police de la Ville de Montr&eacute;al">Montreal Police</a> and pro-<a href="/wiki/Palestine" title="Palestine">Palestinian</a> protestors clashed inside the Henry F. Hall Building.<sup id="cite_ref-CTV_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTV-77"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Five demonstrators were arrested,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> and an additional 12 faced internal disciplinary hearings under the University's Code of Rights and Responsibilities<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university instituted additional measures to avert future incidents, including the banning of any events related to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as well as enabling the use of new student disciplinary rules in case of emergency.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concordia_University_massacre">Concordia University massacre</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Concordia_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Concordia University massacre">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Concordia_University_massacre" title="Concordia University massacre">Concordia University massacre</a>
     </div> 
     <p>On August 24, 1992, <a href="/wiki/Valery_Fabrikant" title="Valery Fabrikant" class="mw-redirect">Valery Fabrikant</a>, a Mechanical Engineering professor, <a href="/wiki/Concordia_University_massacre" title="Concordia University massacre">shot five colleagues</a> on the ninth floor of the Hall Building, accusing them of stealing credit for his research work.</p> 
     <p>Civil Engineering professor Matthew McCartney Douglass, Chemistry professor Michael Gorden Hogben, and Mechanical Engineering Professor Aaron Jaan Saber died that day, while Electrical and Computer Engineering Chair Phoivos Ziogas died a month later from his wounds. Elizabeth Horwood, secretary in the Mechanical Engineering department, recovered from her injuries.</p> 
     <p>Fabrikant was convicted of the murders and sentenced to life imprisonment. The university erected a memorial to the slain professors (four granite tables) in the Hall Building lobby.</p>
