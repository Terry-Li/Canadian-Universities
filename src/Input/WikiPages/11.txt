     <p><b>The University of Western Ontario</b>, commonly referred to among Canadian universities as <b>Western</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> research university located in <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London, Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The university's main campus covers 455 hectares (1,120 acres) of land, with the <a href="/wiki/Thames_River_(Ontario)" title="Thames River (Ontario)">Thames River</a> running through the eastern portion. Western administers a wide variety of academic programs between 12 faculties, professional schools and three affiliated <a href="/wiki/University_college" title="University college">university colleges</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-afil_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afil-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Academically, University of Western Ontario is ranked among the best universities across Ontario and Canada for its research intensity and education quality.</p> 
     <p>The university was founded in 1878 as the Western University of London, Ontario,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> a denominational school of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, by Bishop <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Hellmuth" title="Isaac Hellmuth">Isaac Hellmuth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Diocese_of_Huron" title="Anglican Diocese of Huron" class="mw-redirect">Anglican Diocese of Huron</a>. The university became secular in 1908 and was renamed to its present name in 1923. The school has over 23,000 undergraduate and 5,000 graduate students. More than 220,000 alumni and former students of Western can be found in over 150 countries around the world. The Western varsity athletic teams are known as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Mustangs" title="Western Mustangs" class="mw-redirect">Western Mustangs</a>, and are members of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The university was founded on 7 March 1878 by <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Isaac_Hellmuth" title="Bishop Isaac Hellmuth" class="mw-redirect">Bishop Isaac Hellmuth</a> (1817?1901) of the Anglican Diocese of Huron as &quot;The Western University of London Ontario.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It incorporated <a href="/wiki/Huron_University_College" title="Huron University College">Huron University College</a>, which had been founded in 1863.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The first four faculties were Arts, Divinity, Law and Medicine, and there were originally only 15 students when classes began in 1881.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The first of these students graduated in 1883. The Western University of London was eventually made non-denominational in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1916, the current site of the university was purchased from the Kingsmill family. There are two World War I Memorial plaques on the Physics and Astronomy Building: The first lists the names of 19 students and graduates of the University of Western Ontario who lost their lives; the second honours the men from Middlesex County who fell.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1923, the university was renamed The University of Western Ontario.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The first two buildings constructed at the new site were the Arts Building (now University College) and the Natural Science Building (now the Physics and Astronomy Building). Classes on the present site of the school began in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The University College tower, one of the most distinctive features of the University, was named the Middlesex Memorial Tower in honour of the men from <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Ontario" title="Middlesex County, Ontario">Middlesex County</a> who had fought in World War I. Western later became affiliated with St. Peter's College seminary of <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London, Ontario</a> in 1939, and it eventually became King's College, an arts faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Two World War II memorial honour rolls are hung on the Physics and Astronomy Building: the first lists the names of the UWO students and graduates who served in the Second World War, and the second lists those who served with the No. 10 Canadian General hospital during WWII, the unit was raised and equipped by UWO.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Although enrollment was relatively small for many years, the university began to increase greatly in size after World War II. The university saw the addition of a number of new faculties in the post-war period, such as the Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">Graduate Studies</a> (1947), the School of Business Administration (now the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ivey_School_of_Business" title="Richard Ivey School of Business" class="mw-redirect">Richard Ivey School of Business</a>) (1949), the Faculty of Engineering Science (now the Faculty of Engineering) (1957), the <a href="/wiki/Western_Law_School" title="Western Law School">Faculty of Law</a> (1959), and <a href="/wiki/Althouse_College_of_Education" title="Althouse College of Education" class="mw-redirect">Althouse College</a> for education students (now the Faculty of Education) (1963)<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the Faculty of Music (1968).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2012, the university rebranded itself &quot;Western University&quot;. The legal name of the university, however, remains <i>The University of Western Ontario</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The University of Western Ontario is situated in the city of London, Ontario, located in the southwestern end of the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City_%E2%80%93_Windsor_Corridor" title="Quebec City ? Windsor Corridor" class="mw-redirect">Quebec City ? Windsor Corridor</a>. The majority of the campus is surrounded by residential neighbourhoods, with the <a href="/wiki/Thames_River_(Ontario)" title="Thames River (Ontario)">Thames River</a> bisecting the eastern portion of the campus. <a href="/wiki/List_of_roads_in_London,_Ontario#Western_Road" title="List of roads in London, Ontario">Western Road</a> is the major transportation artery of the university, going north to south. While the campus covers 455 hectares (1,120 acres), the majority of the teaching facilities are centred within the core approximately 169.3 hectares (418&nbsp;acres).<sup id="cite_ref-size_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-size-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Campus sustainability at Western is managed by the President's Advisory Committee on Environment &amp; Sustainability. The committee's mandate includes incorporating sustainability into the academic programming, engaging in research across the disciplines into issues of environmental sustainability, utilizing ecological landscaping methods and preserving green space and building and renovating facilities in accordance with energy efficiency and sustainability principles<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with the other members of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ontario_Universities" title="Council of Ontario Universities">Council of Ontario Universities</a>, Western had signed a pledge in 2009 known as <i>Ontario Universities Committed to a Greener World</i>, with the objective of transforming its campus into a model of environmental responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Western is also a signatory of the <a href="/wiki/Talloires_Declaration" title="Talloires Declaration">Talloires Declaration</a>, a sustainability declaration created for presidents of higher education.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The university campus received a B- grade from the Sustainable Endowments Institute on its College Sustainability Report Card for 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The governance of the university is conducted through the Board of Governors and the Senate. The Senate was the university's first governing body, created in the university's founding document, An Act to Incorporate the Western University of London, Ontario, 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The Board of Governors was later established in An Act to amend the Act to incorporate the Western University of London, Ontario, 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The Board is responsible for the overall management of the university, including financial matters.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Ex officio governors of the Board include the university's chancellor, president, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_London,_Ontario" title="List of mayors of London, Ontario">mayor of London</a>, the warden of <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Ontario" title="Middlesex County, Ontario">Middlesex County</a> and the secretary of the Board of Governors. The Board also consists of 26 other governors, either appointed or elected by the various members of the university's community and the surrounding community, including elected representatives from the student body.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Senate is responsible for the university's academic policies.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The Senate consists of 20 <i>ex officio</i> positions in the Senate granted to the chancellor, the president, the vice-presidents of the university, the senior dean of each faculty, the university librarian and the secretary of the senate. The secretary of the senate is a non-voting <i>ex officio</i> member. The Senate also consists of 46 elected members from the university's faculty, 18 members from the student population, and 9 members from the Western's affiliated colleges, including their principals. The Senate also consists of 9 other members from around the university community. In total, there are 103 members of the Senate, 102 of which may vote and 10?13 official observers of the Senate.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The president and vice-chancellor acts as the chief executive officer of the university who is accountable to the Board of Governors and the Senate, and supervises and directs the academic and administrative work of the university and of its teaching and non-teaching staff.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amit_Chakma" title="Amit Chakma">Amit Chakma</a> is the tenth president of the university, serving in the post since 1 July 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The chancellor of the university acts as the honorary and symbolic head of the university. The position of chancellor is a four-year, non-renewable term.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The current chancellor of the university is <a href="/wiki/John_Thompson_(TD_banker)" title="John Thompson (TD banker)">John Thompson</a>, who held the position since 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Programs at the university is divided amongst 12 faculties and schools, including <a href="/wiki/Althouse_College_of_Education" title="Althouse College of Education" class="mw-redirect">Althouse College of Education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Wright_Faculty_of_Music" title="Don Wright Faculty of Music">Don Wright Faculty of Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ivey_School_of_Business" title="Richard Ivey School of Business" class="mw-redirect">Richard Ivey School of Business</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schulich_School_of_Medicine_%26_Dentistry" title="Schulich School of Medicine &amp; Dentistry">Schulich School of Medicine &amp; Dentistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Law_School" title="Western Law School">Western Law School</a>. The university also is affiliated with three university colleges, <a href="/wiki/Brescia_University_College" title="Brescia University College">Brescia University College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huron_University_College" title="Huron University College">Huron University College</a> and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_University_College_(University_of_Western_Ontario)" title="King's University College (University of Western Ontario)">King's University College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-afil_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afil-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Western is a publicly funded research university, and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Universities_and_Colleges_of_Canada" title="Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada">Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The full-time undergraduate programs comprise the majority of the school's enrollment, made up of 23,690 full-time, part-time undergraduate students and concurrent education students. The graduate student population is 5,297, including full-time students, part-time students and post-graduate medical residents.<sup id="cite_ref-ipbuwo_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipbuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The university conferred 4,504 bachelor degrees, 207 doctoral degrees, 1,427 master degrees, and 1,180 second entry professional degrees in 2008?2009.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Students may apply for financial aid such as the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Student_Assistance_Program" title="Ontario Student Assistance Program">Ontario Student Assistance Program</a> and <a href="/wiki/Student_loans_in_Canada" title="Student loans in Canada">Canada Student Loans and Grants</a> through the federal and provincial governments. The financial aid provided may come in the form of loans, grants, bursaries, scholarships, fellowships, debt reduction, interest relief, and work programs.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Resources_and_Skills_Development_Canada_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Resources_and_Skills_Development_Canada-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Admission requirements at Western differs depending upon the education system in which the applicant has originated from, due to the lack of uniformity in marking schemes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The acceptance rate at Western fell from 66 percent in 2005 to 58 percent in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The secondary school average for full-time first-year students at Western was 86.8 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The University of Western Ontario has ranked as one of Canada's top universities. According to the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a> (ARWU) rankings, the university ranked 201?300 in the world and 8?17 in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_ARWU_CAN_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_ARWU_CAN-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The 2011 <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a> ranked the university 157th in the world, making it seventh in Canada<sup id="cite_ref-QSran_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QSran-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In terms of national rankings, <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> ranked Western 11th in their 2013 Medical Doctoral university rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdoc_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdoc-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Western was ranked in spite of having opted out, along with several other universities in Canada, of participating in <i>Maclean's</i> graduate survey since 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Several of Western's programs were also ranked in individual rankings. Social sciences at Western was ranked 96th in the world in the 2010 QS World University Rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-QSran_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QSran-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, the ARWU similarly ranked social science at Western 76?100 in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_ARWU_SOC_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_ARWU_SOC-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Western Law School was also ranked ninth nationally in Maclean's 2012 rankings for <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> schools in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Western's Richard Ivey School of Business has also ranked well internationally. In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Businessweek" title="Bloomberg Businessweek">Bloomberg Businessweek</a> had ranked the Ivey as the sixth best business school outside of the United States and second in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a> had also ranked Ivey 46th in its 2011 global MBA ranking, placing second nationally.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Western is also renowned for educating many of Canada's top achievers. In 2010, Western ranked first amongst Canadian universities for earning the most Top 40 Under 40 Awards, in both the undergraduate and MBA categories.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Western has four primary fields of research in which it currently operates: life sciences and the human condition, culture analysis and values, the human and physical environments, and social trends, public policy, and economic activity.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In Research Infosource's 2011 ranking of Canada's 50 top research universities, Western was ranked 10th, with a sponsored research income of $221.236 million, averaging $155,600 per faculty member.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The federal government is the largest source of funding providing 46 percent of Western's <a href="/wiki/Funding_of_science" title="Funding of science">research budget</a>, primarily through grants. Private corporations contribute 10% of Western's research budget.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Higher_Education_Evaluation_and_Accreditation_Council_of_Taiwan" title="Higher Education Evaluation and Accreditation Council of Taiwan">Higher Education Evaluation and Accreditation Council of Taiwan</a> (HEEACT), an organization which also evaluates universities based on their scientific paper's performances, ranked Western 184th in the world and ninth nationally in its 2011 rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Western was also ranked 87th in the world within the field of social sciences in HEEACT's 2011 rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Research regarding the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a> has also become a major focus at the university. The Brain and Mind Institute was created to provide a focus for research in cognitive neuroscience at Western.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> and the Institute recently discovered that the blind may echolocate by using the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> of the brain.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Another recent study at Western has suggested that people deaf from birth may be able to reassign the area of their brain used for hearing to boost their sight.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archives_and_Gallery">Archives and Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Archives and Gallery">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The McIntosh Gallery, established in 1942, hosts permanent and temporary exhibitions of the works of students, faculty, Canadian and international artists. The McIntosh Gallery has a collection of over 3,500 objects, with a focus on 18th-20th-century Canadian paintings, sculpture, drawings, photographs and prints; and 18th-20th-century British, French, Italian and American paintings, drawings and prints. The Gallery archives house official records of, or relating to, or people/activities connected with local artists.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The two main <a href="/wiki/Students%27_union" title="Students' union">student unions</a> on administrative and policy issues is the University Students' Council for all undergraduate students and the Society of Graduate Students for graduate students.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> The University Students' Council recognizes more than 180 student organizations and clubs, in which more than 19,500 people are a member.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> These clubs and organizations cover a wide range of interests such as academics, culture, religion, social issues, and recreation.<sup id="cite_ref-club_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-club-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> The University Students' Council also provides additional services such as the campus movie theatre, pub, restaurant, clothing store and print shop.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> These facilities can all be found in the University Community Centre.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performances">Performances</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Performances">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Don_Wright_Faculty_of_Music" title="Don Wright Faculty of Music">Don Wright Faculty of Music</a> offers over 300 concert performances of various styles throughout the year, most of which are open to the public. The Western University Symphony Orchestra and the Western University Chamber Orchestra perform regularly under conductor Alain Trudell. UWOpera, under the direction of Theodore Baerg, performs a wide variety of repertoire ranging from operetta to full operatic works in the Paul Davenport Theatre (refurbished and renamed in 2009 from Talbot Theatre).<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Theatre Western produces a season that includes an annual musical revue of modern and classic Broadway, Purple Shorts (Western's <a href="/wiki/One_act_play" title="One act play" class="mw-redirect">One-Act Play</a> Festival,) and a major musical production each spring. Recent productions include West Side Story, Cabaret, Gypsy, Sweeney Todd, and The 25th Annual Putnam County Spelling Bee.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Huron Underground Drama Society (or &quot;HUDS&quot;) is a student run drama group that puts on several shows per year. Their plays or skits are usually completely student written, and are well known for their edgy comedic content.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Western_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The university's student population operates a number of media outlets throughout the campus environmennt. <i><a href="/wiki/UWO_Gazette" title="UWO Gazette">The Gazette</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Student_newspaper" title="Student newspaper">student newspaper</a> which has been in publication since 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The Faculty of Science and the Faculty of Information and Media Studies (FIMS) both run publications called The Current and OPENWIDE respectively. The University Students' Council also own and operates a <a href="/wiki/Campus_radio" title="Campus radio">campus radio</a> station <a href="/wiki/CHRW-FM" title="CHRW-FM">CHRW-FM</a> (94.9 FM).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> The first campus radio to operate at Western was in 1971, although the present day station CHRW-FM, was not established until 1979, one year after the closure of the Western's first campus radio station.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> The University Students' Council had previously operated a closed-circuit television station, known as tvWestern.ca. The television station began broadcasting in 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> and was discontinued by the student union in 2010 after being cut from the university's operating budget.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Athletics at Western is managed by Sports &amp; Recreation Services, a division of the Faculty of Health Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> The university's varsity teams compete in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_University_Athletics" title="Ontario University Athletics">Ontario University Athletics</a> conference of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>. The varsity teams are known as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Ontario_Mustangs" title="Western Ontario Mustangs">Western Ontario Mustangs</a>. As is mandatory for all members of Canadian Interuniversity Sport, Western does not provide full-ride athletic scholarships.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university has a number of athletic facilities open to both their varsity teams as well as to their students. <a href="/wiki/TD_Waterhouse_Stadium" title="TD Waterhouse Stadium">TD Waterhouse Stadium</a> has been the main stadium of the university since it opened in 2000, with a seating capacity for over 8,000 spectators. The stadium is home to the university's varsity football team, and has hosted a number of events including the <a href="/wiki/World_Lacrosse_Championships" title="World Lacrosse Championships" class="mw-redirect">World Lacrosse Championships</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Games" title="Canada Games">Canada Games</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> The Thompson Recreation &amp; Athletic Centre which houses a number of athletic venues, including an ice rink, tennis facilities and a track, is home to the varsity ice hockey teams and the varsity track and field teams.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> Another athletic facility at the university is Alumni Hall, which is a multipurpose venue for sports such as basketball, volleyball and other indoor events.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Intramural sport leagues and tournaments have a high level of participation at Western.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> Opportunities are offered at multiple skill levels and across a variety of sports. Sports offered include traditional sports like volleyball, basketball and soccer, as well as less traditional events like dodgeball and inner tube water polo. Western also hosts secondary school football games at TD Waterhouse Stadium.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>As of November 2007, the University of Western Ontario has over 220,000 alumni residing in over 100 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> Throughout Western's history, faculty, alumni, and former students have played prominent roles in many different fields and have won the Nobel Prize, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> and other awards such as the Rhodes Scholarship.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> Former faculty member <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Banting" title="Frederick Banting">Frederick Banting</a> received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> for his discovery of insulin.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> Two graduates from Western have also travelled in space, namely <a href="/wiki/Bjarni_Tryggvason" title="Bjarni Tryggvason">Bjarni Tryggvason</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Bondar" title="Roberta Bondar">Roberta Bondar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Many former students have gained local and national prominence for serving in government, such as <a href="/wiki/James_Bartleman" title="James Bartleman">James Bartleman</a>, who served as <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Ontario" title="Lieutenant Governor of Ontario">Lieutenant Governor of Ontario</a> from 2002 to 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> Western's alumni also include a number of provincial premiers, including former <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_Ontario" title="Premier of Ontario">premiers of Ontario</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Robarts" title="John Robarts">John Robarts</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Peterson" title="David Peterson">David Peterson</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> and the former <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_Alberta" title="Premier of Alberta">premier of Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Getty" title="Don Getty">Don Getty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> A number of graduates have also served prominent positions on the international level. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Stevens" title="Glenn Stevens">Glenn Stevens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> the Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Australia" title="Reserve Bank of Australia">Reserve Bank of Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Chan" title="Margaret Chan">Margaret Chan</a>, the Director-General of the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
