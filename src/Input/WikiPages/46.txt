     <p>The <b>University of Massachusetts Amherst</b> (otherwise known as <b>Massachusetts</b>, <b>UMass</b>, or <b>UMass Amherst</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">public</a> research and <a href="/wiki/Land-grant_university" title="Land-grant university">land-grant university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Massachusetts" title="Amherst, Massachusetts">Amherst, Massachusetts</a>, and the flagship of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts" title="University of Massachusetts">University of Massachusetts system</a>. With 1,174 faculty members and more than 27,000 students, UMass Amherst is the largest public university in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university offers <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degrees" title="Bachelor's degrees" class="mw-redirect">bachelor's degrees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degrees" title="Master's degrees" class="mw-redirect">master's degrees</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doctoral_degrees" title="Doctoral degrees" class="mw-redirect">doctoral degrees</a> in 88 undergraduate and 72 graduate areas of study, through eight schools and colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The main campus is situated north of downtown Amherst. In a 2009 article for MSN.com, Amherst was ranked first in Best College Towns in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_and_World_Report" title="U.S. News and World Report" class="mw-redirect">U.S. News and World Report</a> ranked Amherst amongst the Top 10 Great College Towns in America.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Massachusetts Amherst is categorized as a Research University with Very High research activity by the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Foundation_for_the_Advancement_of_Teaching" title="Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching">Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011, UMass Amherst had research expenditures of $181.3 million.<sup id="cite_ref-masslive_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-masslive-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Colleges_(Massachusetts)" title="Five Colleges (Massachusetts)" class="mw-redirect">Five College Consortium</a>.</p> 
     <p>UMass Amherst sports teams are called the <a href="/wiki/UMass_Minutemen" title="UMass Minutemen">Minutemen and Minutewomen</a>, the colors being maroon and white; the school mascot is <a href="/wiki/Sam_the_Minuteman" title="Sam the Minuteman">Sam the Minuteman</a>. All teams participate in <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a>. The university is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_10_Conference" title="Atlantic 10 Conference">Atlantic 10 Conference</a>, while playing ice hockey in <a href="/wiki/Hockey_East" title="Hockey East">Hockey East</a>. In football, UMass has completed their last season in the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Athletic_Association" title="Colonial Athletic Association">Colonial Athletic Association</a> (CAA) at the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_Football_Championship" title="NCAA Division I Football Championship">FCS</a> level, and in 2012 they upgraded to the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_Football_Bowl_Subdivision" title="NCAA Division I Football Bowl Subdivision" class="mw-redirect">FBS</a> level and transition to the <a href="/wiki/Mid-American_Conference" title="Mid-American Conference">Mid-American Conference</a> (MAC).</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_and_early_years">Foundation and early years</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation and early years">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Mass_Aggie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Mass_Aggie.jpg/200px-Mass_Aggie.jpg" width="200" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Mass_Aggie.jpg/300px-Mass_Aggie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Mass_Aggie.jpg/400px-Mass_Aggie.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Mass_Aggie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Massachusetts Agricultural College as it appeared in 1879, with students and faculty standing in front of Old South College, North College, and the college's first chapel.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The university was founded in 1863 under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Morrill_Land-Grant_Colleges_Act" title="Morrill Land-Grant Colleges Act" class="mw-redirect">Federal Morrill Land-Grant Colleges Act</a> to provide instruction to Massachusetts citizens in the &quot;agricultural, mechanical, and military arts.&quot; Accordingly, the university was initially named the <b>Massachusetts Agricultural College</b>, popularly referred to as &quot;Mass Aggie&quot; or &quot;M.A.C.&quot; In 1867, the college had yet to admit any students, had been through two <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="Chancellor of the University of Massachusetts Amherst" class="mw-redirect">Presidents</a>, and still had not completed any college buildings. In that year, <a href="/wiki/William_S._Clark" title="William S. Clark">William S. Clark</a> was appointed President of the college and Professor of Botany. He quickly appointed a faculty, completed the construction plan, and in the fall of 1867 admitted the first class of approximately 50 students. Clark became the first functioning President and arguably the primary founding father of the college.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="floatright">
      <a href="/wiki/File:UMASeal100.svg" class="image" title="The University's Centennial Seal"><img alt="The University's Centennial Seal" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/UMASeal100.svg/160px-UMASeal100.svg.png" width="160" height="164" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/UMASeal100.svg/240px-UMASeal100.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/UMASeal100.svg/320px-UMASeal100.svg.png 2x" /></a>
     </div> 
     <p>The original buildings consisted of Old South College (a dormitory located on the site of the present South College), North College (a second dormitory once located just south of today's Machmer Hall), the Chemistry Laboratory, also known as College Hall (once located on the present site of Machmer Hall), the Boarding House (a small dining hall located just north of the present Campus Parking Garage), the Botanic Museum (located on the north side of the intersection of Stockbridge Road and Chancellor's Hill Drive) and the Durfee Plant House (located on the site of the new Durfee Conservatory).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Although enrollment was slow during the 1870s, the fledgling college built momentum under the leadership of President <a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Hill_Goodell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Henry Hill Goodell (page does not exist)">Henry Hill Goodell</a>. In the 1880s, Goodell implemented an expansion plan, adding the College Drill Hall in 1883 (the first gymnasium), the <a href="/wiki/The_Old_Chapel_(University_of_Massachusetts)" title="The Old Chapel (University of Massachusetts)" class="mw-redirect">Old Chapel Library</a> in 1885 (one of the oldest extant buildings on campus and an important symbol of the University), and the East and West Experiment Stations in 1886 and 1890. The Campus Pond, now the central focus of the University Campus, was created in 1893 by damming a small brook.</p> 
     <p>The early 20th century saw great expansion in terms of enrollment and the scope of the curriculum. The first female student was admitted in 1875 on a part-time basis and the first full-time female student was admitted in 1892. In 1903, Draper Hall was constructed for the dual purpose of a dining hall and female housing. The first female students graduated with the class of 1905. The first dedicated female dormitory, the Abigail Adams House (on the site of today's Lederle Tower) was built in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>By the start of the 20th century, the college was thriving and quickly expanded its curriculum to include the liberal arts. In recognition of the higher enrollment and broader curriculum, the college was renamed <b>Massachusetts State College</b> in 1931.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg/150px-UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg" width="150" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg/225px-UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg/300px-UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:UMass_Amherst_Campus_Center_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> A picture looking north from the W.E.B. DuBois Library at UMass Amherst. Lederle Graduate Research Tower can be seen in the background and the Campus Center and Hotel in the foreground.
       </div>

     </div> 
     <p>Following World War II, the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Bill" title="G.I. Bill">G.I. Bill</a>, facilitating financial aid for veterans, led to an explosion of applicants. The college population soared and Presidents <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Potter_Baker" title="Hugh Potter Baker" class="mw-redirect">Hugh Potter Baker</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Van_Meter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ralph Van Meter (page does not exist)">Ralph Van Meter</a> labored to push through major construction projects in the 1940s and 1950s, particularly with regard to dormitories (now Northeast and Central Residential Areas). Accordingly, the name of the college was changed in 1947 to the &quot;University of Massachusetts.&quot;</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>By the 1970s, the University continued to grow and gave rise to a shuttle bus service on campus as well as many other architectural additions; this included the <a href="/wiki/Murray_D._Lincoln_Campus_Center" title="Murray D. Lincoln Campus Center">Murray D. Lincoln Campus Center</a> complete with a hotel, office space, fine dining restaurant, campus store, and passage way to the parking garage, the <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois_Library" title="W. E. B. Du Bois Library">W. E. B. Du Bois Library</a>, and the Fine Arts Center.</p> 
     <p>Over the course of the next two decades, the <a href="/wiki/John_W._Lederle_Graduate_Research_Center" title="John W. Lederle Graduate Research Center">John W. Lederle Graduate Research Center</a> and the Conte National Polymer Research Center were built and UMass Amherst emerged as a major research facility. The Robsham Memorial Center for Visitors welcomed thousands of guests to campus after its dedication in 1989. For athletic and other large events, the <a href="/wiki/Mullins_Center" title="Mullins Center" class="mw-redirect">Mullins Center</a> was opened in 1993, hosting capacity crowds as the Minutemen basketball team ranked at number one for many weeks in the mid-1990s, and reached the <a href="/wiki/1996_NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="1996 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament">Final Four</a> in 1996.</p> 
     <p>UMass Amherst entered the 21st century with close to 24,000 students enrolled.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2003, for the first time, the Massachusetts State Legislature legally designated UMass Amherst as a Research University and the &quot;flagship campus of the UMass system.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> UMass Faculty members are top performers in terms of the numbers of awards and recognitions they receive, and their supported research activities total more than $140 million per year.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its current student body is the most high-achieving in UMass Amherst history (in terms of admissions test scores and grades) and the university was named a top producer of <a href="/wiki/Fulbright_Award" title="Fulbright Award" class="mw-redirect">Fulbright Award</a> winners in the 2008?2009 academic year.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, in 2010 UMass Amherst was named one of the &quot;Top Colleges and Universities Contributing to <a href="/wiki/Teach_For_America" title="Teach For America">Teach For America</a>'s 2010 Teaching Corps.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Since the University of Massachusetts Amherst was founded as the Massachusetts Agricultural College in 1863, 25 individuals have been at the helm of the institution.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The office, originally known as &quot;President,&quot; was changed to &quot;<a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor</a>&quot; in 1970. The title &quot;President of the University of Massachusetts&quot; now refers to the president of the entire five-campus University of Massachusetts system. The current Chancellor of the Amherst campus is <a href="/wiki/Kumble_R._Subbaswamy" title="Kumble R. Subbaswamy">Dr. Kumble R. Subbaswamy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-seizing_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seizing-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The Chancellor resides in Hillside, the campus residence for chancellors.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are 1,121 permanent faculty at the university<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In 2010 the state budgeted $465 million for the UMass system. State funding is 25% of revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> UMass offers <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degrees" title="Bachelor's degrees" class="mw-redirect">bachelor's degrees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degrees" title="Master's degrees" class="mw-redirect">master's degrees</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doctoral_degrees" title="Doctoral degrees" class="mw-redirect">doctoral degrees</a> in over 90 undergraduate and 65 graduate areas of study. The university is organized into eight schools and colleges:<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Students interested in studying outside of a particular major can apply to enroll in the Bachelor's Degree with Individual Concentration (BDIC) program. This is a unique program which allows students to design their own area of study. A BDIC concentration must be interdisciplinary, drawing from at least three fields or disciplines, and it may not duplicate an existing major. Courses can be selected from any department within the university as well as the campuses in the Five College Consortium. Course selection is guided by the students chosen faculty sponsor and a BDIC faculty supervisor. BDIC students are assigned to one of five academic clusters- Arts and Cultural studies; Business and Law; Communication; Education and Human Development; Natural Health, Computer Sciences and Engineering.</p> 
     <p>Additionally, the university offers two programs designed for adult and continuing education students, known as <a href="/wiki/University_Without_Walls-_UMass_Amherst" title="University Without Walls- UMass Amherst" class="mw-redirect">University Without Walls</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and Continuing and Professional Education.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endowment">Endowment</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Endowment">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>University of Massachusetts Amherst <a href="/wiki/Financial_endowment" title="Financial endowment">financial endowment</a> (the &quot;University Endowment Fund&quot;) was valued at $210.1 million in 2012. In 2013, the university launched $300 million fund-raising campaign which will end in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-Umass_Amherst_Fund_Raising_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Umass_Amherst_Fund_Raising-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Campus_of_the_University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="Campus of the University of Massachusetts Amherst">Campus of the University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_Ridge_Historical_Area" title="Chestnut Ridge Historical Area">Chestnut Ridge Historical Area</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/East_Ridge_Historical_Area" title="East Ridge Historical Area">East Ridge Historical Area</a>,&nbsp;and 
      <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Drive_Historical_Area" title="Ellis Drive Historical Area">Ellis Drive Historical Area</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The University's campus is situated on 1,450 acres, mainly in the town <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_MA" title="Amherst, MA" class="mw-redirect">Amherst</a>, but also partly in the neighboring town of <a href="/wiki/Hadley,_MA" title="Hadley, MA" class="mw-redirect">Hadley</a>. The campus extends about 1 mile (1.6&nbsp;km) from the <a href="/wiki/Murray_D._Lincoln_Campus_Center" title="Murray D. Lincoln Campus Center">Campus Center</a> in all directions and may be thought of as a series of concentric rings, with innermost ring harboring academic buildings and research labs, surrounded by a ring of the five residential areas and one University owned apartment complex. (North Apartments, Sylvan, Northeast, Central, Orchard Hill, Southwest), which in turn is surrounded by a ring of athletic facilities and parking lots. The campus is also divided into &quot;North Campus&quot; and &quot;South Campus.&quot;</p> 
     <p>The campus has its own Combined Heat and Power (CHP) generation facility. The plant, which was dedicated in 2009 after ten years of planning, replaced a coal burning power plant dating back to 1918 and has reduced the campus' greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 75%. In 2011, the CHP was recognized as the cleanest plant of its size in New England and has been recognized for maintaining 80% efficiency over six consecutive quarters. In 2008, the CHP received the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.combinedcyclejournal.com/Archives/4Q2007.html">Combined Cycle Journal Pacesetter Award</a> for the best Combined Heat and Power plant project in the US that year. The award refers to its innovative design, efficiency, reliability, system redundancy, and environmental benefits.In 2009, the CHP received the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.districtenergy-digital.org/districtenergy/2009Q2?pg=96#pg96">Sustainable Campus Leadership Award from the International District Energy Association</a>. The award states it was given &quot;In recognition of exemplary public leadership in advancing energy efficiency and global environmental stewardship through investment in an innovative district energy system.&quot; The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) presented the University of Massachusetts with the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.epa.gov/chp/public-recognition/current_winners.html">2011 Combined Heat and Power Energy Star Award</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span title="&nbsp;since April 2013" style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot">dead link</a></i>]</span></sup> in an effort to recognize the reduced emissions and increased efficiency of the plant.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residential_life">Residential life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Residential life">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst_residence_halls" title="List of University of Massachusetts Amherst residence halls">List of University of Massachusetts Amherst residence halls</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Residential Life at the University of Massachusetts Amherst is one of the largest on campus housing systems in the United States. Over 12,000 students live in 45 residence halls, while families, staff, and graduate students live in 345 units in two apartment complexes. The forty-five residence halls and four apartment buildings are grouped into six separate and very different residential areas: Central, Northeast, Orchard Hill, Southwest, Sylvan and North. Each possesses its own distinctive characteristics, inspired in part by location, in part by architecture, and in part by the different cultural or academic living/learning programs housed within. Each residential area houses classrooms, recreational and social centers, kitchenettes, and cultural centers - in addition to the bedrooms, study areas, laundries, television rooms, and dining facilities you might expect. Each also has its own student governing body and is,in effect, a community unto itself.</p>

     <p>Commonwealth Honors College is the honors college at UMass. The honors college provides students the opportunity to intensify their UMass academic curriculum. The requirements of the college are to complete an honors college writing course, a seminar called &quot;Ideas That Changed the World,&quot; two honors gen ed courses, an honors seminar called &quot;Topics,&quot; and for advanced scholarship honors, several upper-level honors courses, including an honors thesis, project, or capstone course. Membership in the honors college is not required in order to graduate the University with higher Latin honors designations, such as <a href="/wiki/Magna_cum_laude" title="Magna cum laude" class="mw-redirect">magna</a> or <a href="/wiki/Summa_cum_laude" title="Summa cum laude" class="mw-redirect">summa cum laude</a>. Commonwealth Honors College provides honors students an additional community of students to interact with outside of their academic department and holds many social and academic events during the school year.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Five_College_consortium">Five College consortium</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Five College consortium">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>UMass Amherst is part of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Colleges_(Massachusetts)" title="Five Colleges (Massachusetts)" class="mw-redirect">Five Colleges</a> consortium, which allows its students to attend classes, borrow books, work with professors, etc., at four other <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Valley" title="Pioneer Valley">Pioneer Valley</a> institutions: <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">Mount Holyoke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">Smith</a> Colleges.</p> 
     <p>All five colleges are located within 10 miles of Amherst center, and are accessible by public bus. The five share an <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> department and some other <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect">undergraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student" title="Graduate student" class="mw-redirect">graduate</a> departments.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_service">Community service</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Community service">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>UMass Amherst emphasizes community service as part of its academic programs. The Community Engagement Program (CEP) offers courses that combine classroom learning and community service, and sponsors programs such as the first year IMPACT learning community and the Citizen Scholars Program.      <p>Researchers at the university made several high-profile achievements in recent years. A team of scientists at UMass, led by Vincent Rotello, have developed a molecular nose that can detect and identify various proteins. The research appeared in the May 2007 issue of <a href="/wiki/Nature_Nanotechnology" title="Nature Nanotechnology">Nature Nanotechnology</a>, and the team is currently focusing on sensors which will detect the malformed proteins made by cancer cells.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Also, UMass Amherst scientists Richard Farris, Todd Emrick, and Bryan Coughlin lead a research team that developed a synthetic polymer that does not burn. This polymer is a building block of plastic, and the new flame-retardant plastics won't need to have flame-retarding chemicals added to their composition. These chemicals have recently been found in many different areas from homes and offices to fish, and there are environmental and health concerns regarding the additives. The newly developed polymers would not require the addition of these potentially hazardous chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_on_Campus">Arts on Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Arts on Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The UMass Amherst campus offers a variety of artistic venues, both for performance and visual art. The most prominent is the Fine Arts Center (FAC), built in 1975. The FAC brings nationally known theater, music, and dance performances to campus throughout the year to its performance spaces (the Concert Hall, Bezanson Recital Hall, and Bowker Auditorium). These include several popular performance series: Jazz in July Summer Music Program, The Asian Arts &amp; Culture Program, the Center Series,and the Magic Triangle Series presenting music, dance, and theater performances, cultural arts events, films, talks, workshops, masterclasses and special family events. The <a href="/wiki/University_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art" title="University Museum of Contemporary Art">University Museum of Contemporary Art</a> in the FAC has a permanent <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_art" title="Contemporary art">contemporary art</a> collection of about 2,600 works and hosts numerous visual arts exhibitions each year, as well as workshops, master classes, and artist residencies.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The 9,000-seat <a href="/wiki/Mullins_Center" title="Mullins Center" class="mw-redirect">Mullins Center</a>, the multi-purpose arena of UMass Amherst hosts a wide variety of performances including speakers, rock concerts, and Broadway shows. In addition, the Music, Dance, and Theater Departments, the Renaissance Center, and multiple student groups dedicated to the arts provide an eclectic menu of performances throughout the year.</p> 
     <p>Besides the University Museum of Contemporary Art in the FAC, there are four additional art galleries on the UMass Amherst campus that exhibit the work of faculty, students, and artists from around the world. These are the Augusta Savage Gallery in New Africa House (a multicultural and multiarts facility), Herter Gallery in Herter Hall, the Hamden and Central Galleries, located in the residential areas on campus, and the Student Union Art Gallery.</p>

     <p>UMass Amherst has a history of protest and activism among the undergraduate and graduate population<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> and is home to over 200 registered student organizations (RSOs).</p> 
     <p><b>SGA</b></p> 
     <p>The Student Government Association (SGA) is the undergraduate student governmental body, and provides funding for the many registered student organizations (RSOs) and agencies, including the Student Legal Services Office (SLSO) and the Center for Student Business (CSB). The SGA also makes formal recommendations on matters of Administration policy and advocates for undergraduate students to the Administration, non-student organizations, and local and state government. The SGA has a budget of approximately 2 million dollars per year which is collected for students in the form of the Student Activities Fee. The student activities fee is currently $47 per semester (Spring 2009). It is used to fund RSOs, Agencies and the SGA itself.</p> 
     <p>The SGA has three branches: the President and Executive Cabinet, the Undergraduate Student Senate, and the Student Judiciary.</p> 
     <p><b>Area governments</b></p> 
     <p>There are a total of seven area governments. Each of the campus's six residential areas has an area government, and there is also a Commuter Area Government to serve commuter students. Area governments provide social programming for their areas, and are in charge of the house councils for the dorms in their area. They also represent the needs and interests of students in their areas to the Administration, Housing and Residence Life and the SGA.</p> 
     <p>Area Governments have a tradition of sponsoring large events, generally in the Spring, such as Fill the Hill, Bowl Weekend and Southwest Week.</p> 
     <p>Each residence hall or residential &quot;cluster&quot; (a group of residence halls) at UMass Amherst has a house council. House councils report to their respective area governments. Its budget comes from voluntary dues collected in return for access to common supplies (access to the <a href="/wiki/Kitchenette" title="Kitchenette">kitchenette</a>, rental access to vacuums, brooms, games, etc.). House councils also engage in social programming for their halls or clusters, and advocate to housing staff in regards to concerns of students in their hall/cluster.</p> 
     <p><b>Student businesses</b></p> 
     <p>The Center for Student Business provides consultation and resources to seven self-sustaining, student-run workers' collectives. Among them are a natural foods nonprofit, a vegetarian caf&eacute;, a bicycle shop, and a print and design shop? all located in the Student Union building? as well as a restaurant and two snack bars, situated in various residential areas.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> The businesses employ a horizontal structure of governance, with all students participating in decision making processes. Responsibilities such as purchasing, bookkeeping, and hiring are delegated to committees whose members are elected to their positions based on availability and interest. Student businesses at the University of Massachusetts employ more than 130 undergraduate students and generate nearly a million dollars in annual revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>UMass Permaculture</b></p> 
     <p>UMass Permaculture is one the first university permaculture initiatives in the nation that transforms marginalized landscapes on the campus into diverse, educational, low-maintenance and edible gardens according to UMass officials.<sup id="cite_ref-Permaculture_garden_at_UMass_gives_new_meaning_to_the_phrase_fresh_vegetables_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Permaculture_garden_at_UMass_gives_new_meaning_to_the_phrase_fresh_vegetables-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> One of the most important aspects of UMass Permaculture is that it comes from the students and is ecologically and socially responsible. Rather than tilling the soil, a more sustainable landscaping method known as <a href="/wiki/Sheet_mulching" title="Sheet mulching">sheet mulching</a> is employed. During November, 2010, &quot;about a quarter of a million pounds of organic matter was moved by hand&quot;,<sup id="cite_ref-UMass_Embraces_Permaculture_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UMass_Embraces_Permaculture-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> using all student and community volunteer labor and no fossil fuels on-site. The process took about two weeks to complete. Now, the Franklin Permaculture Garden includes a diverse mixture of &quot;vegetables, fruit trees, berry bushes, culinary herbs and a lot of flowers that will attract beneficial insects.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-UMass_Embraces_Permaculture_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UMass_Embraces_Permaculture-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Franklin and Berkshire <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> gardens are part of a small closed loop system. <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">Food waste</a> from the dining commons is turned into rich organic <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> at NESFI. The compost is then used to sheet mulch the sites and build up the soil. The food produced in this soil goes back into the dining commons to be eaten and again the food waste will be composted. Food waste is typically an <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a> which ends up in landfills. There is creates methane, a greenhouse gas which contributes to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the gardens ?over 1,500 fruit trees, berry bushes, herbs, flowers, and vegetables? <sup id="cite_ref-umasspermaculture.com_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umasspermaculture.com-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> have been planted. The <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> mimics natural ecosystems, and different plants fill complimentary biological niches. Biodiversity helps create a stable and sturdy environment. The gardens provide ecosystem services such as water infiltration with planned garden beds. Perennial polycultures are another feature of the gardens which create a low-maintenance, high yield food source.</p> 
     <p>The Franklin Permaculture Garden does not use fossil fuels on site. The garden is instead maintained through human labor. The use of fossil fuels in industrial agriculture maximizes the possible profits per unit of land but create an externality of greenhouse gas pollution into the atmosphere and air.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> The choice to use human labor rather than fossil fuels addresses the issues of resource scarcity and climate change as well as to build community.<sup id="cite_ref-umasspermaculture.com_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umasspermaculture.com-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>UMass permaculture fosters <a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Social wealth (page does not exist)">social wealth</a> through community involvement. There have been over 1,000 volunteers since the gardens creation. Student volunteers gain hands on permaculture experience while giving back to the campus. A number of community groups, such as ?Big Brother, Big Sister? have also partaken in creating the gardens. The social capitol created has given UMass permaculture economic paybacks. Their campus popularity won them the UMass senior class gift of $25,000 to start the new Berkshire Permaculture Garden.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> Plants have been donated from community members as well.</p> 
     <p><b>ROTC</b></p> 
     <p>The Minuteman Battalion is the institution's Army ROTC battalion. Active on the Amherst campus, the program's <a href="/wiki/Scabbard_and_Blade" title="Scabbard and Blade">Scabbard and Blade</a> community service club is very active and represents UMass well throughout the year with food drives, assistance to local veteran's groups and assistance with the Medical Readiness Corps at UMass in preparing for large-scale medical disasters. Most students are on a full tuition scholarship. UMass-Amherst is the host program for the Pioneer Valley and Five Colleges Army ROTC programs including: Smith College, Mount Holyoke College, Amherst College, Hampshire College, Western New England College (WNEC), Springfield College, Westfield State College and American International College (AIC).</p>

     <p><b>Cannabis Reform Coalition</b></p>

Extravaganja 2012 was on April 28.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Massachusetts_Daily_Collegian">The Massachusetts Daily Collegian</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The Massachusetts Daily Collegian">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p><i>The Massachusetts Daily Collegian</i>, the official newspaper of UMass Amherst, is published daily Monday through Friday during the calendar semester. The <i>Collegian</i> is a non-profit student run organization which receives no funding from the University or from student fees. The <i>Collegian</i> operates entirely on advertising revenues. Founded in 1890, the paper began as <i>Aggie Life</i>, became the <i>College Signal</i> in 1901, the <i>Weekly Collegian</i> in 1914 and the <i>Tri-Weekly Collegian</i> in 1956. Published daily since 1967, the <i>Collegian</i> has been broadsheet since January 1994. The <i>Daily Collegian</i> is the largest daily college newspaper in New England and one of the largest in the country.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="UVC-TV_19">UVC-TV 19</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: UVC-TV 19">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The Union Video Center is the University of Massachusetts' student-run television station, located in the basement of the Student Union. UVC-TV 19 is part of the University's Housing Cable Services Network and airs on channel 19 to over 11,000 viewers on campus via a closed circuit system. UVC began as the Student Video Project in 1974, and was renamed the Union Video Center in 1978 after growing into a full-fledged television station. Today, UVC-TV 19 serves as an educational training facility on campus for full-time undergraduate students interested in learning about any aspect of television, video production, or cablecasting by providing access to audio and video equipment, studio, and editing workstations. Student employees/members cover campus events and guest lectures, produce original shows, films and documentaries, and air their work on UVC. Membership to UVC gives students access to check out cameras/other video equipment. It also allows each student the opportunity to learn advanced skills for the production field such as training on editing systems and other high quality film equipment. As a Registered Student Organization (RSO), UVC-TV19 allows undergraduate members to participate in the decision-making and day-to-day operations of managing the facility.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="WMUA_91.1_FM">WMUA 91.1 FM</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: WMUA 91.1 FM">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The student-operated radio station, <a href="/wiki/WMUA" title="WMUA">WMUA</a>, is a federally licensed, non-commercial broadcast facility serving the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River_Valley" title="Connecticut River Valley" class="mw-redirect">Connecticut River Valley</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Massachusetts" title="Western Massachusetts">Western Massachusetts</a>, Northern <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, and Southern <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>. Although the station is managed by full-time undergraduate students of the University of Massachusetts, station members can consist of various members of the University (undergraduate and graduate students, faculty and staff), as well as people of the surrounding communities. WMUA began as an AM station in 1949, and today broadcasts music, news, sports, and public affairs programming. The station is located in the basement of the Lincoln Campus Center.</p>
