     <p><b>Purdue University</b>, located in <a href="/wiki/West_Lafayette,_Indiana" title="West Lafayette, Indiana">West Lafayette, Indiana</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/Flagship_university" title="Flagship university" class="mw-redirect">flagship university</a> of the six-campus <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University_system" title="Purdue University system">Purdue University system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue was founded on May 6, 1869, as a <a href="/wiki/Land-grant_university" title="Land-grant university">land-grant university</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_General_Assembly" title="Indiana General Assembly">Indiana General Assembly</a>, taking advantage of the <a href="/wiki/Morrill_Land-Grant_Colleges_Act" title="Morrill Land-Grant Colleges Act" class="mw-redirect">Morrill Act</a>, accepted a donation of land and money from <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Indiana" title="Lafayette, Indiana">Lafayette</a> businessman <a href="/wiki/John_Purdue" title="John Purdue">John Purdue</a> to establish a college of science, technology, and agriculture in his name.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue.edu_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue.edu-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The first classes were held on September 16, 1874, with six instructors and 39 students.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue.edu_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue.edu-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Today, Purdue is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a>, and is a well known world-class research institution. Purdue enrolls the second largest student body of any university in Indiana as well as the fourth largest <a href="/wiki/International_student" title="International student">international student</a> population of any university in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Purdue offers both <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate</a> programs in over 211 <a href="/wiki/Academic_major" title="Academic major">major</a> areas of study, and is well known for its competitive engineering curricula. The university has also been highly influential in America's <a href="/wiki/Aviation_history" title="Aviation history" class="mw-redirect">history of aviation</a>, having established the first <a href="/wiki/Credit_(education)" title="Credit (education)" class="mw-redirect">college credit</a> offered in <a href="/wiki/Flight_training" title="Flight training">flight training</a>, the first four-year bachelor's degree in aviation, and the first university airport (<a href="/wiki/Purdue_University_Airport" title="Purdue University Airport">Purdue University Airport</a>). Purdue's aviation technology program remains one of the most competitive aviation-specific programs in the world. In the mid-20th century, Purdue's aviation program expanded to encompass advanced <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> technology giving rise to Purdue's nickname, <i>Cradle of Astronauts</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Twenty-three Purdue graduates have gone on to become astronauts, including <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a> (one of the original <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Seven" title="Mercury Seven">Mercury Seven</a> astronauts), <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> (the first person to walk on the moon), and <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a> (the most recent person to walk on the moon).</p> 
     <p>Despite having a reputation for its strong engineering and science programs, Purdue's English Department has achieved prominence for having created the first <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://owl.english.purdue.edu/owl/owlprint/608/">Online Writing Lab (OWL)</a> in 1994.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Many colleges and universities use the website for the Purdue OWL as a reference for the proper word usage, punctuation, grammar, and style in <a href="/wiki/Academic_writing" title="Academic writing">academic writing</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:JohnPurdue.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/JohnPurdue.jpg/220px-JohnPurdue.jpg" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/JohnPurdue.jpg/330px-JohnPurdue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/60/JohnPurdue.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:JohnPurdue.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/John_Purdue" title="John Purdue">John Purdue</a>, the university's eponymous benefactor
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In 1865, the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_General_Assembly" title="Indiana General Assembly">Indiana General Assembly</a> voted to take advantage of the <a href="/wiki/Morrill_Land-Grant_Acts" title="Morrill Land-Grant Acts">Morrill Land-Grant Colleges Act of 1862</a>, and began plans to establish an institution with a focus on agriculture and engineering. Communities throughout the state offered their facilities and money to bid for the location of the new college. Popular proposals included the addition of an agriculture department at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a> or at what is now <a href="/wiki/Butler_University" title="Butler University">Butler University</a>. By 1869, <a href="/wiki/Tippecanoe_County" title="Tippecanoe County" class="mw-redirect">Tippecanoe County</a>?s offer included $150,000 from Lafayette business leader and philanthropist <a href="/wiki/John_Purdue" title="John Purdue">John Purdue</a>, $50,000 from the county, and 100 acres (40 ha) of land from local residents. On May 6, 1869, the General Assembly established the institution in Tippecanoe County as Purdue University, in the name of the principal benefactor. Classes began at Purdue on September 16, 1874, with six instructors and 39 students.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue.edu_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue.edu-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Professor <a href="/wiki/John_S._Hougham" title="John S. Hougham">John S. Hougham</a> was Purdue?s first faculty member and served as acting president between the administrations of presidents <a href="/wiki/Abram_C._Shortridge" title="Abram C. Shortridge">Shortridge</a> and White.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue.edu_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue.edu-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> A campus of five buildings was completed by the end of 1874.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue issued its first degree, a Bachelor of Science in chemistry, in 1875 and admitted its first female students that fall.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerson_E._White&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Emerson E. White (page does not exist)">Emerson E. White</a>, the university?s president from 1876 to 1883, followed a strict interpretation of the Morrill Act. Rather than emulate the classical universities, White believed that Purdue should be an &quot;industrial college&quot; and devote its resources toward providing a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_education" title="Liberal education">liberal</a> (or broad) education with an emphasis on science, technology, and agriculture. He intended not only to prepare students for industrial work, but also to prepare them to be good citizens and family members.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Part of White?s plan to distinguish Purdue from classical universities included a controversial attempt to ban fraternities. This ban was ultimately overturned by the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Supreme_Court" title="Indiana Supreme Court" class="mw-redirect">Indiana Supreme Court</a> and led to White?s resignation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The next president, <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_H._Smart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James H. Smart (page does not exist)">James H. Smart</a>, is remembered for his call in 1894 to rebuild the original Heavilon Hall &quot;one brick higher&quot; after it had been destroyed by a fire.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:1904_Panorama_of_campus,_Purdue_Univ.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg/330px-1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg" width="330" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg/495px-1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg/660px-1904_Panorama_of_campus%2C_Purdue_Univ.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:1904_Panorama_of_campus,_Purdue_Univ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> A view of Purdue University in 1904
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>By the end of the nineteenth century, the university was organized into schools of agriculture, engineering (mechanical, civil, and electrical), and pharmacy, and former U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Benjamin Harrison</a> was serving on the board of trustees.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue?s engineering laboratories included testing facilities for a locomotive and a <a href="/wiki/Corliss_steam_engine" title="Corliss steam engine">Corliss steam engine</a>, one of the most efficient engines of the time. The School of Agriculture was sharing its research with farmers throughout the state with its cooperative extension services and would undergo a period of growth over the following two decades. Programs in education and home economics were soon established, as well as a short-lived school of medicine. By 1925 Purdue had the largest undergraduate engineering enrollment in the country, a status it would keep for half a century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>President <a href="/wiki/Edward_C._Elliott" title="Edward C. Elliott">Edward C. Elliott</a> oversaw a campus building program between the world wars. Inventor, alumnus, and trustee David E. Ross coordinated several fundraisers, donated lands to the university, and was instrumental in establishing the Purdue Research Foundation. Ross?s gifts and fundraisers supported such projects as <a href="/wiki/Ross%E2%80%93Ade_Stadium" title="Ross?Ade Stadium">Ross?Ade Stadium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Memorial_Union" title="Purdue Memorial Union">Memorial Union</a>, a civil engineering surveying camp, and <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University_Airport" title="Purdue University Airport">Purdue University Airport</a>. Purdue Airport was the country?s first university-owned airport and the site of the country?s first college-credit flight training courses.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a> joined the Purdue faculty in 1935 as a consultant for these flight courses and as a counselor on women?s careers. In 1937, the Purdue Research Foundation provided the funds for the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Model_10_Electra" title="Lockheed Model 10 Electra">Lockheed Electra 10-E</a> that Earhart flew on her attempted round-the-world flight.</p> 
     <p>Every school and department at the university was involved in some type of military research or training during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> During a project on radar receivers, Purdue physicists discovered properties of <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> that led to the making of the first <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The Army and the Navy conducted training programs at Purdue and more than 17,500 students, staff, and alumni served in the armed forces.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue set up about a hundred centers throughout Indiana to train skilled workers for defense industries.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> As veterans returned to the university under the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Bill" title="G.I. Bill">G.I. Bill</a>, first-year classes were taught at some of these sites to alleviate the demand for campus space. Four of these sites are now degree-granting regional campuses of the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University_system" title="Purdue University system">Purdue University system</a>. Purdue?s on-campus housing became racially desegregated in 1947, following pressure from Purdue President <a href="/wiki/Frederick_L._Hovde" title="Frederick L. Hovde">Frederick L. Hovde</a> and Indiana Governor <a href="/wiki/Ralph_F._Gates" title="Ralph F. Gates">Ralph F. Gates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>After the war, Hovde worked to expand the academic opportunities at the university. A decade-long construction program emphasized science and research. In the late 1950s and early 1960s the university established programs in veterinary medicine, industrial management, and nursing, as well as the first computer science department in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Undergraduate humanities courses were strengthened, although Hovde only reluctantly approved of graduate-level study in these areas. Purdue awarded its first Bachelor of Arts degrees in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The programs in liberal arts and education, formerly administered by the School of Science, were soon split into their own school.</p> 
     <p>In recent years, Purdue?s leaders have continued to support high-tech research and international programs. In 1987, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> visited the West Lafayette campus to give a speech about the influence of technological progress on job creation.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 1990s, the university added more opportunities to study abroad and expanded its course offerings in world languages and cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The first buildings of the Discovery Park interdisciplinary research center were dedicated in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue launched a Global Policy Research Institute in 2010 to explore the potential impact of technical knowledge on public policy decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Engineeringmallentrance.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Engineeringmallentrance.jpg/220px-Engineeringmallentrance.jpg" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Engineeringmallentrance.jpg/330px-Engineeringmallentrance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Engineeringmallentrance.jpg/440px-Engineeringmallentrance.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Engineeringmallentrance.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> View of the entrance to the Purdue Mall
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Purdue's campus is situated just outside the city limits of <a href="/wiki/West_Lafayette,_Indiana" title="West Lafayette, Indiana">West Lafayette</a>, near the western bank of the <a href="/wiki/Wabash_River" title="Wabash River">Wabash River</a>, across which sits the larger city of <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Indiana" title="Lafayette, Indiana">Lafayette</a>. State Street, which is concurrent with <a href="/wiki/Indiana_State_Road_26" title="Indiana State Road 26">State Road 26</a>, divides the northern and southern portions of campus. Academic buildings are mostly concentrated on the eastern and southern parts of campus, with residence halls to the west, and athletic facilities to the north. The <a href="/wiki/Greater_Lafayette_Public_Transportation_Corporation" title="Greater Lafayette Public Transportation Corporation">Greater Lafayette Public Transportation Corporation</a> (CityBus) operates eight campus loop bus routes on which students, faculty, and staff can ride free of charge.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium_Mall">Stadium Mall</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stadium Mall">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG/220px-Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG/330px-Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG/440px-Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Purdue_Engineering_Fountain.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Engineering Fountain
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The Stadium Mall is the central quad of Purdue University. Also known as the Purdue Spine, due to its proximity to several important academic buildings, it was created to connect the academic campus with Ross-Aide Stadium. It is also known as the Engineering Mall, due to its proximity to several engineering buildings. The most prominent feature of the Stadium Mall is at its end (where it intersects the Purdue Mall) is the 38-foot (12&nbsp;m)-tall concrete <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Fountain" title="Engineering Fountain">Engineering Fountain</a>, and also features the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_L._Hovde" title="Frederick L. Hovde">Frederick L. Hovde</a> Hall of Administration, which houses the office of the university president, <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_E._Daniels" title="Mitchell E. Daniels" class="mw-redirect">Mitchell E. Daniels</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Bell_Tower" title="Purdue Bell Tower">Purdue Bell Tower</a> is located between the Stadium and Centennial Malls. The Bell Tower is considered an icon of the university and can be found on many Purdue logos and those of the cities of <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Indiana" title="Lafayette, Indiana">Lafayette</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Lafayette,_Indiana" title="West Lafayette, Indiana">West Lafayette</a>.</p> 
     <p>Southwest of the Stadium Mall is the <a href="/wiki/Edward_C._Elliott_Hall_of_Music" title="Edward C. Elliott Hall of Music" class="mw-redirect">Edward C. Elliott Hall of Music</a>, one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Proscenium" title="Proscenium">proscenium theaters</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue's Student Concert Committee often invites famous entertainers to perform there for an audience of students, faculty, and the general public.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorial_Mall">Memorial Mall</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Memorial Mall">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Purdue_Student_Union.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Purdue_Student_Union.JPG/220px-Purdue_Student_Union.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Purdue_Student_Union.JPG/330px-Purdue_Student_Union.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Purdue_Student_Union.JPG/440px-Purdue_Student_Union.JPG 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Purdue_Student_Union.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Purdue Memorial Union
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The Purdue Memorial Mall, located south of the Purdue Mall, is the original section of campus. A popular meeting place for students, the grassy, open Memorial Mall is surrounded by the Stewart Student Center, Stanley Coulter Hall, the Class of 1950 Lecture Hall, Recitation Building, Winthrop Stone Hall, and University Hall. The Memorial Mall also features the Hello Walk. Near this section of campus is Felix Haas Hall, which was constructed in 1909 as Memorial Gymnasium in memory of the 17 Purdue University football players, coaches, alumni, and fans who perished in the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Wreck" title="Purdue Wreck">Purdue Wreck</a> railroad accident on October 31, 1903. The structure was renovated in 1985 to house the Computer Science department. In 2006, it was renamed in honor of retired Provost Felix Haas and began to also house the Statistics department. East of the Memorial Mall is the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Memorial_Union" title="Purdue Memorial Union">Purdue Memorial Union</a>, Purdue's <a href="/wiki/Student_activity_center" title="Student activity center">student union</a> building, and the adjacent Union Club Hotel.</p> 
     <p>University Hall is the only building remaining from the original six-building campus. Construction began in 1871, when the building was known as &quot;The Main Building&quot;. The building was dedicated in 1877 and the project cost $35,000 to complete. University Hall originally housed the office of the president, a chapel, and classrooms, but was remodeled in 1961 to house only the department of history and classrooms used by the School of Liberal Arts. At the request of <a href="/wiki/John_Purdue" title="John Purdue">John Purdue</a>, he was buried in the Memorial Mall, directly across from the main entrance of University Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The western portion of campus consists of student housing, dining, and recreation facilities. Students can play club and <a href="/wiki/Intramural_sports" title="Intramural sports">intramural sports</a> at the C&oacute;rdova Recreational Sports Center, the Boilermaker Aquatic Center, and the intramural playing fields in this area. The C&oacute;rdova Recreational Sports Center, built in 1957, is the first building in the nation created solely to serve university student recreational needs. As a replacement for the previously separate women's and men's gymnasiums, it was originally called the &quot;Co-recreational Gymnasium&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite several expansions and official name changes, it has been nicknamed &quot;the Co-rec&quot; ever since.</p>

     <p>Purdue offers more than 200 options for major areas of study at the West Lafayette campus alone, and a variety of options for minors.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue is organized into ten colleges and schools. On July 1, 2010 the College of Health and Human Sciences was formed. The new college was created by combining existing academic units. These units include the School of Nursing, the School of Health Sciences, the College of Consumer and Family Sciences, and non-humanities majors from the College of Liberal Arts; namely psychology and hearing and speech pathology.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/University_President" title="University President" class="mw-redirect">University President</a>, appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trustees" title="Board of Trustees" class="mw-redirect">Board of Trustees</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/Chief_administrative_officer" title="Chief administrative officer">chief administrative officer</a> of the university. The office of the president oversees admission and registration, student conduct and counseling, the administration and scheduling of classes and space, the administration of student athletics and organized extracurricular activities, the libraries, the appointment of the faculty and conditions of their employment, the appointment of all non-faculty employees and the conditions of employment, the general organization of the university, and the planning and administration of the university budget.</p> 
     <p>The Board of Trustees directly appoints other major officers of the university including a <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">provost</a>, who serves as the chief academic officer for the university, a number of vice presidents with oversight over specific university operations, and the regional campus chancellors.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University expended $472.7 million in support of research system-wide in 2006?07, using funds received from the state and federal governments, industry, foundations, and individual donors. The faculty and more than 400 research laboratories put Purdue University among the leading research institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue University is considered by the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Classification_of_Institutions_of_Higher_Education" title="Carnegie Classification of Institutions of Higher Education">Carnegie Classification of Institutions of Higher Education</a> to have &quot;very high research activity&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue also was rated the nation's fourth best place to work in academia, according to rankings released in November 2007 by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scientist" title="The Scientist">The Scientist</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue's researchers provide insight, knowledge, assistance, and solutions in many crucial areas. These include, but are not limited to Agriculture; Business and Economy; Education; Engineering; Environment; Healthcare; Individuals, Society, Culture; Manufacturing; Science; Technology; Veterinary Medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Purdue University established the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Park_(Purdue)" title="Discovery Park (Purdue)">Discovery Park</a> to bring innovation through multidisciplinary action.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In all of the eleven centers of Discovery Park, ranging from entrepreneurship to energy and advanced manufacturing, research projects reflect a large economic impact and address global challenges.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue University's nanotechnology research program, built around the new Birck Nanotechnology Center in Discovery Park, ranks among the best in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Research_Park" title="Purdue Research Park">Purdue Research Park</a> which opened in 1961<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> was developed by Purdue Research Foundation which is a private, nonprofit foundation created to assist Purdue. The park is focused on companies operating in the arenas of life sciences, homeland security, engineering, advanced manufacturing and information technology.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> It provides an interactive environment for experienced Purdue researchers and for private business and high-tech industry.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> It currently employs more than 3,000 people in 155 companies, including 90 technology-based firms.<sup id="cite_ref-news.uns.purdue.edu_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.uns.purdue.edu-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> The Purdue Research Park was ranked first by the Association of University Research Parks in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-research_park_rank_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-research_park_rank-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Ten_Committee_on_Institutional_Cooperation">Big Ten Committee on Institutional Cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Big Ten Committee on Institutional Cooperation">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Purdue University is a participant in the <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Institutional_Cooperation" title="Committee on Institutional Cooperation">Committee on Institutional Cooperation</a>. The Committee on Institutional Cooperation (CIC) is the academic consortium of the universities in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> plus former conference member, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>. The initiative also allows students at participating institutions to take distance courses at other participating institutions. The initiative also forms a partnership of research. Engaging in $8 billion in research in 2010, CIC universities are providing powerful insight into important issues in medicine, technology, agriculture, and communities.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Students at participating schools are also allowed &quot;in-house&quot; viewing privileges at other participating schools' libraries.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> They also employ collective purchasing, which has saved member institutions $19 million to date.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Purdue's Sustainability Council, composed of University administrators and professors, meets monthly to discuss environmental issues and sustainability initiatives at Purdue.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainability_Council_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainability_Council-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> The University's first <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> Certified building was an addition to the Mechanical Engineering Building, which was completed in Fall 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Campus_Buildings_.26_Features_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campus_Buildings_.26_Features-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The school is also in the process of developing an arboretum on campus.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue.27s_Sustainability_Initiatives_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue.27s_Sustainability_Initiatives-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, a system has been set up to display live data detailing current energy production at the campus utility plant.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue.27s_Sustainability_Initiatives_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue.27s_Sustainability_Initiatives-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> The school holds an annual &quot;Green Week&quot; each fall, an effort to engage the Purdue community with issues relating to environmental sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-GreenWeek_2008_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenWeek_2008-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The Purdue student body is composed primarily of students from <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>. In 2012?13, 19,689 out of a total of 39,256 students enrolled were Indiana residents.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2013, the racial diversity of the US-resident undergraduate student body was 5.7% <a href="/wiki/Asian_people" title="Asian people">Asian</a>, 4.4% <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino" class="mw-redirect">Latino</a>, and 4.0% <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black</a> or <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ethnicity_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethnicity-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Of the undergraduate students, 42.6% were female.<sup id="cite_ref-gender_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gender-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Domestic minorities constitute a total of 10.8% in the graduate student body population<sup id="cite_ref-ethnicity_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethnicity-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> of which 37.3% are female.<sup id="cite_ref-gender_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gender-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Twenty-two percent of the student body is international, representing 126 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> In graduate and professional student population, non-Indiana residents occupy an overwhelming majority, about 75%.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Almost all undergraduates and about 70% of the graduate student population attend full-time.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The school's selectivity for admissions is &quot;more selective&quot; by <a href="/wiki/USNWR" title="USNWR" class="mw-redirect">USNWR</a>: approximately 70% of applicants are admitted.</p>

     <p>Purdue University operates fifteen separate <a href="/wiki/Residence_hall" title="Residence hall" class="mw-redirect">residence halls</a> for its undergraduate and graduate students, including Cary Quadrangle, <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Earhart</a> Hall, First Street Towers, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Harrison</a> Hall, <a href="/wiki/Hawkins_Hall_at_Purdue_University" title="Hawkins Hall at Purdue University">Hawkins Hall</a>, Hillenbrand Hall, Hilltop Apartments, <a href="/wiki/John_T._McCutcheon" title="John T. The newest residence hall, First Street Towers, opened in July 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are 12 <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a> houses at Purdue (5 men's houses and 7 women's houses). The men's houses include Circle Pines, Fairway, Marwood, Chauncey, and Gemini. The women's houses include Ann Tweedale, Glenwood, Twin Pines, Maclure, Stewart, Devonshire, and Shoemaker. All cooperative houses are governed under the Purdue Cooperative Council which is led by Purdue University students who live in these houses. The cooperative system allows for a much lower cost of living than other types of housing,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> as the members take an active role in sharing chores and cooking all meals themselves, as opposed to hiring out cleaning and cooking staff.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue4_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue4-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Purdue University hosts the nation's third largest <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities" class="mw-redirect">Greek community</a>, with approximately 5,000 students participating in one of the 46 men's fraternities or 29 women's sororities.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Several of Purdue's most distinguished graduates are members of fraternities and sororities.<sup id="cite_ref-purdue4_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdue4-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Purdue's Greek system is very strong and works together in various aspects, including the Inter-Fraternity Council, Panhellenic, and many very successful philanthropies. For example, in 2010, <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Tau_Alpha" title="Zeta Tau Alpha">Zeta Tau Alpha</a>'s philanthropy, Big Man On Campus (BMOC) raised over $125,000 with the help of the rest of the Greek community, all benefitting Breast Cancer awareness and research. Every Chapter has their own national philanthropy dedicated to a certain cause that many chapters also participate in. Besides philanthropy, Purdue Greeks are involved all over campus including College Mentors for Kids, Purdue University Dance Marathon, Boiler Gold Rush, Purdue Student Government and many other various activities.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Purdue_Exponent" title="Purdue Exponent">Purdue Exponent</a></i>, an independent <a href="/wiki/Student_newspaper" title="Student newspaper">student newspaper</a>, has the largest circulation of any Indiana college newspaper, with a daily circulation of 17,500 copies during the spring and fall semesters.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> From 1889 to 2008 Purdue published a <a href="/wiki/Yearbook" title="Yearbook">yearbook</a> called the <i>Debris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/WBAA" title="WBAA">WBAA</a> is a radio station owned by Purdue University. The station operates on the AM frequency of 920&nbsp;kHz and FM frequency of 101.3&nbsp;MHz. Its studios are in the <a href="/wiki/Edward_C._Elliott_Hall_of_Music" title="Edward C. Elliott Hall of Music" class="mw-redirect">Edward C. Elliott Hall of Music</a> on the Purdue campus, and the transmitters are located in <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Indiana" title="Lafayette, Indiana">Lafayette, Indiana</a>. WBAA is the longest continuously-operating radio station in Indiana, having been licensed on April 4, 1922. WBAA airs <a href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio" class="mw-redirect">NPR</a> and local news/talk programming during the day. Overnight, the AM station airs <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> while the FM station airs <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" title="European classical music" class="mw-redirect">classical music</a>.</p> 
     <p>There are also a few <a href="/wiki/Campus_radio" title="Campus radio">campus radio</a> stations on campus. A fourth student station, the Purdue Student Radio club operates from the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Memorial_Union" title="Purdue Memorial Union">Purdue Memorial Union</a> and broadcasts on low power AM in addition to internet streaming.<sup id="cite_ref-WCCRwebsite_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCCRwebsite-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WILYwebsite_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WILYwebsite-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AM1610website_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AM1610website-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHHRwebsite_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHHRwebsite-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Purdue is home to 18 Division I/I-A <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> teams including football, basketball, cross country, tennis, wrestling, golf, volleyball, and others. Traditional rivals include Big Ten colleagues the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_(Bloomington)" title="Indiana University (Bloomington)" class="mw-redirect">Indiana</a> <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Hoosiers" title="Indiana Hoosiers">Hoosiers</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Indiana%E2%80%93Purdue_rivalry" title="Indiana?Purdue rivalry">Indiana?Purdue rivalry</a>), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign" class="mw-redirect">Illinois</a> <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Fighting_Illini" title="Illinois Fighting Illini">Fighting Illini</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">Notre Dame</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighting_Irish" title="Fighting Irish" class="mw-redirect">Fighting Irish</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Big_East_Conference_(1979%E2%80%932013)" title="Big East Conference (1979?2013)">Big East Conference</a> (football program independent, however).</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascots.2C_logos.2C_and_colors">Mascots, logos, and colors</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mascots, logos, and colors">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In the more than 130 years since the founding of the university, several mascots have emerged in support of the Boilermaker athletic teams, including: The <a href="/wiki/Boilermaker_Special" title="Boilermaker Special">Boilermaker Special</a>, <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Pete" title="Purdue Pete">Purdue Pete</a>, and more recently, Rowdy.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Boilermaker_Special" title="Boilermaker Special">Boilermaker Special</a> has been the official mascot of Purdue University since 1940. Designed to look like a train locomotive, the Special was originally designed to demonstrate Purdue's engineering programs and is maintained by the members of the Purdue Reamer Club.</p> 
     <p>As the official mascot of Purdue Athletics, <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Pete" title="Purdue Pete">Purdue Pete</a> is one of the most recognized symbols of Purdue University.</p> 
     <p>An 18-foot (5.5&nbsp;m) statue of &quot;<a href="/wiki/The_Boilermaker" title="The Boilermaker">The Boilermaker</a>&quot; was erected in 2005 across from Ross Ade Stadium.</p> 
     <p>Purdue University adopted its school colors, <a href="/wiki/Old_Gold" title="Old Gold" class="mw-redirect">Old Gold</a> and Black, in the fall of 1887. Members of Purdue's first football team in 1887 felt that the squad should be distinguished by certain colors, and since Princeton was at the time the most successful gridiron unit, its colors were considered. Though actually orange and black, the Princeton colors were known by many as yellow and black. Purdue gridders opted for old gold over yellow, kept the black, and began flying the colors that endure today.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seal">Seal</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Seal">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The official seal of Purdue was officially inaugurated during the University's centennial in 1969. The seal, approved by the Board of Trustees, was designed by Prof. Al Gowan, formerly at Purdue. It replaced one that had been in use for 73 years, but was never officially accepted by the board.</p> 
     <p>In medieval heraldry, a griffin symbolized strength, and Abby P. Lytle used it in her 1895 design for a Purdue seal. When Professor Gowan redesigned the seal, he retained the griffin symbol to continue identification with the older, unofficial seal. As on the older seal, the words &quot;Purdue University&quot; are set in the typeface Uncial. The three-part shield indicates three stated aims of the University: education, research, and service, replacing the words Science, Technology, and Agriculture on the earlier version.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Oaken_Bucket">Old Oaken Bucket</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Old Oaken Bucket">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Found on a farm in southern <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, the oaken bucket is one of the oldest football trophies in the nation. The winner of the annual Purdue vs. <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a> <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> game gets to add a bronze &quot;P&quot; or &quot;I&quot; chain link and keep the trophy until the next face-off. Ironically, the first competition in 1925 led to a 0?0 tie, resulting in the first link on the chain being an &quot;IP.&quot; Purdue currently leads the trophy series at 57-27-3.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiler_Gold_Rush">Boiler Gold Rush</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Boiler Gold Rush">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Boiler Gold Rush (BGR) is Purdue's new-student orientation program. BGR, which takes place before each fall semester, was formed to ease the transition to college for incoming students and to help them get acquainted with successful college life. The Boiler Gold Rush program was formed by Roger Sharritt in 1993. In the first year, Sharritt?s operation, then called Corn Camp, served just 100 new students with around 15 staff.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 1993, the program has gone from being called Corn Camp to Boiler Gold Rush. It started operations in the Cary Quadrangle residence hall, then moved to the Office of Admissions, and eventually became part of the Orientation and New Student Programs (ONSP)office, which reported to the Office of Enrollment Management. BGR is now coordinated by the Student Access, Transition and Success (SATS) office, and has increased to more than 5,500 in 2012. Other significant changes are that the program now takes place the week immediately preceding the beginning of the fall semester and that during BGR, students live in the rooms they will be living in for the rest of the year, which was not the case in the first few years of the program?s development.</p> 
     <p>Boiler Gold Rush activities include speaker presentations from various academic, cultural, safety and professional organizations on campus, campus tours led by Team Leaders, academic 'meet the schools' picnic and interest sessions, late night events at the Purdue Memorial Union, Recreational Sports Center and local stores, and a sports pep rally.</p>

     <p>Best described as a cross between a pep rally and a Halloween party, students and even some alumni dress up in costumes, from traditional Halloween garb to creative hand-made costumes, as they bar-hop before Boilermaker home football games. The Breakfast Club plays a significant role during the football season and is informally a part of Purdue tradition. Many Boilermaker fans are dedicated; getting up at 5&nbsp;am on Saturdays and lining up at the bars on Chauncey Hill and the levee by 6&nbsp;am.</p> 
     <p>The tradition started with the Lambda Chapter of the Phi Kappa Tau fraternity. The fraternity would wake up early in the morning on the morning of home football games and purchase enough beer kegs that they would get a truck with tap spouts. They invited several other &quot;Greeks&quot; and would even go into their houses in the morning to wake them up for the tradition. Bar owners began to realize that college students, alumni, and even parents in some cases had a demand for this and started opening up their establishments to take advantage of what was going on. Thus Breakfast Club was born and quickly grew to the entire student body.</p> 
     <p>Costumes came in to play at a later date. A few students were denied being served at one of the bars because they had &quot;reached their limit&quot;. They went home, put on a costume, and were to be served again. Soon the costume idea caught on and more and more students were dressing up in costumes to their bar-hop tradition.</p> 
     <p>This tradition also takes place the day of the annual Grand Prix race in April.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculty">Faculty</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purdue_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Faculty">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The original faculty of six in 1874 has grown to 2,563 tenured and tenure-track faculty in the Purdue Statewide System by Fall 2007 totals. The number of faculty and staff members system-wide is 18,872.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> The current faculty includes scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Shreeram_Shankar_Abhyankar" title="Shreeram Shankar Abhyankar">Shreeram Shankar Abhyankar</a> ? known for his contributions to <a href="/wiki/Singularity_theory" title="Singularity theory">singularity theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arden_L._Bement_Jr." title="Arden L. Bement Jr." class="mw-redirect">Arden L. Bement Jr.</a> ? Director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Graham_Cooks" title="R. Graham Cooks">R. Graham Cooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Francisco" title="Joseph Francisco">Joseph Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Comer" title="Douglas Comer">Douglas Comer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Branges_de_Bourcia" title="Louis de Branges de Bourcia">Louis de Branges de Bourcia</a> who proved the <a href="/wiki/Bieberbach_conjecture" title="Bieberbach conjecture" class="mw-redirect">Bieberbach conjecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ei-ichi_Negishi" title="Ei-ichi Negishi">Ei-ichi Negishi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Raskin" title="Victor Raskin">Victor Raskin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Rossmann" title="Michael Rossmann">Michael Rossmann</a> who mapped human common <a href="/wiki/Cold_virus" title="Cold virus" class="mw-redirect">cold virus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leah_Jamieson" title="Leah Jamieson">Leah Jamieson</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_L._Mohler" title="James L. Mohler">James L. Mohler</a>, who has written several manuals of computer graphics, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brent_D._Bowen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brent D. Bowen (page does not exist)">Brent D. Bowen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> whose research on aviation and airline quality is viewed annually by millions, and <a href="/wiki/H._Jay_Melosh" title="H. Jay Melosh">H. Jay Melosh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Purdue's tenured faculty comprises sixty <a href="/wiki/Dean_(education)" title="Dean (education)">Academic Deans</a>, Associate Deans, and Assistant Deans; 63 Academic Department Heads; 753 Professors; 547 Associate Professors; and 447 Assistant Professors. Purdue employs 892 non-tenure-track faculty, Lecturers, and Postdoctorals at its West Lafayette campus. Purdue employs another 691 tenured and 1,021 Non-Tenure Track Faculty, Lecturers, and Postdoctorals at its Regional Campuses and Statewide Technology.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Two faculty members (chemists <a href="/wiki/Herbert_C._Brown" title="Herbert C. Brown">Herbert C. Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ei-ichi_Negishi" title="Ei-ichi Negishi">Ei-ichi Negishi</a>) have been awarded Nobel Prizes while at Purdue. In all, 13 Nobel Prizes in five fields have been associated with Purdue including students, researchers, and current and previous faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> Other notable faculty of the past have included Golden Gate Bridge designer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alton_Ellis" title="Charles Alton Ellis">Charles Alton Ellis</a>, efficiency expert <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Gilbreth" title="Lillian Gilbreth" class="mw-redirect">Lillian Gilbreth</a>, food safety advocate <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Wiley" title="Harvey Wiley" class="mw-redirect">Harvey Wiley</a>, aviator <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a>, radio pioneer <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Fessenden" title="Reginald Fessenden">Reginald Fessenden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yeram_S._Touloukian" title="Yeram S. Touloukian">Yeram S. Touloukian</a>, founder of the Thermophysical Properties Research Center.</p>

     <p>Purdue alumni have achieved recognition in a range of areas, particularly in science and engineering industry. The university's alumni pool collectively holds over 15,000 United States patents.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Purdue has produced 23 astronauts, including <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a>, the first vertically launched person to return to space, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>, the first to walk on the moon, and <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a>, the most recent astronaut to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> Over one third of all of NASA's manned space missions have had at least one Purdue graduate as a crew member.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In science, Purdue has also produced Nobel prize winning physicists in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mills_Purcell" title="Edward Mills Purcell">Edward Mills Purcell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Roy_Mottelson" title="Ben Roy Mottelson">Ben Roy Mottelson</a>; as well as Nobel prize winning chemist <a href="/wiki/Akira_Suzuki_(chemist)" title="Akira Suzuki (chemist)">Akira Suzuki</a>. Other noted Purdue alumni in science include pioneer of robotics and remote control technology <a href="/wiki/Thomas_B._Sheridan" title="Thomas B. Sheridan">Thomas B. Sheridan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debian" title="Debian">Debian</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Ian_Murdock" title="Ian Murdock">Ian Murdock</a>, Founding father and key contributor to the Chinese nuclear weapon programs: Chinese physicist <a href="/wiki/Deng_Jiaxian" title="Deng Jiaxian">Deng Jiaxian</a>, biochemist <a href="/wiki/Edwin_T._Mertz" title="Edwin T. Mertz">Edwin T. Mertz</a>, who is credited with discovery of high-protein corn and beans.</p> 
     <p>In business and economics, Purdue alumni include <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Bechtel,_Jr." title="Stephen Bechtel, Jr.">Stephen Bechtel, Jr.</a>, owner of <a href="/wiki/Bechtel" title="Bechtel">Bechtel</a> Corporation; Federal Reserve Bank president <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Lacker" title="Jeffrey Lacker" class="mw-redirect">Jeffrey Lacker</a>; and popcorn specialist <a href="/wiki/Orville_Redenbacher" title="Orville Redenbacher">Orville Redenbacher</a>. In 2010, Bloomberg also revealed that Purdue was one of the universities in America with the most undergraduate alumni serving as chief executive officers of Standard &amp; Poor's 500 firms.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> They are <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Wasson" title="Gregory Wasson">Gregory Wasson</a>, president/CEO of Walgreen Co, Mark Miller, chairman/president/CEO of Stericycle Inc, Charles Davidson, chairman/CEO of Noble Energy Inc, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Allen" title="Samuel Allen" class="mw-redirect">Samuel Allen</a>, chairman /president/CEO of Deere &amp; Co., Donald Thompson, president/COO of McDonald's Corp., and John Martin, chairman/CEO of Gilead Sciences Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>In sports, Purdue has produced basketball coach <a href="/wiki/John_Wooden" title="John Wooden">John Wooden</a>, basketball Hall of Famers <a href="/wiki/Stretch_Murphy" title="Stretch Murphy">Stretch Murphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piggy_Lambert" title="Piggy Lambert" class="mw-redirect">Piggy Lambert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rick_Mount" title="Rick Mount">Rick Mount</a>; NBA Champions <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hoffman_(basketball)" title="Paul Hoffman (basketball)">Paul Hoffman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herm_Gilliam" title="Herm Gilliam">Herm Gilliam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Kendrick" title="Frank Kendrick">Frank Kendrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Sichting" title="Jerry Sichting">Jerry Sichting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Robinson" title="Glenn Robinson">Glenn Robinson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cardinal" title="Brian Cardinal">Brian Cardinal</a> and NBA All-Stars <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Robinson" title="Glenn Robinson">Glenn Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brad_Miller_(basketball)" title="Brad Miller (basketball)">Brad Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Dischinger" title="Terry Dischinger">Terry Dischinger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Barry_Carroll" title="Joe Barry Carroll">Joe Barry Carroll</a>. Purdue also produced <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_IV" title="Super Bowl IV">Super Bowl IV</a> winning coach <a href="/wiki/Hank_Stram" title="Hank Stram">Hank Stram</a>. Daytona 500 winner <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Newman" title="Ryan Newman">Ryan Newman</a> graduated from Purdue with a Bachelor's degree in vehicle structure engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
