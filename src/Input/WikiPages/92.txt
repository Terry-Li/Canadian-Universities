     <p><b>George Mason University</b> (<b>GMU</b>), frequently referred to as <b>Mason</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public university</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, south of and adjacent to the city of <a href="/wiki/Fairfax,_Virginia" title="Fairfax, Virginia">Fairfax</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Additional campuses are located nearby in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">Arlington County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_William_County,_Virginia" title="Prince William County, Virginia">Prince William County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Loudoun_County,_Virginia" title="Loudoun County, Virginia">Loudoun County</a>. The university's <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> is <i>Freedom and Learning</i> while its <a href="/wiki/Slogan" title="Slogan">slogan</a> or tagline is <i>Where Innovation Is Tradition</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Named after <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">revolutionary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">patriot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">founding father</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Mason" title="George Mason">George Mason</a>, the university was founded as a branch of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> in 1957 and became an independent institution in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Today, Mason is recognized for its strong programs in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creative_writing" title="Creative writing">creative writing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In recent years, George Mason faculty have twice won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Economics" title="Nobel Prize in Economics" class="mw-redirect">Nobel Prize in Economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The university enrolls over 32,500 students, making it the largest university by head count in the Commonwealth of Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a> passed a resolution in January 1956, establishing a branch college of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Virginia" title="Northern Virginia">Northern Virginia</a>. In September 1957 the new college opened its doors to seventeen students, all of whom enrolled as freshmen in a renovated elementary school building at <a href="/wiki/Bailey%27s_Crossroads" title="Bailey's Crossroads" class="mw-redirect">Bailey's Crossroads</a>. John Norville Gibson Finley served as Director of the new branch, which was known as University College.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:George_Mason.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/George_Mason.jpg/200px-George_Mason.jpg" width="200" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/George_Mason.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/George_Mason.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:George_Mason.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/George_Mason" title="George Mason">George Mason</a>, (1725–1792) after whom the University is named.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The city of Fairfax purchased and donated 150 acres (0.61&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) of land to the University of Virginia for the college's new site,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> which was referred to as the <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_University_Fairfax_Campus" title="George Mason University Fairfax Campus" class="mw-redirect">Fairfax Campus</a>. In 1959, the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Visitors" title="Board of Visitors" class="mw-redirect">Board of Visitors</a> of UVA selected a permanent name for the college: George Mason College of the University of Virginia. The Fairfax campus construction planning that began in early 1960 showed visible results when the development of the first 40 acres (160,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) of Fairfax Campus began in 1962. In the Fall of 1964 the new campus welcomed 356 students.</p> 
     <p>Local jurisdictions of <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">Arlington County</a>, and the cities of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falls_Church,_Virginia" title="Falls Church, Virginia">Falls Church</a> agreed to appropriate $3 million to purchase land adjacent to Mason to provide for a 600-acre (2.4&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) Fairfax Campus in 1966 with the intention that the institution would expand into a regional university of major proportions, including the granting of graduate degrees.</p> 
     <p>On April 7, 1972 the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a> enacted legislation which separated George Mason College from its parent institution, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>. Renamed that day by the legislation, George Mason College became George Mason University.</p> 
     <p>In 1978, the George Mason University Foundation purchased the former Kann's department store in Arlington. In March 1979 the Virginia General Assembly authorized the establishment of the George Mason University School of Law (GMUSL) – contingent on the transfer of the Kann's building to George Mason University. GMUSL began operations in that building on July 1, 1979 and received provisional accreditation from the American Bar Association in 1980. The ABA granted full approval to GMUSL in 1986.</p> 
     <p>Also, in 1979, the university moved all of its athletic programs to <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> Division I. Enrollment that year passed 11,000. The university opened its Arlington campus in 1982, two blocks from the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Square-GMU_(Washington_Metro)" title="Virginia Square-GMU (Washington Metro)" class="mw-redirect">Virginia Square-GMU station</a> in Arlington. In 1986 the university's governing body, the Board of Visitors, approved a new master plan for the year based on an enrollment of 20,000 full-time students with housing for 5,000 students by 1995. That same year university housing opened to bring the total number of residential students to 700.</p> 
     <p>Through a bequest of Russian immigrant Shelley Krasnow the University established the <a href="/wiki/Krasnow_Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Krasnow Institute for Advanced Study">Krasnow Institute for Advanced Study</a> in 1991. The Institute was created to further the understanding of the mind and intelligence by combining the fields of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurobiology" title="Neurobiology" class="mw-redirect">neurobiology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>. In 1992, Mason's new Prince William Institute began classes in a temporary site in <a href="/wiki/Manassas,_Virginia" title="Manassas, Virginia">Manassas, Virginia</a>. The Institute moved to a permanent 124-acre (0.50&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) site located on the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Route_234" title="Virginia State Route 234">Rt. 234</a> bypass, ten miles (16&nbsp;km) south of Manassas, by the year 1997, and is now known as the Prince William Campus. The university graduated more than 5,000 students that following spring.</p> 
     <p>While George Mason University is young compared to established research universities in Virginia, it has grown rapidly, reaching an enrollment of 30,714 students in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> According to a 2005 report issued by the university, enrollment is expected to reach 35,000 students by 2011 with more than 7,000 resident students.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2002, Mason celebrated its 30th anniversary as a university by launching its first capital campaign, trying to raise $110 million. The school raised $142 million, $32 million more than its goal. The George Mason University logo, originally designed in 1982, was updated in 2004.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_School_of_Management" title="George Mason School of Management">School of Management</a> celebrated its 30th anniversary. Also, in 2008 Mason changed its mascot from the &quot;Gunston&quot; animal to the &quot;Patriot&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fairfax_Campus">Fairfax Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fairfax Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>The main campus of George Mason University is situated on 677 acres (2.74&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) just south of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Fairfax,_Virginia" title="City of Fairfax, Virginia" class="mw-redirect">City of Fairfax, Virginia</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County</a>, approximately 15 miles (24&nbsp;km) west of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The Fairfax campus is served on the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metro" title="Washington Metro">Washington Metro</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_(WMATA_station)" title="Vienna (WMATA station)">Vienna</a> station on the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Line_(Washington_Metro)" title="Orange Line (Washington Metro)">Orange line</a>. A 15 minute shuttle in addition to the <a href="/wiki/City-University-Energysaver" title="City-University-Energysaver" class="mw-redirect">CUE</a> bus, free for students with a Mason ID card, serves the students through routes from the Metro station to the University.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>In the early 1960s four buildings were constructed around a lawn in Fairfax, appropriately named East, West, North (later, Krug Hall), and South (later, Finley Hall). The first four structures, today dubbed &quot;The Original Four,&quot; &quot;around a lawn&quot; were understood as a clear reference to the buildings around <a href="/wiki/The_Lawn" title="The Lawn">The Lawn</a> of the University of Virginia in <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">Charlottesville</a>. In addition, in the words of the architects, the architecture of the buildings was meant to reflect <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Jeffersonian</a> influence through the use of red brick with buff colored mortar, white vertical columns, and sloped shingled roofs.</p> 
     <p>Master plans were developed to incorporate further development, which saw new additions such as Fenwick Library and Lecture Hall. By 1979 master plan development was handled by the firm of <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Sasaki" title="Hideo Sasaki">Sasaki &amp; Associates</a>, which continued to work alongside the university in the years that followed. Student housing first became available in 1977. The 1980s saw the university expand with a new building being added on each year, including the <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Center" title="Patriot Center">Patriot Center</a>. As well as the construction of the Fairfax campuses network of hot and cold water piping that provides power efficient, centralized heating and cooling for the university's buildings.</p> 
     <p>Recent years have once again brought a new construction boom to the Fairfax campus, which is currently undergoing a massive, $900 million construction campaign (between 2002–2012) based on the 2002 University Master Plan.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> This has brought about a huge influx of new buildings to campus, and renovations of existing buildings, most recently:</p>

     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_campus_buildings">Notable campus buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable campus buildings">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Johnson_Center">Johnson Center</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Johnson Center">edit</a>]</span></h5> 
     <p>The George W. Johnson Learning Center, more commonly known as the Johnson Center or JC, is the central hub on campus, completed in 1995 and named after University President of 18 years, George W. Johnson. Located in the center of campus, the $30 million, 320,000-square-foot (30,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) building was built as the first of its kind building on any American campus, acting both as a <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Student_activity_center" title="Student activity center">student union</a>. The ground floor includes a buffet style restaurant named the Bistro, the campus radio station <a href="/wiki/WGMU_Radio" title="WGMU Radio">WGMU Radio</a>, a coffee shop named Jazzman's, 300-seat movie theater, and Dewberry Hall. The main floor includes the campus bookstore, a large food court with several fast food restaurants, a patisserie and the ground floor of the library. The second and third floors of the Johnson Center are primarily used by the library, with multiple group meeting rooms, computer labs, a news and media resource, and a full service restaurant named George's located on the third floor.</p> 
     <p>The Johnson Center serves as the center for student life with many activities and productions sponsored by Program Board and Student Government. In 2004 during the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)_presidential_primaries,_2004" title="Democratic Party (United States) presidential primaries, 2004" class="mw-redirect">Democratic Primaries</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a>, the eventual Democratic Nominee for President, visited George Mason University and gave a speech on the floor of the Johnson Center. In 2007, shortly after announcing on his website that he would establish a <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_committee" title="Exploratory committee">presidential exploratory committee</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> gave a speech at the &quot;Yes We Can&quot; rally at the Johnson Center atrium. The next week he formally announced his intentions of running for president.</p>

     <h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Fenwick_Library">Fenwick Library</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fenwick Library">edit</a>]</span></h5> 
     <p>Fenwick Library was originally built in 1967, with additions in 1974, a tower in 1983, and renovations in 2005–2006. It was named for Charles Rogers Fenwick, one of George Mason's founders. Fenwick Library is the main <a href="/wiki/Research_library" title="Research library" class="mw-redirect">research library</a> at George Mason. Its resources include: most of the university's books, microfilms, print and bound journals, government documents, and maps. Electronic resources include networked and stand-alone <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROMs</a>, the libraries' <a href="/wiki/OPAC" title="OPAC" class="mw-redirect">online catalog</a>, a number of databases available through the libraries' membership in various consortia, and <a href="/wiki/Internet_access" title="Internet access">Internet access</a>. Another important collection of research materials housed in Fenwick is the Government Documents collection. This collection includes both federal and Virginia state documents. Both sets of documents contain items from the <a href="/wiki/Executive_branch" title="Executive branch" class="mw-redirect">administrative</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">legislative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judicial_branch" title="Judicial branch" class="mw-redirect">judicial</a> branches of government, and constitute an invaluable source of primary source materials for students and faculty in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">public policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> and other fields. There is also a special GIS center in Fenwick Library which conducts <a href="/wiki/GIS" title="GIS" class="mw-redirect">GIS</a> drop-in sessions every week.</p> 
     <p>George Mason University is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Consortium_of_Universities_of_the_Washington_Metropolitan_Area" title="Consortium of Universities of the Washington Metropolitan Area">Consortium of Universities of the Washington Metropolitan Area</a>, granting it access to resources of thirteen other libraries in the District of Columbia.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ras_Al_Khaimah">Ras Al Khaimah</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ras Al Khaimah">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>George Mason opened a 'campus' in the <a href="/wiki/Ras_Al_Khaimah" title="Ras Al Khaimah" class="mw-redirect">Ras Al Khaimah</a> emirate of the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> No one ever graduated from the Ras al Khaimah 'campus' and it never grew beyond one building.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The Ras Al Khaimah 'campus' nominally offered three undergraduate Bachelors of Science degrees in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, business administration, and <a href="/wiki/Electronics_and_communications_engineering" title="Electronics and communications engineering" class="mw-redirect">electronics and communications engineering</a>. They subsequently added a course in &quot;educational leadership and management.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>On February 27, 2009, Mason announced they would close the Ras Al Khamimah campus at the end of the Spring 2009 semester. University Provost, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stearns" title="Peter Stearns">Peter Stearns</a>, cited that the relationship between George Mason University and the partner foundation in RAK worked smoothly until early 2009. He explained that the foundation would be reducing the financial support as well as attempting to change the academic reporting structure. In an e-mail to students Stearns wrote, &quot;We have not been able to reach agreement with our RAK partner on a budget and administrative structure that, in our judgment, assures our ability to provide an education that meets Mason standards.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The university has strength in the basic and applied sciences with critical mass in <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computational sciences</a>. Research support comes to Mason faculty from such agencies as the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency" class="mw-redirect">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a>. Likewise, the Center for Secure Information Systems is designated as a Center of Academic Excellence (CAE) as well as a Center of Academic Excellence in Research (CAE-R) in Information Assurance Education by the National Security Agency.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Mason's <a href="/wiki/Center_for_History_and_New_Media" title="Center for History and New Media" class="mw-redirect">Center for History and New Media</a> attracts more than one million visitors to its websites every month.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It curates such digital archives as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://911digitalarchive.org/">The September 11th Digital Archive</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.hurricanearchive.org/">The Hurricane Digital Memory Bank</a>, which documents the records of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>.</p> 
     <p>The Fairfax campus also serves as the headquarters for the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Writers_%26_Writing_Programs" title="Association of Writers &amp; Writing Programs">Association of Writers &amp; Writing Programs</a>.</p> 
     <p>Mason's Center for Global Education's <a href="/wiki/Study_abroad" title="Study abroad" class="mw-redirect">study abroad</a> program has been rated highly offering dozens of programs ranging from one-week spring break programs to full year programs.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p> 
     <p>Mason's flagship Study Abroad experience is the prestigious Oxford Honors Program in which highly qualified students endure a rigorous application and interview process and, if selected, travel to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> where they study for 6–12 months as matriculated students of <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University" class="mw-redirect">Oxford University</a>.</p> 
     <p>Mason was awarded $25 million in 2005 from the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Allergy_and_Infectious_Diseases" title="National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases">National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases</a> (NIAID), part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>, for construction of a Regional Biocontainment Laboratory at the Prince William Campus in <a href="/wiki/Manassas,_Virginia" title="Manassas, Virginia">Manassas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools_and_colleges">Schools and colleges</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Schools and colleges">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Research at Mason is organized into centers, laboratories, and collaborative programs.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> These include the College of Humanities and Social Sciences, the College of Education and Human Development, New Century College, the College of Health and Human Services, the College of Visual and Performing Arts, the Institute for Conflict Analysis and Resolution, the <a href="/wiki/Krasnow_Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Krasnow Institute for Advanced Study">Krasnow Institute for Advanced Study</a>, the School of Computational Sciences, the <a href="/wiki/Volgenau_School_of_Information_Technology_and_Engineering" title="Volgenau School of Information Technology and Engineering">Volgenau School of Information Technology and Engineering</a>, the <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_University_School_of_Law" title="George Mason University School of Law">School of Law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_University_School_of_Public_Policy" title="George Mason University School of Public Policy">School of Public Policy</a>, the College of Science, and the <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_School_of_Management" title="George Mason School of Management">School of Management</a>. In addition, Mason's Office of the <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">Provost</a> includes research centers that deal with economics, global education, and teaching excellence.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <p>In addition to a business undergraduate major and minor, Mason's <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_School_of_Management" title="George Mason School of Management">School of Management</a> has graduate programs for the Master of Business Administration degree (MBA) with a wide variety of concentrations/specializations, an Executive Master of Business Administration degree (EMBA), a Master of Science in Accounting (MSA), a joint MBA/MSA degree and a Master of Science in Technology Management degree.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_Patriots" title="George Mason Patriots">George Mason Patriots</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_Patriots_men%27s_basketball" title="George Mason Patriots men's basketball">George Mason Patriots men's basketball</a>&nbsp;and 
      <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_George_Mason_Patriots_men%27s_basketball_team" title="2005–06 George Mason Patriots men's basketball team">2005–06 George Mason Patriots men's basketball team</a>

     <p>The school's sports teams are called the Patriots. The university's men's and women's sports teams participate in the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a>, and are members of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_10_Conference" title="Atlantic 10 Conference">Atlantic 10 Conference</a>, or A-10. The school's colors are green and gold. George Mason has two NCAA Division I National Championship to its credit; <a href="/wiki/1985_in_sports" title="1985 in sports">1985</a> <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Women%27s_Soccer_Championship" title="NCAA Women's Soccer Championship" class="mw-redirect">Women's Soccer</a> and <a href="/wiki/1996_in_sports" title="1996 in sports">1996</a> <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Indoor_Track_and_Field_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Indoor Track and Field Championship">Men's Indoor Track &amp; Field</a>.</p> 
     <p>George Mason University was catapulted into the national spotlight in March 2006, when its men's basketball team advanced to the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">Final Four</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2006_NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="2006 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament">NCAA Men's Basketball Tournament</a> by defeating the <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Spartans_men%27s_basketball" title="Michigan State Spartans men's basketball">Michigan State Spartans</a>, the defending champion <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_North_Carolina_Tar_Heels_men%27s_basketball_team" title="2005–06 North Carolina Tar Heels men's basketball team">North Carolina Tar Heels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wichita_State_Shockers_men%27s_basketball" title="Wichita State Shockers men's basketball">Wichita State Shockers</a>, and the top-seeded <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Huskies_men%27s_basketball" title="Connecticut Huskies men's basketball">Connecticut Huskies</a>. Their &quot;<a href="/wiki/Cinderella_(sports)" title="Cinderella (sports)">Cinderella</a>&quot; journey ended in the Final Four with a loss to the eventual tournament champion <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Florida_Gators_men%27s_basketball_team" title="2005–06 Florida Gators men's basketball team">Florida Gators</a> by a score of 73–58.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> As a result of the team's success in the tournament, the Patriots were ranked 8th in the final <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a>/<a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a> Poll for the <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_NCAA_Division_I_men%27s_basketball_season" title="2005–06 NCAA Division I men's basketball season">2005–06 season.</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Sun" title="Baltimore Sun" class="mw-redirect">Baltimore Sun</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> featured the story on their front pages, and was ranked by several publications as the sports story of the year.</p> 
     <p>The Patriots, who had never won an NCAA tournament game before 2006, became the first team from the CAA to crash the Final Four and were the first true <a href="/wiki/Mid-major" title="Mid-major">mid-major</a> conference team since 1979 to do so (that year, the <a href="/wiki/Larry_Bird" title="Larry Bird">Larry Bird</a>-led <a href="/wiki/1978%E2%80%9379_Indiana_State_Sycamores_men%27s_basketball_team" title="1978–79 Indiana State Sycamores men's basketball team">Indiana State Sycamores</a> as a #1 seed, and the <a href="/wiki/Penn_Quakers_men%27s_basketball" title="Penn Quakers men's basketball">Penn Quakers</a> as a #9 seed both reached the Final Four). As #11-seeds, the 2006 Patriots also tied the 1986 <a href="/wiki/LSU_Tigers" title="LSU Tigers" class="mw-redirect">LSU Tigers</a> as the lowest-seeded team ever to reach the Final Four.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NCAA_Division_I_men%27s_basketball_season" title="2007–08 NCAA Division I men's basketball season">2008</a>, the Patriots returned to the <a href="/wiki/2008_NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="2008 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament">NCAA Tournament</a> after winning the CAA Tournament. They were given a 12 seed and matched up against 5th-seeded <a href="/wiki/2007-08_Notre_Dame_Fighting_Irish_men%27s_basketball_team" title="2007-08 Notre Dame Fighting Irish men's basketball team" class="mw-redirect">Notre Dame</a>. The Patriots were unable to make another miracle run, losing to the Irish by a score of 68–50.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>George Mason offers more than 200 clubs and organizations, including 16 <a href="/wiki/Fraternities" title="Fraternities" class="mw-redirect">fraternities</a>, 15 <a href="/wiki/Sororities" title="Sororities" class="mw-redirect">sororities</a>, 24 International-student organizations, 25 religious organizations, a student programming board, <a href="/wiki/Student_government" title="Student government" class="mw-redirect">student government</a>, club sports, debate team, and student media. In a 2004 survey of 357 universities GMU was ranked number one for diversity.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The Office of Student Involvement at Mason administrates Student Government, Program Board, Fraternity and Sorority Life, Recognized Student Organization (RSO), Graduate and Professional Student Association (GAPSA), and Weekends at Mason (WAM).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Mason also offers an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> <a href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC" class="mw-redirect">ROTC</a> program, called The &quot;Patriot Battalion.&quot; Mason's club sports include <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_(sport)" title="Ultimate (sport)">ultimate frisbee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">crew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a>, football, lacrosse, <a href="/wiki/Underwater_hockey" title="Underwater hockey">underwater hockey</a>, fencing, and <a href="/wiki/College_rugby" title="College rugby">rugby</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The George Mason University Forensics program is one of the top ranked competitive speech teams in the United States and has achieved international recognition in the field of communication studies.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> The team was founded in 1970 and has won nearly 10,000 individual speech awards. From 2010 to 2012 the team placed 4th at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Forensics_Association_National_Individual_Events_Tournament&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Forensics Association National Individual Events Tournament (page does not exist)">American Forensics Association National Individual Events Tournament</a> and has won the International Forensics Association Championship each of the last five years. The Forensics Program has been extremely active on the George Mason campus with an active Community Service Committee. GMU hosts the annual Virginia is for Lovers collegiate speech tournament, the Patriot Games Classic (which in 2009 had over 1,000 entries), and hosted the 2011 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Forensics_League_Grand_National_Tournament&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Catholic Forensics League Grand National Tournament (page does not exist)">Catholic Forensics League Grand National Tournament</a>. Currently Dr. Peter Pober is the Program Director with Jeremy Hodgson as Assistant Director.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The George Mason University Debate Team is a nationally ranked program. The team participates in the <a href="/wiki/Policy_debate" title="Policy debate">Policy Debate</a> activity. It competes regionally, as well as frequently attending the <a href="/wiki/National_Debate_Tournament" title="National Debate Tournament">National Debate Tournament</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cross_Examination_Debate_Association" title="Cross Examination Debate Association">Cross Examination Debate Association</a> National Tournament, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Debate_Association" title="American Debate Association">American Debate Association</a> National Tournament. The current and only ever Director of Debate is Dr. Warren Decker, who personally started the team in 1975. In the 2012-2013 season, the debate team was ranked first in NDT-CEDA points for a majority of the season, ultimately finishing second behind Liberty University (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.tabroom.com/jbruschke/NDTChecker.php">See NDT-CEDA Rankings</a>). The team is regionally and nationally competitive with teams in the Novice, Junior Varsity, and Varsity divisions. The team's official page is <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://masondebate.pbworks.com/w/page/20463608/Home">here</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Mason also sponsors several student-run publications through its Office of Student Media,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> including <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.Connect2Mason.com">Connect2Mason.com</a>, an online media and news convergence website,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> the <i>VoxPop</i>, a feature magazine,<i>Volition</i>, an undergraduate student literary and art magazine, <i>Phoebe</i>, a graduate literary journal, <i>So to Speak</i>, a feminist literary journal, <i>GMView and Senior Speak</i>, an annual yearbook publication and video, <i>New Voices in Public Policy</i>, School of Public Policy student journal, and <i>Hispanic Culture Review</i>, a student bilingual (Spanish/English) journal on Hispanic literature and culture. Mason also sponsors several academic journals including, '<b>TABLET, the International Affairs Journal of George Mason University'</b>. Between approximately 1993 and 1998, the University was also the home of <i>The Fractal: Journal of Science Fiction and Fantasy</i>. Mason offers one regular print publication, <i><a href="/wiki/Broadside_(newspaper)" title="Broadside (newspaper)">Broadside</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Student_newspaper" title="Student newspaper">student newspaper</a>. Mason also operates a <a href="/wiki/Campus_radio" title="Campus radio">Campus radio</a> station, <a href="/wiki/WGMU_Radio" title="WGMU Radio">WGMU Radio</a>. The online radio station offers music, entertainment, news, and public affairs relating to the University community, regional area and the country. GMU-TV is the university's professional <a href="/wiki/Distance_learning" title="Distance learning" class="mw-redirect">Educational-access television</a> station. GMU-TV is an award-winning leader in educational, informational and public interest programming. The unit offers a broad spectrum of content, ranging from public affairs and humanities to science, medicine and the arts. The Mason Cable Network, or MCN, is the student organized and operated station, and offers student produced entertainment and information on campus channel 89, available on the Fairfax campus of GMU.</p> 
     <p>Between 1990 and 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Underground_newspaper" title="Underground newspaper" class="mw-redirect">underground newspaper</a> <i>Expulsion</i> was distributed on the Mason campus. It also experienced a brief online resurgence in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The staff of the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_History_and_New_Media" title="Center for History and New Media" class="mw-redirect">Center for History and New Media</a> produces a podcast called <i>Digital Campus</i>.</p> 
     <p>In fall 2008, the satirical online newspaper, <i>The Mason Squire</i>, premiered.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> The site featured fake news stories criticizing the university. The newspaper's mottos were &quot;Because fake news doesn't report itself&quot; and &quot;Fake news just got a whole lot sexier&quot;. However, the site has been inactive since late 2009.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraternity_and_sorority_life">Fraternity and sorority life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Mason_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Fraternity and sorority life">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fraternities_and_Sororities_at_George_Mason_University" title="List of Fraternities and Sororities at George Mason University" class="mw-redirect">List of Fraternities and Sororities at George Mason University</a>
     </div> 
     <p>George Mason University does not have traditional Fraternity &amp; Sorority housing or a &quot;Greek row.&quot; For several years, three Panhellenic Council organizations had established &quot;Living/Learning Floors&quot; in the University Commons. <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Omicron_Pi" title="Alpha Omicron Pi">Alpha Omicron Pi</a> had a floor 2004–2010, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Phi_Beta" title="Gamma Phi Beta">Gamma Phi Beta</a> had a floor 2006–2010, and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi" title="Alpha Phi">Alpha Phi</a> had a floor 2007–2010.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Officially, Mason refers to &quot;Greek Life&quot; as &quot;Fraternity &amp; Sorority Life&quot; to avoid confusion with the Hellenic Society club, a student organization focusing on the people and culture of Greece.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Most organizations in the Interfraternity Council (IFC) and Panhellenic Council (PHC) hold one or two large charitable events each year. Most organizations in the National Pan-Hellenic Council(NPHC) and Multicultural Greek Council (MGC) hold a series of smaller charitable events throughout the year. The NPHC is also known for its annual Step Show.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The most well-known<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2010)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> event associated with Fraternity &amp; Sorority Life on campus is held each spring and is called Greek Week. This annual event includes competitive sporting and trivia events, charitable fund raising, and is usually ended with Greek Sing. Organizations participating in Greek Sing put together 10–15 minute themed shows which have included extravagant costumes, set designs, lighting displays, multimedia presentations, dances, singing, acrobatics, and more.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>PHC holds a formal recruitment each fall. Informal recruitment is held in spring. Many PHC organizations also offer continuous open recruitment (or continuous open bidding) after the designated recruitment period. IFC has a designated one-week rush period in the fall and spring. This week is regulated and monitored, but participants are not registered or tracked.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
