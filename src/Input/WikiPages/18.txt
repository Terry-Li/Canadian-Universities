     <p><b>Carleton University</b> is a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/National_Capital_Region_(Canada)" title="National Capital Region (Canada)">capital</a> of Canada, <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. The enabling legislation is <i>The Carleton University Act, 1952, S.O. 1952.</i> Originally founded on rented premises in 1942, Carleton would grow in size to meet the needs of returning <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> veterans and later became Ontario's first private, non-denominational college. It would expand further in the 1960s, consistent with government policy that saw increased access to higher education as a social good and means to economic growth, and is today a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public university</a>, offering more than 65 academic programs across a wide range of disciplines. Carleton is reputed for its strength in a variety of fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_business" title="International business">international business</a> and many of the disciplines housed in its Faculty of Public Affairs (including <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international affairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">administration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Legal_studies" title="Legal studies" class="mw-redirect">legal studies</a>).</p> 
     <p>It is named after the former <a href="/wiki/Carleton_County,_Ontario" title="Carleton County, Ontario">Carleton County, Ontario</a>, which included the city of Ottawa at the time Carleton was founded. Carleton County, in turn, was named in honour of <a href="/wiki/Guy_Carleton,_1st_Baron_Dorchester" title="Guy Carleton, 1st Baron Dorchester">Guy Carleton, 1st Baron Dorchester</a>, an early Governor-General of <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a>. Carleton currently houses more than 22,000 undergraduate and more than 3,000 postgraduate students. Its campus is located west of <a href="/wiki/Old_Ottawa_South" title="Old Ottawa South">Old Ottawa South</a>, within close proximity to <a href="/wiki/The_Glebe" title="The Glebe">The Glebe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confederation_Heights" title="Confederation Heights">Confederation Heights</a>, and is bounded to the north by the <a href="/wiki/Rideau_Canal" title="Rideau Canal">Rideau Canal</a> and Dow's Lake and to the south by the <a href="/wiki/Rideau_River" title="Rideau River">Rideau River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The university is represented in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Carleton_Ravens" title="Carleton Ravens">Carleton Ravens</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>I learned very early the life lesson that it is people, not buildings, that make up an institution. And if we put our hearts to it we can do something worthwhile. -- <i>Henry Marshall Tory</i></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg/220px-Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg/330px-Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Carleton_pres_Tory.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Henry_Marshall_Tory" title="Henry Marshall Tory">Henry Marshall Tory</a>, first President of Carleton College
       </div>

     <p>Carleton College, a non-denominational institution, was founded in 1942<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War" class="mw-redirect">Second World War</a> by the Ottawa Association for the Advancement of Learning.<sup id="cite_ref-Carleton_Act_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carleton_Act-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>It was originally located in a rented building and only offered night courses in public administration and introductory university subjects. When the war ended in 1945, the College began expanding to meet the needs of veterans coming home. The Faculty of Arts and Science was established, which included courses in <a href="/wiki/Carleton_School_of_Journalism" title="Carleton School of Journalism">journalism</a> and first-year engineering.</p> 
     <p>In 1946 the college moved to <a href="/wiki/The_Glebe" title="The Glebe">The Glebe</a> neighbourhood along First Avenue at the former <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Ladies%27_College" title="Ottawa Ladies' College">Ottawa Ladies' College</a>. Its first degrees were conferred in 1946 to graduates of its programs in Journalism and Public Administration.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For nearly a decade the College operated on a shoestring budget, with funds raised mainly through community initiatives and modest student fees. During the war, student fees were kept low and Carleton gave special grants to <a href="/wiki/Veterans" title="Veterans" class="mw-redirect">veterans</a> returning home who wished to continue their studies. The faculty was composed largely of part-time professors who worked full-time in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">Public Service</a>; some of whom were convinced to leave for full-time tenure positions. However, full-time teaching staff were still mostly young scholars at the beginning of their careers.</p> 
     <p>In 1952 the <i>Carleton College Act</i> was passed by the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Legislature" title="Ontario Legislature" class="mw-redirect">Ontario Legislature</a>, changing the official corporate name to Carleton College and officially conferring the power to grant degrees. Carleton thus became the province's first private, non-sectarian college.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In the same year, the 62 hectare property nestled between the <a href="/wiki/Rideau_Canal" title="Rideau Canal">Rideau Canal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rideau_River" title="Rideau River">Rideau River</a> on which the current campus is located was acquired. Some of the land was donated by a prominent Ottawa businessman <a href="/wiki/Harry_Stevenson_Southam" title="Harry Stevenson Southam">Harry Stevenson Southam</a>. Construction began on the new campus in 1953.</p> 
     <p>In 1957 the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.carleton.ca/secretariat/act/index.html#RSO_1937"><i>Carleton University Act</i>, 1952</a> was amended, officially granting Carleton status as a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public university</a> and thus changing its name to Carleton University.<sup id="cite_ref-Carleton_Act_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carleton_Act-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Carleton's motto, &quot;Ours the Task Eternal,&quot; is taken from <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>'s poem, <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneers!_O_Pioneers!" title="Pioneers! O Pioneers!">Pioneers! O Pioneers!</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The governance was modelled on the provincial University of Toronto Act of 1906 which established a bicameral system of university government consisting of a senate (faculty), responsible for academic policy, and a board of governors (citizens) exercising exclusive control over financial policy and having formal authority over all other matters. The president, appointed by the board, was to provide a link between the two bodies and to perform institutional leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1959 construction was completed on the new Rideau River campus, and Carleton moved to its current location.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The original buildings included three that still stand today, the Maxwell MacOdrum Library, Norman Paterson Hall and the Henry Marshall Tory Building. Following this, Carleton rapidly expanded to meet the need for tertiary education in Canada.</p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Guy_carleton_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>

     <p>The policy of university education initiated in the 1960s responded to population pressure and the belief that higher education was a key to social justice and economic productivity for individuals and for society.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1967, a Catholic institution, Saint Patrick's College, was incorporated into Carleton. Founded in 1942, it had been granting its diplomas via the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ottawa" title="University of Ottawa">University of Ottawa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pound_2005_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pound_2005-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Both University of Ottawa and Saint Patrick's had been inaugurated by the Catholic order Oblates of Immaculate Mary (OMI). The college was housed in a building on Echo Drive, near the Pretoria Bridge. Around 1973, a new building was erected on the Carleton campus proper. The college was dissolved as a separate entity after the 1979 academic year. Its final dean was Gerald Clarke who had been a professor from 1954. It had been known for its school of Social Work.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> To this day, Carleton's School of Social Work continues to offer undergraduate and graduate programs.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Improvements in Carleton's financial situation have resulted in many enhancements to the campus. These include, <i>inter alia</i>, the $30 million construction of new athletics facilities and the $22 million, 9,011&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup> (97,000&nbsp;ft<sup>2</sup>) Human Computer Interaction (HCI) Institute Facility and Centre for Advanced Studies in Visualization and Simulation (V-SIM). More well-known, perhaps, is the $17 million upgrade and expansion to the University Centre. In 2008, a green globe designed residence was added named Frontenac House.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Affairs">Public Affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public Affairs">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Many of Carleton's flagship offerings are housed in the Faculty of Public Affairs (FPA). This includes the <a href="/wiki/Carleton_School_of_Journalism" title="Carleton School of Journalism">School of Journalism and Communication</a>, which offers the university's Bachelor of Journalism and Master of Journalism programs<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> and has educated many leading personalities in the field,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Paterson_School_of_International_Affairs" title="Norman Paterson School of International Affairs">Norman Paterson School of International Affairs</a> (NPSIA), which houses Canada's oldest foreign affairs graduate program. NPSIA, founded in 1965, is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Professional_Schools_of_International_Affairs" title="Association of Professional Schools of International Affairs">Association of Professional Schools of International Affairs</a> (APSIA).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Carleton_University%27s_School_of_Public_Policy_and_Administration" title="Carleton University's School of Public Policy and Administration">School of Public Policy and Administration</a> is the oldest such academic division in Canada and one of the most respected, with the university's first graduate degree in the discipline having being granted in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Carleton's <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Kroeger_College_of_Public_Affairs" title="Arthur Kroeger College of Public Affairs">Arthur Kroeger College of Public Affairs</a> offers the unique honours Bachelor of Public Affairs and Policy Management (B.P.A.P.M) and is home to the Clayton H. Riddell Graduate Program in Political Management.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In September 2006, Carleton was designated a European Union Centre of Excellence by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and was the first university to offer a BA (Honours) in European and Russian Studies and MA in European, Russian and Eurasian Studies.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Its Department of Law &amp; Legal Studies offers a BA (Honours) in Law and MA and Ph.D. programs in Legal Studies, and is Canada's oldest legal department to take an <a href="/wiki/Epistemic" title="Epistemic" class="mw-redirect">epistemic</a>, rather than professional approach.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The Department of Political Science, which offers both undergraduate and graduate programs, was ranked 1st in 2006 amongst Canadian comprehensive universities based on total publications and citations by Research Infosource Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The faculty also features the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www1.carleton.ca/politicaleconomy/">Institute of Political Economy</a>, the Institute of <a href="/wiki/Criminology" title="Criminology">Criminology and Criminal Justice</a> and <a href="/wiki/African_Studies" title="African Studies" class="mw-redirect">African Studies</a>, and is home to the School of <a href="/wiki/Social_Work" title="Social Work" class="mw-redirect">Social Work</a> and Department of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">Economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_Social_Sciences">Arts and Social Sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arts and Social Sciences">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The Faculty of Arts and Social Sciences (FASS) offers a variety of programs leading to the Bachelor of Arts (BA), Bachelor of Arts (Honours), Bachelor of Arts (Combined Honours), and Bachelor of Music degrees.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> It also notably houses the <i>College of the Humanities</i>, one of Canada's few <a href="/wiki/Great_Books" title="Great Books" class="mw-redirect">Great Books</a> programs, which leads to a B.Hum (Bachelor of Humanities) degree,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> and Carleton's Institute of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_Science" title="Cognitive Science" class="mw-redirect">Cognitive Science</a>, which offers the only fully structured Ph.D. program in Cognitive Science in the country, as well as undergraduate and masters programs.<sup id="cite_ref-CogSci_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CogSci-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> FASS offers, in total, 14 master's and nine doctoral programs.<sup id="cite_ref-CogSci_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CogSci-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The Faculty of Science offers programs leading to the Bachelor of Science (BSc), Bachelor of Computer Science, Bachelor of Mathematics, Master of Science, Master of Computer Science, and Ph.D.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Business">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Sprott_School_of_Business" title="Sprott School of Business">Sprott School of Business</a> was the first in Canada to offer a Bachelor of International Business (BIB).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Its principal undergraduate offering, however, is the 4-year Bachelor of Commerce (Honours) degree, and at the postgraduate level both MBA and Ph.D. programs are also offered.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The Sprott School has won the Overall Institution Performance Award, for its research contribution, at the Administrative Sciences Association of Canada (ASAC), in 2004, 2005, 2006, 2007, 2008, 2010, and 2012<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> among business schools at Canadian comprehensive universities.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_and_Design">Engineering and Design</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Engineering and Design">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Carleton's <i>Faculty of Engineering and Design</i> houses one of the country's first <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Design" title="Industrial Design" class="mw-redirect">Industrial Design</a> programs,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Carleton's collaborative <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Information_Technology" title="Bachelor of Information Technology">Bachelor of Information Technology</a> (BIT) program with <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_College" title="Algonquin College">Algonquin College</a>, the university's <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">Architecture</a> program, and programs in a variety engineering disciplines leading to the Bachelor of Engineering (B.Eng) degree, including Canada's oldest in <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_Engineering" title="Aerospace Engineering" class="mw-redirect">Aerospace Engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admissions">Admissions</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Admissions">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Undergraduate admission requirements vary by academic program, with some specialized and limited enrolment offerings (e.g. Bachelor of Journalism, B.Hum., B.P.A.P.M and Aerospace Engineering) requiring admissions averages markedly higher (i.e. in the A-/A range) than their faculty norms (generally in the B range).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Many undergraduates find it difficult to retain their entrance scholarship, adding to their financial burden. Only 18 percent of Carleton undergraduates retain their scholarship.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>At the postgraduate level, admissions requirements also vary depending on the program, and the university provides significant funding to support students as they complete their programs of study and research, totalling $43 million in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The Carleton campus was the subject of art exhibit conceived by local artist Adrian Gr&ouml;llner. The MODERN U project sought to highlight the late <a href="/wiki/Modernist_architecture" title="Modernist architecture" class="mw-redirect">modernist architecture</a> exemplified by many of Carleton's early buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The buildings of the campus are connected to each other via an extensive underground tunnel system, which avails students of the need to walk outside when traveling across campus; an especially useful asset during rainy periods and cold winters.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university is served by the <a href="/wiki/OC_Transpo" title="OC Transpo">OC Transpo</a>, which operates the <a href="/wiki/O-train" title="O-train" class="mw-redirect">O-train</a>—linking the university to <a href="/wiki/Mechanicsville,_Ottawa" title="Mechanicsville, Ottawa">Mechanicsville</a> in the north and South Keys in the south—as well as multiple bus routes. The university is served by routes <a href="/wiki/OC_Transpo_Route_4" title="OC Transpo Route 4">4</a>, 111, and 7.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_accommodation">Student accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Student accommodation">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Carleton has eleven student residences. Each is either a traditional dorm or a suite-style residence.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Traditional style residences include Dundas House, Glengarry House, Grenville House, Lanark House, Lennox and Addington House, Renfrew House, Russell House and Stormont House. Suite-style residences include Leeds House, Frontenac House, and Prescott House. The Houses — all named after counties in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ontario" title="Eastern Ontario">Eastern Ontario</a> — are inter-connected and linked to the rest of the University by Carleton's tunnel system. The university's residence facilities currently house more than 3,000 students during the academic year, and serve both undergraduates and postgraduates.<sup id="cite_ref-rrra.ca_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rrra.ca-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_projects">Building projects</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Building projects">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In the 2010-2011 school year, three more buildings were built and an addition to an existing building began. River Building, Canal Building, and Lennox-Addington Residence were newly constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-CanalBld_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanalBld-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Canal Building will both house classes and serve as an extension to the Engineering faculties; River Building will house the School of Journalism and Communication, the Norman Paterson School of International Affairs and the School of Public Policy and Administration.<sup id="cite_ref-CanalBld_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanalBld-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Lastly, an extension was added to the Residence Commons building. In the 2011-2012 school year, the university announced the extension of the Library, to be completed in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_Forces">Canadian Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canadian Forces">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tleft">

     <p>Each summer, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> use Carleton residence facilities—notably Stormont and Dundas Houses and the Residence Commons dining hall—to house and feed the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_Guard" title="Ceremonial Guard">Ceremonial Guard</a>. The Guard performs daily parades on <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Hill" title="Parliament Hill">Parliament Hill</a>, and mounts sentries at <a href="/wiki/Rideau_Hall" title="Rideau Hall">Rideau Hall</a> and the War Memorial. The Guard marches and drills at Carleton between June and August, and it is possible to watch formations carrying rifles in full ceremonial uniform marching to parking lots 6 and 7 to prepare for their daily parade.<br style="clear:both;" /></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarships_.26_bursaries">Scholarships &amp; bursaries</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Scholarships &amp; bursaries">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Carleton University has joined Project Hero, a scholarship program co-founded by General (Ret'd) <a href="/wiki/Rick_Hillier" title="Rick Hillier">Rick Hillier</a> for the families of fallen <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> members.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Government of Canada sponsors an Aboriginal Bursaries Search Tool that lists over 680 scholarships, bursaries, and other incentives offered by governments, universities, and industry to support Aboriginal post-secondary participation. Carleton University scholarships for <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">Aboriginal</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis" title="M&eacute;tis">M&eacute;tis</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> students include the Gordon Robertson National Inuit Scholarship.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archives_and_Research_Collections.2C_Carleton_University_MacOdrum_Library">Archives and Research Collections, Carleton University MacOdrum Library</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Archives and Research Collections, Carleton University MacOdrum Library">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Carleton is home to the <a href="/wiki/MacOdrum_Library" title="MacOdrum Library" class="mw-redirect">MacOdrum Library</a>, named after former Carleton President and Vice-Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Murdoch_Maxwell_MacOdrum" title="Murdoch Maxwell MacOdrum">Murdoch Maxwell MacOdrum</a>, whose Archives and Research Collections includes more than three million items. The W. McAllister Johnson collection houses rare books. The Modern Poetry collection includes poetry broadsides. The archival research collections include Heritage Conservation Research Collection. The Carleton University heritage material includes student newspapers, yearbooks, university heritage photograph collection and ephemera. <sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Its collections include the Maps, Data and Government Information Centre (MADGIC), the Geographic Information Systems (GIS) Collection, and Special Collections &amp; Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-servicepoints_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-servicepoints-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Included in the Special Collections &amp; Archives collection are many of the papers, drawings and digital records of renowned Canadian architect <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Cardinal" title="Douglas Cardinal">Douglas Cardinal</a>, which led to the Douglas Cardinal Archives Project <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> which includes interviews conducted through the Carleton Centre for Public History <sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> about his work. <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are two resource centres at the university: an Audio Visual Resource Centre,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> and a European and Russian Studies resource centre.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Other research facilities include the Herzberg Laboratories, Life Sciences Research Building, H.H.J. Nesbitt Biology Building, National Wildlife Research Centre, and Social Sciences Research Building.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_unions_and_services">Student unions and services</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Student unions and services">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>All undergraduate students are members of the <a href="/wiki/Carleton_University_Students%27_Association" title="Carleton University Students' Association">Carleton University Students' Association</a> (CUSA), <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Federation_of_Students" title="Canadian Federation of Students">Canadian Federation of Students</a> Local 1.<sup id="cite_ref-Member_Local_Students.27_Unions_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Member_Local_Students.27_Unions-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> It was founded in 1942 and has a long history of being a nucleus of political activity<sup id="cite_ref-cusaonline.ca_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cusaonline.ca-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> The organization advocates on behalf of undergraduates, organizes and delivers the annual <a href="/wiki/Frosh_week" title="Frosh week" class="mw-redirect">frosh week</a> in conjunction with the university, certifies and financially supports student-run clubs and societies and provides a variety of services to students. Students elect an executive and council members to represent them and their academic units within CUSA on an annual basis.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> The organization administers a number of student centres designed to cater to the safety and well-being of various members of the student body; these are the Aboriginal Service Centre, BECAMPS (for mature students), the Carleton Disability Awareness Centre, Food Centre, Foot Patrol, GLBTQ centre, International Students' Centre, Race, Ethnicity and Cultural Hall, and the Womyn's Centre.<sup id="cite_ref-cusaonline.ca_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cusaonline.ca-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> It also runs a number of businesses: Oliver's, the undergraduate student pub which hosts a range of events throughout the year;<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Rooster's Coffeehouse, a cafe that serves a variety of non-alcoholic refreshments and fast foods;<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Henry's, a convenience store;<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> and Haven Books, a discount textbooks outlet<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Undergraduate students who live in the university's residence facilities are also members of the Rideau River Residence Association (RRRA). Founded in 1968 and incorporated in 1976, student members elect executives and floor representatives to the body, which endeavours to represent the interests of Carleton's undergraduate residents.<sup id="cite_ref-rrra.ca_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rrra.ca-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> It hosts a variety of events for resident students, including an annual formal,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> and runs Abstentions, a convenience store located in Residence Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>All of the university's graduate students are members of the Carleton University Graduate Students' Association (GSA), Canadian Federation of Students Local 78.<sup id="cite_ref-Member_Local_Students.27_Unions_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Member_Local_Students.27_Unions-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Graduate students elect an executive and council members to represent their respective interests within the organization, which in turn advocates on their behalf and provides a variety of services that cater to postgraduates, which include the operation of a 'Grad Lounge' and graduate students' pub called Mike's Place (named after the late Prime Minister Lester B. Pearson), and the provision of access to a variety of office services.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_media">Arts and media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Arts and media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The student newspaper is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Charlatan" title="The Charlatan">The Charlatan</a></i>, which celebrated its 60th anniversary in 2005. A newspaper for residence students, <i>The Resin</i>, is also published. During the school year the School of Journalism publishes a community newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/Centretown_News" title="Centretown News">Centretown News</a></i>, which reports on the <a href="/wiki/Centretown" title="Centretown">Centretown</a> neighbourhood of Ottawa, and an online newspaper, <i>Capital News Online</i>, as well as producing <i>Midweek</i>, a 90-minute current affairs radio show which is broadcast to the city. There is also the student-run writers' zine, <i>In/Words</i>, which is sponsored by the Department of English Language and Literature, as well as <i>The Iron Times</i>, published by the Carleton Student Engineering Society.</p> 
     <p>Carleton is home to a <a href="/wiki/Community_radio" title="Community radio">community radio</a> station, <a href="/wiki/CKCU-FM" title="CKCU-FM">CKCU-FM</a>. Broadcasting for the first time on 14 November 1975, CKCU-FM was the first licensed community-based campus radio station in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>While Carleton does not have a theatre department, its student-driven <a href="/wiki/Sock_%27n%27_Buskin_Theatre_Company" title="Sock 'n' Buskin Theatre Company">Sock 'n' Buskin Theatre Company</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> which was founded in 1943, is one of the institution's longstanding fixtures.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Carleton_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The men's Raven's basketball team has won the national championship nine times between 2002 and 2013 with five consecutive titles (between 2002–03 and 2006–07) surpassing the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">University of Victoria</a> at the top of the all-time list.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Vikes" title="Victoria Vikes">Vikes</a> had seven consecutive wins in the 1980s.</p> 
     <p>After being abolished on March 3, 1999 due to a lack of success and the ensuing financial burden, the Ravens' Football team will be making a comeback in the 2013 season.<sup id="cite_ref-SunFootball_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunFootball-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> The idea for revival was first brought forward by the <i>Old Crow Society</i>, which represents Carleton Football's alumni, in 2000, but it was deemed that such a move would be too premature at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-SunFootball_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunFootball-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> Subsequently, a 2008 survey indicated that 86% of students were in favour of resuscitating the university's football program.<sup id="cite_ref-SunFootball_69-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunFootball-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> The team will form an independent corporate entity with its own revenue stream—a model that has proven successful at other Canadian schools, notably <a href="/wiki/Laval_University" title="Laval University">Laval University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SunFootball_69-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunFootball-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Past chancellors include two <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel</a> laureates; pioneering scientist <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Herzberg" title="Gerhard Herzberg">Gerhard Herzberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Lester_Bowles_Pearson" title="Lester Bowles Pearson" class="mw-redirect">Lester B. His company has designed a revolutionary new camera that uses <a href="/wiki/Light_field" title="Light field">light field</a> technology.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

Atkinson">David W. Atkinson</a> and his <i>pro tempore</i> (acting) successor <a href="/wiki/Samy_Mahmoud" title="Samy Mahmoud">Samy Mahmoud</a>, the previous Vice-President (academic).<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
