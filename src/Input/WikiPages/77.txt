     <p>The <b>University of Rochester</b> (<b>U of R</b>, <b>UR</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonsectarian" title="Nonsectarian">nonsectarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university" class="mw-redirect">research university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The university grants <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graduate_degree" title="Graduate degree" class="mw-redirect">graduate degrees</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctoral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professional_degree" title="Professional degree" class="mw-redirect">professional degrees</a>. The university has six schools and various interdisciplinary programs.</p> 
     <p>The University of Rochester is noted for its <a href="/wiki/Eastman_School_of_Music" title="Eastman School of Music">Eastman School of Music</a>. The university is also home to the Institute of Optics, founded in 1929, the nation's first educational program devoted exclusively to optics.<sup id="cite_ref-rochester.edu_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rochester.edu-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Rochester's <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Laser_Energetics" title="Laboratory for Laser Energetics">Laboratory for Laser Energetics</a> is home to the second most powerful <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In its history five university alumni, two faculty, and one senior research associate at <a href="/wiki/Strong_Memorial_Hospital" title="Strong Memorial Hospital">Strong Memorial Hospital</a> have been awarded a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a>; eight alumni and four faculty members have won a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>, and 19 faculty members have been awarded a <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellowship" title="Guggenheim Fellowship">Guggenheim Fellowship</a>. Faculty and alumni of Rochester make up nearly one-quarter of the scientists on the board advising <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in the development of the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a>, which will replace the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> as of 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-rochester.edu_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rochester.edu-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Rochester, across all of its schools and campuses, enrolls approximately 5,600 undergraduates and 4,600 graduate students. Its 158 buildings house over 200 academic majors. Additionally, Rochester (along with its affiliated Strong Health System) is the largest employer in the Greater Rochester area and the sixth largest employer in <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a> State.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Rochester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>The University of Rochester was founded in 1850 as a Baptist-sponsored institution. The impetus to form the university came primarily from the town of <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_(town),_New_York" title="Hamilton (town), New York" class="mw-redirect">Hamilton, New York</a>, which has been home to <a href="/wiki/Colgate_University" title="Colgate University">Colgate University</a> since 1819.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1848, the Baptist Education Society planned to move Colgate University (then known as Madison University) to the city of Rochester, but was halted by legal action. Dissenting Colgate trustees, faculty, and students founded the University of Rochester, receiving a charter from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_State_of_New_York" title="University of the State of New York">Regents of the University of the State of New York</a> on January 31, 1850. Classes began that November, with approximately 60 students enrolling, including 28 transfers from Madison.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">

     <p>The University of Rochester's campus was originally in downtown Rochester at the United States Hotel, which was located on Buffalo Street near Elizabeth Street, which today is West Main Street near the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_490_(New_York)" title="Interstate 490 (New York)">I-490</a> overpass. In 1853, the campus moved east to a then-suburban location on what is now University Avenue. Local businessman and Congressman <a href="/wiki/Azariah_Boody" title="Azariah Boody">Azariah Boody</a> donated 8 acres (3.2&nbsp;ha) of land for the new campus, and the University purchased a further 17 acres (6.9&nbsp;ha) from him.<sup id="cite_ref-May.2C_Arthur_J._1977_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-May.2C_Arthur_J._1977-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> UR would remain on this campus until the current River Campus was constructed in 1930, and the university continues to own a small part of the University Avenue campus (where the university-owned Memorial Art Gallery is located).</p> 
     <p>The first female students were admitted in 1900, the result of an effort led by <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Barrett_Montgomery" title="Helen Barrett Montgomery">Helen Barrett Montgomery</a>. During the 1890s, a number of women took classes and labs at the university as &quot;visitors&quot; but were not officially enrolled nor were their records included in the college register. President <a href="/wiki/David_Jayne_Hill" title="David Jayne Hill">David Jayne Hill</a> allowed the first woman, Helen E. Wilkinson, to enroll as a normal student, although she was not allowed to matriculate or to pursue a degree. Thirty-three women enrolled among the first class in 1900, and Ella S. Wilcoxen was the first to receive a degree, in 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-May.2C_Arthur_J._1977_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-May.2C_Arthur_J._1977-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> When the River Campus was completed in 1930, male students moved there while the female students remained on the University Avenue campus until 1955.</p> 
     <p>Major growth occurred under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rush_Rhees" title="Benjamin Rush Rhees">Rush Rhees</a>, during his 1900-1935 tenure. During this time, <a href="/wiki/George_Eastman" title="George Eastman">George Eastman</a> became a major donor, giving more than $50 million to the university. The first <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D." class="mw-redirect">Ph.D.</a> was awarded in 1925. In 1955, the separate colleges for men and women were merged into <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_College_of_Arts_Sciences_and_Engineering" title="University of Rochester College of Arts Sciences and Engineering">The College</a>. In 1958, three new schools were created in <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Rochester was one of 131 colleges and universities nationally that took part in the <a href="/wiki/V-12_Navy_College_Training_Program" title="V-12 Navy College Training Program">V-12 Navy College Training Program</a> which offered students a path to a Navy commission.<sup id="cite_ref-rochester-v-12_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rochester-v-12-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Between 1946 and 1947, in infamous <a href="/wiki/Human_experimentation_in_the_United_States#Uranium_experiments" title="Human experimentation in the United States" class="mw-redirect">uranium experiments</a> researchers at the university injected <a href="/wiki/Uranium-234" title="Uranium-234">uranium-234</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a> into six people to study how much uranium their kidneys could tolerate before becoming damaged.<sup id="cite_ref-goliszek-136-137_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goliszek-136-137-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the mid-1980s, the University commissioned a study to determine if the name of the institution should be changed, most likely to &quot;Eastman University.&quot; The study concluded that a name change could be beneficial because the use of a place name in the title led respondents to incorrectly believe that it was a public university, and because the name &quot;Rochester&quot; connoted a &quot;cold and distant outpost.&quot; Reports of the latter conclusion led to controversy and criticism in the Rochester community. Ultimately, the name &quot;University of Rochester&quot; was retained.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1995, university president <a href="/wiki/Thomas_H._Jackson" title="Thomas H. Jackson">Thomas H. Jackson</a> announced the launch of a &quot;Renaissance Plan&quot; for The College that, among several changes, reduced enrollment and created a more selective admissions process. The plan also revised the undergraduate curriculum significantly, creating the current system with only one required course and only a few distribution requirements (known as &quot;clusters&quot;).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The <b>River Campus</b> is the center of the university's academic and administrative activities. It is located in a bend of the <a href="/wiki/Genesee_River" title="Genesee River">Genesee River</a> about 2 miles (3&nbsp;km) south of downtown Rochester and covers around 200 acres (81&nbsp;ha). It is bounded by Bausch &amp; Lomb Riverside Park, an 18-acre (7.3&nbsp;ha) public park along the east bank of the Genesee River, and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hope_Cemetery,_Rochester" title="Mount Hope Cemetery, Rochester" class="mw-redirect">Mount Hope Cemetery</a>, where the grave sites of <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a> can be found.</p> 
     <p>The original buildings of the campus were dedicated in 1930. The main academic buildings, designed in the Greek revival style, are centered around the <a href="/wiki/George_Eastman" title="George Eastman">Eastman Quadrangle</a> (generally referred to as the academic quad) which is formed by <a href="/wiki/Rush_Rhees_Library" title="Rush Rhees Library">Rush Rhees Library</a> and Dewey, Bausch &amp; Lomb, Morey, and Lattimore Halls. The Eastman Quad is widely considered the best landscaped area of the university. Rush Rhees Library, the unofficial symbol of the university, is also home to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_carillons#Eastern_United_States" title="List of carillons">Hopeman Memorial Carillon</a>, the largest carillon in New York State, featuring 50 bells that chime on the quarter hour. During the summer, the carillon features a recital series in which various artists perform on the instrument.</p> 
     <p>Over the course of the last several decades, other academic buildings have been built south of the Eastman Quad, including Meliora Hall (1972), Hoyt Hall (1962), Harkness Hall (1946), Gavett Hall (dedicated with the Eastman Quad in 1930), and the Hopeman Engineering Building (1963). The southernmost part of the River Campus contains the new Science and Engineering Quadrangle: Hutchison Hall (1972), Hylan Building (1971), the Computer Studies Building and Carlson Library (1987), Wilmot Building (1961), the <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Goergen" title="Robert B. Goergen">Robert B. Goergen</a> Hall for Biomedical Engineering and Optics (2007), and LeChase Hall (2013). <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The recently constructed LeChase Hall (dedicated in May 2013) houses the Warner School of Education and is the first building to be constructed on the Wilson Quadrangle in 30 years. The building is named after R. Wayne LeChase, a university trustee whose donation helped make construction of the building possible.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> A four-story, 65,000-square-foot facility, it provides a unified home for the Warner School and features an expansive suite of 14 classrooms on the first floor that serves the College during the day and the Warner School in the evening. The Warner School is housed on the upper three floors, unified by a dramatic three-story atrium, with additional classrooms, offices and spaces specifically designed to support the preparation and professional development of educators and the conduct of educational research and reform work.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Students often congregate outdoors during the warmer months on the various quads. Other centers of student life include Todd Union, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a> Dining Center, various locations inside Rush Rhees Library, and Wilson Commons, a student union designed by the architectural firm of <a href="/wiki/I._M._Pei" title="I. M. Pei">I.M. Pei</a>. Many academic buildings, including Rush Rhees Library, are connected by a series of tunnels,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> which are used extensively, especially during unfavorable weather. Most academic buildings and common areas, as well as residence halls, have <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authenticated</a> <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> internet access.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The <b><a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_Medical_Center" title="University of Rochester Medical Center">University of Rochester Medical Center</a></b> (<b>URMC</b>) is the primary campus for the university's medical education and research as well as the main patient care facility. The Medical Center is located adjacent to the River Campus and is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Strong_Memorial_Hospital" title="Strong Memorial Hospital">Strong Memorial Hospital</a>, the School of Medicine and Dentistry building and the Arthur Kornberg Medical Research Building. URMC also houses the School of Nursing and a variety of research centers, including the Wilmot Cancer Center, the Aab Institute of Biomedical Sciences, and the Clinical and Translational Sciences Institute.</p> 
     <p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Eastman_School_of_Music" title="Eastman School of Music">Eastman School of Music</a></b> is situated on its own campus in downtown Rochester, which includes a residence for students, classroom and performance facilities, and the <a href="/wiki/Eastman_Theatre" title="Eastman Theatre">Eastman Theatre</a>, a 3,094-seat concert hall which also serves as the primary venue of the <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Rochester Philharmonic Orchestra">Rochester Philharmonic Orchestra</a>. The campus also features the <a href="/wiki/Sibley_Music_Library" title="Sibley Music Library">Sibley Music Library</a>, which is the largest academic music library in North America, as well as the largest privately owned collection of <a href="/wiki/Sheet_music" title="Sheet music">sheet music</a>. Students are housed at 100 Gibbs Street, a dormitory building constructed in 1991.</p> 
     <p>The <b>South Campus</b> is located in <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_Monroe_County,_New_York" title="Brighton, Monroe County, New York">Brighton</a>, immediately south of Rochester proper. The campus includes the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Laser_Energetics" title="Laboratory for Laser Energetics">Laboratory for Laser Energetics</a>, the Center for Optics Manufacturing, the Center for Optoelectronics and Imaging, and the now defunct Nuclear Structure Research Laboratory (NSRL). Graduate student housing is also provided at the Whipple Park complex.</p>

     <p>University of Rochester's <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect">undergraduate</a> enrollment consists of about 4,500 full-time and about 100 part-time students from across the U.S. and over 90 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Graduate enrollment comprises about 3,300 full-time and about 550 part-time <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student" title="Graduate student" class="mw-redirect">graduate students</a>. The university has more than 103,000 living alumni and employs nearly 2,300 tenure-track faculty, with more than 20,000 faculty and staff across the university and the Strong Health System.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> UR's faculty include fellows of all four <a href="/wiki/National_Academies" title="National Academies" class="mw-redirect">National Academies</a> of the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellows" title="Guggenheim Fellows" class="mw-redirect">Guggenheim Fellows</a>, and recipients of many other awards and recognitions.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The unique, defining feature of the undergraduate program at the University is the Rochester Curriculum. There are no required subjects outside of a prerequisite writing course, which may be petitioned out of. The Curriculum requires that undergraduates study in all of three areas of knowledge: <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences" class="mw-redirect">social sciences</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" title="Natural sciences" class="mw-redirect">natural sciences</a>. Aside from the area of study which a student's major falls in, undergraduates must complete 3-4 courses of related materials (called &quot;clusters&quot;) within each of the remaining areas. For example, a student with a concentration in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a natural science field, must complete at least one cluster in the humanities and one in social sciences. A second concentration or a <a href="/wiki/Academic_minor" title="Academic minor" class="mw-redirect">minor</a> also satisfies this requirement. The only exceptions are students concentrating in an <a href="/wiki/ABET" title="ABET">accredited</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> field (<a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical and computer engineering</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>), who are only required to have one cluster in either humanities or social sciences.</p> 
     <p>The University of Rochester is known for its several unique 5th year programs. Students may apply for the <b>Take Five Scholars</b> program, a tuition free 5th year of study in a field of interest unrelated to their undergraduate major. The university also offers a <b>Kauffman Entrepreneurial Year</b> (<b>KEY</b>) Scholarship, a tuition-free 5th year of study offered to those with an innovative and unique entrepreneurial project impacting the campus or Rochester community. Both programs are competitive and students may apply during their junior or senior year.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Various other scholar programs exist for UR students, including the highly selective <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_Medical_Center" title="University of Rochester Medical Center">Rochester Early Medical Scholars</a> (<b>REMS</b>) and Rochester Early Business Scholar (<b>REBS</b>) programs. Students must apply for these prior to entering the university.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>UR was one of the 25 New Ivies in the 2007 Kaplan/Newsweek &quot;How to Get into College Guide.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-tws03_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws03-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The list names institutions whose caliber of students is considered to rival traditional <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> schools.<sup id="cite_ref-tws03_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws03-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The rankings are based on admissions statistics as well as interviews with administrators, students, faculty, and alumni.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Admission into UR has become increasingly competitive, with an average acceptance rate of 33% (20% for transfer students) and average SAT scores falling in the 95th percentile, for the fall of 2011. <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i>, in their popular National University Rankings, placed the University of Rochester 33rd in the nation for their 2013 ranking.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> rated the University of Rochester 48th among the best universities in the world and 21st among American educational institutions. The University of Rochester also placed 21st on The Washington Monthly College Rankings list. University of Rochester places within the top 10 for the staff-to-student ratio.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University has also been ranked as 34th worldwide according to <a href="/wiki/Global_University_Ranking" title="Global University Ranking" class="mw-redirect">Global University Ranking</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> and 21st in the United States among research universities.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Additionally its graduate programs are consistently ranked within the top 20 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Eastman_School_of_Music" title="Eastman School of Music">Eastman School of Music</a> ranks first among graduate music programs in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Other schools in the university also rank highly, with the School of Medicine and Dentistry at 30th overall among medical schools and its primary-care program ranked 15th among primary-care medical schools, and the Simon School ranked 23rd among graduate business schools. <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> also ranked the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hajim_School_of_Engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hajim School of Engineering (page does not exist)">Hajim School of Engineering</a>'s graduate program 38th nationally. Additionally, the graduate programs of political science, economics, and medical research were ranked 15th, 22nd, and 31st in the nation, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Internationally</b>, in 2012 <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> ranked the University of Rochester 81st in the world.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Rochester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>UR is a leading private university and a major center for diverse fields of research. The university has eight <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> winners among its faculty and alumni.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> UR consistently ranks among the top 40 colleges and universities nationwide in federally financed <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical" title="Medical" class="mw-redirect">medical</a>, and other research,<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with a total research budget of around $350 million spread across many departments and research centers, including the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Laser_Energetics" title="Laboratory for Laser Energetics">Laboratory for Laser Energetics</a>, a laser-based <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> facility, and the extensive research facilities at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_Medical_Center" title="University of Rochester Medical Center">University of Rochester Medical Center</a>. Recently, the university has also engaged in a series of new initiatives to expand its programs in <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, including the construction of the new $37 million Robert B. Goergen Hall for Biomedical Engineering and Optics on the River Campus.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Other new research initiatives include a <a href="/wiki/Cancer_stem_cell" title="Cancer stem cell">cancer stem cell</a> program and a Clinical and Translational Sciences Institute.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> UR also has the ninth highest <a href="/wiki/Patent_portfolio" title="Patent portfolio">technology revenue</a> among U.S. higher education institutions, with $30 million being paid for commercial rights to university technology and research in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Notable patents include Zoloft and Gardasil.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> WeBWorK, a web-based system for checking homework and providing immediate feedback for students, was developed by University of Rochester professors Gage and Pizer. The system is now in use at over 100 universities and colleges.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

     <p>UR's official symbol is the seal of the university, which features a book, representing <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts" class="mw-redirect">arts and sciences</a>, a lyre symbolizing <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, and a modified <a href="/wiki/Rod_of_Asclepius" title="Rod of Asclepius">symbol of medicine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The official flower of the university is the <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" title="Dandelion" class="mw-redirect">dandelion</a>, purportedly prolific on the cow pasture that became the university's second campus.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The official mascot of the university is a predatory wasp found throughout Rochester, the <a href="/wiki/Yellowjacket" title="Yellowjacket" class="mw-redirect">Yellowjacket</a>. From 1983 to 2008, the yellowjacket mascot was named &quot;URBee.&quot; However, when the university re-designed the mascot during the 2007-2008 academic year, a new name was chosen and as of February 1, 2008, the school's mascot is now known as &quot;Rocky&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university uses Dandelion Yellow and Rochester Blue as its official colors, which are the prominent colors on the official regalia.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> of UR is <a href="/wiki/Meliora" title="Meliora">Meliora</a>, which loosely translates to &quot;better&quot; with the connotation of &quot;ever better,&quot; the meaning adopted by the university.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The image of <a href="/wiki/Rush_Rhees_Library" title="Rush Rhees Library">Rush Rhees Library</a>'s main dome serves as an additional icon for the University of Rochester.</p> 
     <p>UR also has official logos for the university as a whole as well as individual units, including the College of Arts, Sciences, and Engineering, the University of Rochester Medical Center, and the Eastman School of Music. President Seligman, as part of his efforts to improve UR's external appearance, commissioned Bill Murphy, the Vice President of Communications, to start an initiative to develop a new graphic identity, including a new logo, in hopes of improving uniformity and overall usage of official standards.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> During March 2007, the communications office was soliciting opinions and comments on finalist designs for the new logo, which was unveiled later that fall.</p> 
     <p>The song most often sung at college events, led often by the school's many <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> groups, is <i>The Genesee</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Although less frequently used, the university also has an official <a href="/wiki/Alma_Mater" title="Alma Mater" class="mw-redirect">Alma Mater</a>, <i>The Dandelion Yellow</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_organizations">Student organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Rochester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Student organizations">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The student body at the University of Rochester is both ethnically and socioeconomically diverse. There are over 200 active <b>Students' Association</b> recognized groups on campus, which range from cultural dance groups to U of R's comedy improv troupe <a href="/wiki/In_Between_The_Lines_Comedy_Improv_Troupe" title="In Between The Lines Comedy Improv Troupe">In Between the Lines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IBTLcappella_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBTLcappella-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 1893, the University has regularly printed its student newspaper, the <i>Campus Times</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CampusTimes_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CampusTimes-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Several a cappella groups play a prominent role in campus life. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_YellowJackets" title="University of Rochester YellowJackets">YellowJackets</a><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> competed on <a href="/wiki/The_Sing-Off_(season_3)" title="The Sing-Off (season 3)">Season 3 of NBC's &quot;The Sing-Off&quot;</a> during the fall 2011 season, finishing 7th nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Midnight_Ramblers" title="Midnight Ramblers">The Midnight Ramblers</a> are the centerpiece of the university's new admissions video, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NiCbfSXoftI">Remember oUR Name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rreview_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rreview-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> The campus also hosts a volunteer EMT squad (The University of Rochester MERT) which provides free New York State certified Basic Life Support services to the River Campus community. The University of Rochester is also home to its own radio station, <a href="/wiki/WRUR-FM" title="WRUR-FM">WRUR</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residences">Residences</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Rochester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Residences">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The majority of undergraduate students at the university live and take classes on the River Campus. Underclassmen are generally required to live on campus while upperclassmen have the option to live off campus. Some graduate housing is provided by the university, but a significant number also live off campus. Housing is provided at multiple locations spread across the several campuses.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

Special Interest Housing groups include Tiernan Project (Burton 2), Inter-Class Living Community (ICLC - Crosby 1), Music Interest Floor (MIF - Wilder 9), Computer Interest Floor (CIF - Anderson 3), Anime Interest Floor (AIF - Anderson 7) and Greenspace (Burton 1).</p> 
     <p><b>Eastman School of Music Campus</b> Housing is provided at the <a href="/wiki/Eastman_School_of_Music" title="Eastman School of Music">Eastman School of Music</a> campus at the <b>Eastman Student Living Center</b> at 100 Gibbs Street in downtown Rochester. The new building was opened in 1991 at the northeast corner of Main and Gibbs Streets, replacing the University Avenue dormitories built nearly 70 years earlier. It is a four-story quadrangle and 16-story tower surrounding a landscaped inner courtyard.</p> 
     <p><b>URMC and Mount Hope Campuses</b> Graduate student housing is provided at 4 locations near the URMC and Mount Hope. It should be noted that these facilities also house some select River campus, non-traditional students who have been deemed too old for traditional housing.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>George Washington Goler House</b> (<b>GHS</b>) immediately adjacent to the grounds of the URMC. It is a high rise apartment building with 321 apartments. The building also houses university community members, including faculty and staff.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>University Towne House</b> (<b>UTH</b>) is a group-living style, two story building located on the Mount Hope Campus with 60 studios. The building is adjacent to Mount Hope businesses as well as university offices.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>University Park</b> (<b>UPK</b>) is a complex of two story buildings that include 40 studio, 86 one-bedroom, and 80 two-bedroom unfurnished apartments. UPK is located near the URMC, directly across from Southside off of Kendrick Road. Graduate students and their families are the primary occupants of these apartments, but some non-traditional undergraduate students are housed here who have been deemed too old for traditional undergraduate housing on the River Campus. Students who live here typically take up residence year round.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p><b>South Campus</b> The South Campus has graduate student housing at the <b>Whipple Park</b> (<b>WPK</b>) complex, which features 250 garden apartments and townhouses with ample storage space. WPK also features a park-like setting with large wooded and lawn areas, playgrounds, areas for gardens and low street noise. Some housing is also provided at the <b>River Road</b> complex, which tends to serve as overflow housing for both undergraduate and graduate students.</p>

     <p>The <b>Students' Association</b> (<b>SA</b>) is the primary student governing body and includes most of the student groups at UR. The SA is governed by the SA Senate, President and Vice President, all of whom are elected by the student body. The SA President may choose to appoint an advisory cabinet made up of a group of volunteer students. There is also a judicial branch, composed of the All Campus Judicial Council (ACJC), the members of whom are nominated by an interview committee and approved by the SA Senate. The SA Senate meets weekly and the longest meeting on record lasted longer than 8 hours. The offices of the SA are located in the Wilson Commons student union.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>All student groups are required to have a constitution, elected officers, and approval from senate in order to be recognized by the SA and have access to university funds. These funds are given yearly based on budgets submitted to the Students' Association Appropriation Committee (SAAC)<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> with supplemental funds available through special forms. All funds are derived from the mandatory Student Activities Fee.</p>

     <p>UR's athletics teams are called the <a href="/wiki/Yellowjacket" title="Yellowjacket" class="mw-redirect">Yellowjackets</a>. They participate in the <a href="/wiki/Division_III_(NCAA)" title="Division III (NCAA)">Division III</a> of the <a href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA" class="mw-redirect">NCAA</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/University_Athletic_Association" title="University Athletic Association">University Athletic Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberty_League" title="Liberty League">Liberty League</a>. One exception to this is the men's <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a> team, which is consistently ranked top 5 in <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The University fields men's teams in <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross country</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>. On the women's side, UR sponsors teams in <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse" title="Lacrosse">lacrosse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Softball" title="Softball">softball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>. Popular club sports include the University of Rochester Thestrals (Quidditch) and the Piggies (Ultimate Frisbee). The Men's <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">Rugby</a> team has also enjoyed recent success, with a New York State Conference Championship in 2011. The team is currently ranked 9th in the nation out of 151 Division III teams.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2009 women's soccer coach <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gurnett" title="Terry Gurnett">Terry Gurnett</a> set records with over 400 lifetime wins.<sup id="cite_ref-tws13novq01_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws13novq01-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tws13novq12_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws13novq12-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> In March 2010 the women's basketball team made it to the NCAA's Final Four.</p> 
     <p>There are also numerous club and intramural athletics groups.</p> 
     <p>The main athletics facilities of the university are in the <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Goergen" title="Robert B. Goergen">Robert B. Goergen</a> Athletic Center and Fauver Stadium on the River Campus, with other facilities located in the Spurrier building (River Campus) and the URMC.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The UR campuses have their own University-sponsored system of buses, or shuttles, which provide free transportation from the River Campus to the Medical Center, South Campus, Eastman Campus, and Riverview. There are also lines that run between the River Campus and local shopping and entertainment in <a href="/wiki/Henrietta,_New_York" title="Henrietta, New York">Henrietta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pittsford_(town),_New_York" title="Pittsford (town), New York" class="mw-redirect">Pittsford</a>. On the weekends, a shuttle loops to the Rochester <a href="/wiki/Public_Market" title="Public Market" class="mw-redirect">Public Market</a>, a student favorite. Most of the University-sponsored buses are named using a color system (i.e. Red Line) that indicates their respective route and allows for easy identification. Several bus lines of the <a href="/wiki/Rochester-Genesee_Regional_Transportation_Authority" title="Rochester-Genesee Regional Transportation Authority">Rochester-Genesee Regional Transportation Authority</a> (RTS) also make stops at the University.</p> 
     <p>The university participates in the <a href="/wiki/Zipcar" title="Zipcar">Zipcar</a> program, which allows students to rent cars on an hourly or daily basis.<sup id="cite_ref-tws08janxxx_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws08janxxx-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tws08janyyy_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws08janyyy-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Greater_Rochester_International_Airport" title="Greater Rochester International Airport">Greater Rochester International Airport</a> is a ten-minute drive to the west of the River Campus. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Rochester_(Amtrak_station)" title="Rochester (Amtrak station)">Amtrak train</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_Lines" title="Greyhound Lines">Greyhound bus</a> have stations in downtown Rochester to the north of the campus.</p>

     <p>UR features several traditional events throughout the year with diverse history.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>ArtAwake</b> brings students and community members together for a day of art and music.</li> 
      <li>The <b>Boar's Head Dinner</b> began in 1934 and continues as an annual event.</li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Convocation" title="Convocation">Convocation</a></b> celebrates the start of the academic year and provides the opportunity for students, faculty, and staff to come together. The ceremony opens with a processional by faculty and administrators in traditional <a href="/wiki/Regalia" title="Regalia">regalia</a>, features presentation of the Goergen Awards for contributions to undergraduate education, and is accompanied by a picnic, activities fair and performances.</li> 
      <li><b>Dandelion Day</b>, colloquially known as <b>D-Day</b>, is a day late in the spring semester that was established as an annual respite around final exams with extensive celebrations, often accompanied by a carnival and musical guests. Previous years have featured <a href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Mash_Bros&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Super Mash Bros (page does not exist)">Super Mash Bros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reel_Big_Fish" title="Reel Big Fish">Reel Big Fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eve_6" title="Eve 6">Eve 6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Big_Bad_Voodoo_Daddy" title="Big Bad Voodoo Daddy">Big Bad Voodoo Daddy</a>. <a href="/wiki/OK_Go" title="OK Go">OK Go</a> performed in 2010.</li> 
      <li><b>Mela</b> is an annual event held late in the spring semester celebrating South Asian culture and dance. The tradition has been a significant showcase for the University and Rochester Community since 1986.</li> 
      <li><b>Meliora Weekend</b> is celebrated in early October during a weekend around the University anniversary, combining <a href="/wiki/Class_reunion" title="Class reunion">class reunions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homecoming" title="Homecoming">homecoming</a>, family weekend, and a <a href="/wiki/Regatta" title="Regatta">regatta</a>. Past speakers include <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Colbert" title="Stephen Colbert">Stephen Colbert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Powell" title="Colin Powell">Colin Powell</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Secretary of Energy">United States Secretary of Energy</a> and University of Rochester alumnus <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Steven Chu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_Gupta" title="Sanjay Gupta">Sanjay Gupta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>.</li> 
      <li>A <b>Pillow Fight</b> occurs annually on the academic quad at the conclusion of the spring semester .</li> 
      <li><b>Wilson Day</b> is day of community service for all incoming university students which include working on neighborhood picnics, voter registrations, painting, landscaping, meal service, and various other service efforts in the community.</li> 
      <li><b>Winterfest Weekend</b> takes place early in the second semester and helps celebrate the snowy Rochester winters. Highlights of the weekend include comedic performances (previous comedians include <a href="/wiki/Demetri_Martin" title="Demetri Martin">Demetri Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/B.J._Novak" title="B.J. Novak" class="mw-redirect">B.J. Novak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ian_Black" title="Michael Ian Black">Michael Ian Black</a>), sleigh rides and ice skating.</li> 
      <li><b>Yellowjacket Weekend</b> occurs at the beginning of the fall semester and is a celebration of school spirit; it includes a free concert and carnival among other events.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul>
