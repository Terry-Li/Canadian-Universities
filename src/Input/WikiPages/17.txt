     <p>The <b>University of Guelph</b>, also known as <b>U of G</b>, is a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university" class="mw-redirect">research university</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guelph" title="Guelph">Guelph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. It was established in 1964 after the amalgamation of <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Agricultural_College" title="Ontario Agricultural College">Ontario Agricultural College</a>, the Macdonald Institute, and the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Veterinary_College" title="Ontario Veterinary College">Ontario Veterinary College</a>, and has since grown to an institution of more than 23,000 students and academic staff. It currently offers over 94 undergraduate degrees, 48 graduate programs, and 6 associate degrees in many different disciplines.</p> 
     <p>The University of Guelph is consistently ranked as a top comprehensive university in Canada by <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> magazine, and given top marks for student satisfaction among medium-sized universities in Canada by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i>. It has held these rankings with its reputation, innovative research-intensive programs, and lively campus life cited as particular strengths. The University of Guelph has also been ranked 50 among the top 100 universities under 50 years old by Times Higher Education.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Currently, the faculty at the University of Guelph hold 39 <a href="/wiki/Canada_Research_Chair" title="Canada Research Chair">Canada Research Chair</a> positions in the research areas of natural sciences, engineering, health sciences and social sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Recent academic achievements include the first scientific validation of <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">water on Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle_X-ray_spectrometer" title="Alpha particle X-ray spectrometer">Alpha particle X-ray spectrometer</a> (APXS) on board the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rovers" title="Mars Exploration Rovers" class="mw-redirect">Mars Exploration Rovers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/DNA_barcoding" title="DNA barcoding">Barcode of Life</a> project for species identification.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The University of Guelph traces its origins back to when the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_government" title="Ontario government" class="mw-redirect">Ontario government</a> bought 500 acres (200&nbsp;ha) of farmland and opened the Ontario School of Agriculture on May 1, 1874, which was renamed the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Agricultural_College" title="Ontario Agricultural College">Ontario Agricultural College</a> (OAC) in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-UoG_history_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UoG_history-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Its first building was Moreton Lodge, located where Johnston Hall now stands, which included classrooms, residences, a library, and a dining room.</p> 
     <p>The Macdonald Institute was established in 1903 to house women's <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a> programs, nature studies, and some domestic art and science.<sup id="cite_ref-UoG_history_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UoG_history-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cowbird_egg_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowbird_egg-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It was named after its financier, <a href="/wiki/William_Christopher_Macdonald" title="William Christopher Macdonald">Sir William Macdonald</a>, who worked to promote domestic sciences in rural Canada, and founded <a href="/wiki/Macdonald_Campus" title="Macdonald Campus">Macdonald College</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">McGill University College</a>. The Ontario Veterinary College (OVC), originally founded in Toronto in 1862, was moved to Guelph in 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-CAN_Encyclopedia_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAN_Encyclopedia-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>These three adjacent colleges would be amalgamated into the single body of the University of Guelph by the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Ontario" title="Legislative Assembly of Ontario">Ontario Legislature</a> on May 8, 1964. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.uoguelph.ca/secretariat/download/uofgact.pdf">University of Guelph Act</a> also brought about the Board of Governors to oversee administrative operations and financial management, and the Senate to address academic concerns. The non-denominational graduate and undergraduate institution was, and remains known especially for the agricultural and veterinary programs that shaped it.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowbird_egg_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowbird_egg-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Wellington College was established shortly after the University of Guelph Act, and five years later, was split three ways into the College of Arts (COA), which exists in the present day, the College of Physical Science and the College of Social Science. The Macdonald Institute would also be renamed the College of Family and Consumer Studies during the split.<sup id="cite_ref-UoG_history_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UoG_history-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> After this split, the University of Guelph started reorganizing into its present day form, starting from the establishment of the College of Biological Sciences (CBS) in 1971. The College of Physical Science would be married to the OAC's School of Engineering in 1989, creating the College of Physical and Engineering Sciences (CPES). The College of Social Science and the College of Family and Consumer Studies were joined to create the College of Social and Applied Human Sciences (CSAHS) in 1998. Finally, the College of Management and Economics (CME) would be established from the segregation of offered business, management and economic degrees and courses in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-UoG_history_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UoG_history-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>The main university campus spans 1,223 acres (495&nbsp;ha), including the 408 acres (165&nbsp;ha) <a href="/wiki/University_of_Guelph_Arboretum" title="University of Guelph Arboretum">University of Guelph Arboretum</a> and a 30-acre (12&nbsp;ha) research park.</p> 
     <p>The campus mixes old-fashioned brick buildings with mid-century <a href="/wiki/Brutalism" title="Brutalism" class="mw-redirect">Brutalism</a>, as well as more contemporary stone structures. It is well-populated with trees, including those which line the main walkways, many of which are paved with red brick. The campus includes an arboretum.</p> 
     <p>Campus safety is provided by the University of Guelph Campus Police, <i>First Response Team</i> and Fire Prevention officers.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_campuses">Regional campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Regional campuses">edit</a>]</span></h3>

       Ontario Agricultural College (OAC) Campuses
      </dt> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Agricultural_College" title="Ontario Agricultural College">Ontario Agricultural College</a> has a network of campuses and research stations throughout Ontario, which were formerly operated by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs_(Ontario)" title="Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs (Ontario)">Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Rural Affairs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CAN_Encyclopedia_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAN_Encyclopedia-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Courses are offered in English in Guelph, Kemptville and Ridgetown, and in French at Campus d?Alfred. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Campus_d%27Alfred&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Campus d'Alfred (page does not exist)">Campus d'Alfred</a> is located in the eastern Ontario, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Ontario" title="Alfred, Ontario" class="mw-redirect">Alfred, Ontario</a> close to Canada?s capital city, Ottawa. It offers diploma and certificate programs which are all taught in French. The <a href="/wiki/Kemptville_Campus" title="Kemptville Campus" class="mw-redirect">Kemptville Campus</a> of Ontario Agriculture College is located in <a href="/wiki/Kemptville,_Ontario" title="Kemptville, Ontario" class="mw-redirect">Kemptville, Ontario</a>. It has been serving the residents of Eastern Ontario since 1917. The campus and research station is located on over 800 acres (320&nbsp;ha) and features 21st century facilities. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ridgetown_Campus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ridgetown Campus (page does not exist)">Ridgetown Campus</a> is located on over 450 acres (180&nbsp;ha) in <a href="/wiki/Ridgetown,_Ontario" title="Ridgetown, Ontario" class="mw-redirect">Ridgetown, Ontario</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Guelph-Humber">University of Guelph-Humber</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: University of Guelph-Humber">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University of Guelph-Humber is a university-college partnership between the University of Guelph and <a href="/wiki/Humber_College" title="Humber College">Humber College</a>. It is located on Humber's North Campus in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The school offers eight regular four-year academic programs, each of which grant both a university honours degree and college diploma.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Profile_and_programs">Profile and programs</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Profile and programs">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_University_of_Guelph_academic_programs" title="List of University of Guelph academic programs">List of University of Guelph academic programs</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The University of Guelph offers over 90 majors in 13 degree programs and 63 Open Learning/Distance Education Opportunities. The overall average for all students entering Guelph is 82%. Guelph students also have the highest graduation rate among Canadian comprehensive universities (at 89%), 5.8% higher than the national average. As well, University of Guelph has been stated to be the best comprehensive university of Canada by Macleans magazine in 2006 and 2007. The school is noted for receiving the most health related research funding than any other Canadian university without a medical school and for having one of the highest proportions of life science expertise per capita than any other university in North America. This has ultimately led to the current focus of the University in the areas of health, food, environment and community.</p>

     <p>The University of Guelph, along with <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Laurier_University" title="Wilfrid Laurier University">Wilfrid Laurier University</a> is a member of the Tri-University History group, which combines the history departments of the three universities at the graduate level. Graduate students are registered at one of the three universities according to their supervisor, but can take courses at any of the campuses. This allows the group to have more diverse course offerings more efficiently. The University of Guelph specializes in Scottish History, as well as local and rural history.</p>

     <p>Several buildings constructed during the establishment of the OAC still exist as part of the main campus today. These include the President's Residence, Raithby House, and Day Hall. From the turn of the century to the movement of the OVC, many more buildings were added to the campus: MacDonald Hall, Massey Hall, the Bullring, Mills Hall, and Food Science.</p> 
     <p>The War Memorial Hall was erected in 1924 of stone-cut limestone by the Ontario Agriculture College;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> This landmark building on the campus of the University of Guelph, was designed by Harry Reginald Coales (architect) as a lecture hall or theatre to honour students who had enlisted and died in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War" class="mw-redirect">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Two bronze tablets in the Memorial Chapel remembers the alumni who died in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War" class="mw-redirect">First World War</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War" class="mw-redirect">Second World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Johnston Hall, a signature symbol of the university, was constructed in 1931, taking the place of the torn-down Moreton Lodge and becoming the home for the OAC Administration. The Johnston Clock tower overlooks Winegard Walk and is visible from much of the campus. The building also overlooks Johnston Green, a popular location for recreational sporting activities and outdoor concerts.</p> 
     <p>Rozanski Hall is located in the heart of the University of Guelph campus. Equipped with state-of-the-art technology, including electronic white boards, laptop sound, picture and wireless internet and high luminance video/data projectors, Rozanski Hall accommodates over 1,500 students in several lecture halls.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Science Complex opened for the 2007/2008 academic year. It is the largest integrated science teaching and research facility in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> This facility houses 150 faculty and 4500 students, and centralizes physical, biological and computational sciences.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A new and improved building consisted of <a href="/wiki/Pathobiology" title="Pathobiology" class="mw-redirect">Pathobiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_Health" title="Animal Health">Animal Health</a> Laboratory was opened in 2010. Its goal is to strengthen Canada's ability to prevent <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> and solve health issues at the human/animal interface. Supporting the growing role of <a href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian" class="mw-redirect">veterinarians</a> in research and educational initiatives related to public health, this four-storey building includes a lecture theatre, seminar rooms, a teaching lab, and research and laboratory facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Biodiversity Institute of Ontario is the world?s first centre for high-volume <a href="/wiki/DNA_barcoding" title="DNA barcoding">DNA barcoding</a> ? the rapid identification of millions of species. It is anticipated that faculty will enter over 500,000 barcode analyses per year.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The University's School of Engineering is in the midst of an approximately $50 million expansion between 2009 and 2011 in the form of new construction and renovations. This is in response to recently introduced Mechanical, Biomedical and Computer Engineering programs, increased enrolment in existing undergraduate and graduate programs and expanding fields of research especially in areas related to sustainability. Alexander Hall is an environmental teaching and research centre. The Animal Cancer Center is Canada's first institute for comparative cancer investigation. It includes a linear accelerator offering animal radiation treatment available. It is anticipated that discoveries in animal cancer will help study cancer treatment in humans as well.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Originally built in the 1940s and expanded in the 1950s, the current W.F. Mitchell Athletic Centre is to be upgraded and expanded to keep up with university and community needs. 70% of Guelph students currently participate in athletics, recreation or fitness programming, and the demand continues to grow. The full build-out of the proposed plan includes a total of 800,000 square feet (74,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>), 11 fields, site improvements and parking; mostly to be implemented over the next 10 years.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>On June 25, 1988, No. 4 Wireless School Association erected a bronze plaque as a war memorial the Royal Canadian Air Force No. 4 Wireless School, which was located on the campus (1941-1945); the plaque honours the memory of their comrades who died in the armed service of Canada during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_residences">Student residences</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Student residences">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>A large portion of students reside on campus in co-ed residences. Those that do typically live in the East Residence (610 residents), East Townhouses (645), <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Hall_(University_of_Guelph)" title="Johnston Hall (University of Guelph)">Johnston Hall</a> (315), Lambton Hall (400), Lennox/Addington Hall (520), Macdonald Hall (150, female only), Maids Hall (50, also known as Artz Ha&uuml;s), Mills Hall (160), Watson Hall (67, also known as International House), West Residences (110 students living amongst the Family Housing community), and South Residence (1700 residents evenly distributed across Mountain, Prairie and Maritime Halls).</p> 
     <p>The LLC (Living Learning Centre) community is made up of Maids and Watson Halls, as well as two sections of the Dundas area in East known as Eco House and French House sometimes linked together as Freco. The program is conducted such that students who are interested in extracurricular development of their interests peripheral to academic achievement can cohabitate among others with the same goal. Each individual community has Residence Life Staff personnel assigned to facilitate programming and community development centred on their respective focuses.</p> 
     <p>Also on campus are the East Village Townhouses that were opened during the fall of 2001. The townhouses consist of 164 four-, five- and six-bedroom self-contained units. These primarily house upper-year students and international students.</p> 
     <p>West Residences, consisting of the 78 College Avenue and 252 Stone Road (also referred to as Wellington Woods) locations, is home to 110 upper-year students. These students live amongst the Family Housing residents in either two bedroom townhouses, or one to two bedroom apartments (only available at the College Avenue location). West Residences promote diverse programs and includes many opportunities for community involvement.</p> 
     <p>South Residence, the largest residence on campus, is home to 1800 students, as well as over 50 Residence Life Staff members.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.housing.uoguelph.ca/page.cfm?group=subnav&amp;id=9">[3]</a>. South Residence is split into three self-contained Halls with independent fire alarm grids. It was built in 1968 by <a href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian" class="mw-redirect">Australian</a> architect <a href="/wiki/John_Andrews_(architect)" title="John Andrews (architect)">John Andrews</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brutalist_architecture" title="Brutalist architecture">brutalist architect</a> who has designed several Canadian university residences, as well as Toronto's iconic CN Tower.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The persistent rumour that the residence was designed by the same architect as the <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Penitentiary" title="Kingston Penitentiary">Kingston Penitentiary</a> is false. This would be all but impossible, as the historic Canadian prison was constructed over a century before South Residence.</p> 
     <p>With 14 different campus living environments, U of G has one of the largest university housing systems in Canada. The University is planning to construct a new student residence on campus with assistance from a private-sector builder that would provide the capital for the new building.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Library">Library</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Library">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The six-storey McLaughlin Library provides students with more than 400 computers in the library and access to books, periodicals, films, audiovisual and archival materials, government documents and maps. The library provides support for everyone's research needs, from undergraduate essays to specialized graduate-level investigations. The library has more than 1 million visitors annually.</p> 
     <p>The Library has student-centred services from building hours, computer access, individual and group study space, and a main floor lounge serving food and refreshments. The Learning Commons also offers resources and services to help users with research, technology, writing, and learning with locations on the main floor of McLaughlin Library, the Ontario Veterinary College (OVC) Learning Commons and Guelph-Humber Learning Commons.</p> 
     <p>The Tri-University Group of Libraries (TUG) is part of a partnership involving the libraries of the Universities of Guelph, Waterloo and Wilfrid Laurier. Students have access to library resources totaling 7.5 million items through the automated library system. Guelph students, faculty and staff also have access to electronic resources from any location at any time. The Library is a leader in offering electronic resources, including nearly 10,000 e-journals as well as databases, reference resources, and live online help.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Student government on campus is governed officially through the university's Student Organization Policy or &quot;SOP&quot;. This document, created initially in 2005 provides the basis for accrediting student groups on campus. The Student Groups listed as Primary Student Organizations under the policy are:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>The Central Student Association (CSA)</li> 
      <li>The Graduate Students' Association (GSA)</li> 
      <li>The College of Arts Student Union (CA-SU)</li> 
      <li>The College of Biological Science Student Council (CBS-SC)</li> 
      <li>The College of Management &amp; Economics Student Association (CME-SA)</li> 
      <li>The College of Physical &amp; Engineering Science Student Council (CPES-SC)</li> 
      <li>The College of Social &amp; Applied Human Sciences Student Alliance (CSAHS-SA)</li> 
      <li>The Student Federation of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Agricultural_College" title="Ontario Agricultural College">Ontario Agricultural College</a> (SF-OAC)</li> 
      <li>The Central Veterinary Students' Association (CVSA)</li> 
      <li>Interhall Council (IHC)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Each of the above PSOs accredit and thus are held accountable for many of the various clubs and student groups on campus. By and large the CSA accredits the most student groups with approximately 70 accredited today. Generally CSA-accredited groups are special interest groups like CHAT (a multi-lingual group), the Jewish Students' Organization (JSO), the Muslim Students' Association and more, catering to those who wish to begin new interest-specific clubs on campus. The College Governments (CA-SU, CBS-SC, CME-SA, CPES-SC, CSAHS-SA, SF-OAC and the CVSA) accredit academically focused groups while IHC accredits 14 groups (includes the temporary residence Brock House for the 2011/2012 year) as hall councils, one for each residence hall on campus.</p> 
     <p>The University also has a department called Student Life which offers a comprehensive package of programs and services that help students make a successful transition to, through and from university life and study. The curricular and co-curricular initiatives, advising and support activities are set out to serve as vehicles through which students can explore their leadership capacity, make long lasting connections, and optimize opportunities to learn through experiences. As well as develop a sense of civic/community responsibility, and be engaged in the campus and community life of the University.</p> 
     <p>Across campus, members of the Residence Student Government, known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interhall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Interhall Council (page does not exist)">Interhall Council</a>, contribute to the programming in each hall. This group of 62 elected members works with students within their halls and are also responsible for facilitating a hall council for hall members to attend. During the 2011/2012 year, a council was stricken at the West Residence Family Housing units and the temporary Brock House residence, bringing the total number of councils to 14. Interhall Council also acts as a liaison between students and Student Housing Services, University Administration, and other on-campus organizations.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The university is represented in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_University_Athletics" title="Ontario University Athletics">Ontario University Athletics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Guelph_Gryphons" title="Guelph Gryphons">Guelph Gryphons</a>. The University offers 15 <a href="/wiki/Varsity_team" title="Varsity team">varsity</a> sports for men and 15 for women. OUA only sports include <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> (men), <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skating</a> (women), <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nordic_skiing" title="Nordic skiing">Nordic skiing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> (men). Currently 7 out of every 10 Guelph students participate in athletics, recreation or fitness programming.</p> 
     <p>Nationally, the OUA is one of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">CIS</a> conferences, along with <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_University_Sport" title="Atlantic University Sport">Atlantic University Sport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada_West_Universities_Athletic_Association" title="Canada West Universities Athletic Association">Canada West Universities Athletic Association</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Student_Sports_Federation" title="Quebec Student Sports Federation" class="mw-redirect">Quebec Student Sports Federation</a>. CIS sports which UG participates in include <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross country running</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a> (women), <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a> (men), <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> (women), <a href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer" class="mw-redirect">soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Track_%26_field" title="Track &amp; field" class="mw-redirect">track &amp; field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>. The Gryphon's men's football team won its only national championship in 1984. In 2008 the Gryphon's Men's Lacrosse team won the Baggataway Cup at the Canadian national field lacrosse champions with a 14-9 win over McGill University at Ron Joyce Stadium in Hamilton. The Gryphons are particularly well known for their exploits in athletics, having won the men's and women's <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross-country</a> titles consecutively six (2006?present) and seven (2007-present) times respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition to this, the Gryphons won the men's track &amp; field title in 2010, and both the men's and women's titles in 2008, and are seeking a resurgence in the year 2013. In addition their Field Hockey team won the national title in 2011. <sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_traditions">Campus traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Campus traditions">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_Old_Jeremiah">Painting Old Jeremiah</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Painting Old Jeremiah">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p><i>Old Jeremiah</i> is the name of an antique British naval gun that currently rests along Winegard Walk in Branion Plaza, at the heart of the University of Guelph campus. Rumoured to have seen battle during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, Old Jeremiah was last fired in April 1913. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the gun's barrel was plugged and it was brought to campus by students as a sign of remembrance for those lost in battle. It is often referred to simply and affectionately as <b>The Cannon</b>. During the 1970s, Old Jeremiah was briefly relocated to Johnston Green and renamed The Big Johnston.</p> 
     <p>As a result of jovial rivalry between Engineering and Agricultural Science students (&quot;Aggies&quot;), the cannon has enjoyed plenty of movement around the Guelph campus in the past as a result of practical jokes between the two majors. Although it is nearly impossible to nail down the exact previous locations of the cannon, it is rumoured to have travelled all over campus, at one point even perching on top of MacNaughton (a prominent university building containing the Bookstore), and at another even disappearing altogether and showing up a day later on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a> campus. Eventually, fed up with the movement of Old Jeremiah, university officials cemented the cannon in place where it sits today. However, as a final stab at humour, a group of students shifted the still-mobile direction of the cannon's face, and aimed it at the fourth floor of the University Centre, home of the institution's senior administration. Old Jeremiah rests in this position today.</p> 
     <p>Despite its movement, the cannon enjoyed relative tranquility until the 1950s when an aspiring entrepreneurial student came up with the idea of painting a message on the highly visible landmark. The act of &quot;painting the cannon&quot; has since become a campus tradition with students, residences, sports teams, clubs and others braving the early morning hours to paint messages on the cannon, most often about upcoming events but also including birthday announcements, wedding proposals and public insults. The etiquette governing &quot;painting the cannon&quot; is unofficial but well-understood: 1) do not begin painting the cannon until the sun has set, 2) be finished by the time the first students arrive for classes in the morning, and 3) avoid profanity or coarse language. It is well-accepted practice to &quot;guard&quot; the cannon until sunrise so as to avoid another person or group painting over one's message.</p> 
     <p>In the fall of 2011, Master of Fine Arts student and art teacher Dawn Johnston began to strip Old Jeremiah of all the layers of paint it had accumulated since the 1950s as an art project. Calling it &quot;[her] form of sculpture,&quot; Johnston completed the project over a week's time within a wooden enclosure to avoid the watchful eyes of passing students. Some students were upset about the removal, claiming that Johnston was &quot;taking away [their] history,&quot; although the project was done with the approval of university faculty. Upon completion, the enclosure was removed and the bare cannon was revealed, however the tradition of painting Old Jeremiah has since resumed.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pep_Rally">The Pep Rally</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The Pep Rally">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>During the University of Guelph?s Orientation program, which takes place each year at the beginning of Orientation Week, all new students within each residence are taught a dance - often referred to as the Hall Boogie - which is performed to a variety of mixed popular songs. Awards are presented to the Halls which demonstrate the best spirit, creativity, synchronisation and co-ordination. Many of the dances are very impressive, despite being practised in typically an hour or less.</p> 
     <p>A University of Guelph dance move, Winding Your Toy, is almost always incorporated into each boogie, usually to a bass-heavy dance track. A winding motion is made with the rear hand - as if winding a wind-up toy - while the knees are bent in rhythm. The origins of &quot;winding the toy&quot; are not well known, yet it retains notoriety among students and friends of students at the university.</p>

     <p>The Rally is the kick-off to the remainder of Orientation activities. The University of Guelph must apply for a special noise permit for the event as the activity can often be heard for miles.</p> 
     <p>In 2004, &quot;Student Power&quot; was introduced as a low-key alternative event to the Pep Rally for anyone who may not be as inclined to participate in the highly energetic and boisterous Pep Rally.</p> 
     <p>During the renovation the location the Pep Rally is held (The School's football field) in 2012, an event named &quot;Rally for Change&quot; was held in place; in which hundreds of University of Guelph students went out into the local community and did street performances to raise money for Cancer research. The Pep Rally was held on a later date. This event has since also become a tradition with plans for it to also be incorporated in the Orientation program in 2013.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_Royal">College Royal</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Guelph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: College Royal">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>An annual feature of the university is its open house, known as College Royal. For a weekend each March, every part of the campus and its programs is exhibited to the public, from the barns of the Agricultural College to the sugar bush in the arboretum. It is highly popular with visitors of all ages, especially families with children who take advantage of the March break (the usual Ontario school break) to have an outing.</p> 
     <p>The 2006 College Royal was visited by <a href="/wiki/Rick_Mercer" title="Rick Mercer">Rick Mercer</a>, taping a segment for his show, the <i><a href="/wiki/Rick_Mercer_Report" title="Rick Mercer Report">Rick Mercer Report</a></i>.</p>

     <p>The Cannon<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> is an online website co-founded by The Guelph Campus Co-operative and the CSA created for, and funded by, <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect">undergraduate</a> students. Although it has a paid editor, all students are encouraged to submit news articles, announcements for upcoming events, opinion pieces, digital photographs and other content that Guelph students may find interesting or useful. Founded in September 2002, the site has features such as <i>Rate-a-Prof</i>, where students share insight and opinions regarding professors, and a free classifieds section, available as a means of buying and selling used textbooks and course materials. The name of the site is a reference to Old Jeremiah, as the website parallels the use of the cannon as a campus-wide message board.</p>
