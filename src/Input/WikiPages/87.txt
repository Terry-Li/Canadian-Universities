     <p><b>Northeastern University</b> (<b>NU</b>), is a <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular" class="mw-redirect">secular</a>, coeducational <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> university in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. Northeastern offers undergraduate degrees in 96 majors and master's, doctoral, and professional degrees in 158 programs from 9 colleges and schools.<sup id="cite_ref-College_and_Offerings_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_and_Offerings-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Founded in 1898, the university is the home of more than 35 specialized research and education centers.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its main campus is located in the <a href="/wiki/Fenway-Kenmore" title="Fenway-Kenmore" class="mw-redirect">Fenway Cultural District</a> of Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenway_Cultural_District_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenway_Cultural_District-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Northeastern is classified as a RU/H institution (high research activity) by the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Foundation_Classifications_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Foundation_Classifications-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Among Northeastern's past and present faculty are several <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellows" title="Guggenheim Fellows" class="mw-redirect">Guggenheim Fellows</a> and a <a href="/wiki/MacArthur_Foundation" title="MacArthur Foundation" class="mw-redirect">MacArthur Foundation</a> <a href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellows_Program" title="MacArthur Fellows Program">&quot;Genius Award&quot;</a> winner,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> a former Democratic nominee for President of the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> winner.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2012, Northeastern received 44,189 applications for the 2,800 seats of the fall freshman class.<sup id="cite_ref-Record_Applications_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Record_Applications-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> 32% of that year's applicants were accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The upward trend in application continues in 2013 where 47,322 students applied for the 2,800 freshman seats. Northeastern's <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_education" title="Cooperative education">cooperative education</a> program was one of the first of its kind in the world, with many corporate and non-profit co-op partners both in the United States and abroad. Employers have included major newspapers, popular television shows, international law firms, banks, government offices, and corporations and many <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Co-op_Employers_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Co-op_Employers-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Huntington_ymca.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Huntington_ymca.jpg/220px-Huntington_ymca.jpg" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Huntington_ymca.jpg/330px-Huntington_ymca.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Huntington_ymca.jpg/440px-Huntington_ymca.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Huntington_ymca.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> The 
        <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue" title="Huntington Avenue">Huntington Avenue</a> 
        <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a> circa 1920, site of the &quot;Evening Institute for Younger Men&quot;
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Northeastern was established in 1898 as the &quot;Evening Institute for Younger Men&quot; at the <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue" title="Huntington Avenue">Huntington Avenue</a> <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-About_Northeastern_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_Northeastern-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Its first class was held October 3, 1898. The institute catered to the needs of the quickly growing immigrant population in Boston.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Within a few years of its formation, it offered classes in <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>. In 1909 the school began offering day classes and it moved to a new location on Huntington Avenue in 1913. The school was officially organized as a college in 1916 and in 1922 it was renamed &quot;Northeastern University of the Boston Young Men's Christian Association.&quot; In a period of rapid campus expansion, the university purchased the <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue_Baseball_Grounds" title="Huntington Avenue Baseball Grounds" class="mw-redirect">Huntington Avenue Grounds</a> (former <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> ballpark) in 1929, but did not build on the land due to financial constraints during <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Depression" title="The Great Depression" class="mw-redirect">The Great Depression</a>.</p> 
     <p>In 1935, the College of Liberal Arts was added to Northeastern, and the university's name was simplified to &quot;Northeastern University.&quot;<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1937 The Northeastern University Corporation was established, creating a board of trustees made up of 31 members of the NU Corporation and 8 members of the <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a>. In 1948 Northeastern separated itself completely from the YMCA.<sup id="cite_ref-northeastern_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northeastern-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Following World War II, Northeastern began admitting women, and in the boom of post-war college-bound students, Northeastern created a College of Education (1953), University College (now called the College of Professional Studies)<sup id="cite_ref-neu1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu1-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> (1960), College of Pharmacy and College of Nursing (1964). The College of Pharmacy and College of Nursing were subsequently combined into the Bouv&eacute; College of Health Sciences. Northeastern also added the College of Criminal Justice (1967) and College of Computer Science (1982), since renamed the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_University_College_of_Computer_and_Information_Science" title="Northeastern University College of Computer and Information Science">College of Computer and Information Science</a>.</p> 
     <p>Similar to a number of urban universities, Northeastern began as a commuter school with many part-time and evening students, and by the early 1980s had grown to 60,000 enrollees.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 1990s, the university reduced the number of enrolled students in order to become a &quot;smaller, better&quot; university and began building more residence halls on campus. It cut its freshman class size from around 4500 students to 2800 students.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg/220px-Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg/330px-Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg/440px-Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Northeastern_Shillman_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>

     <p>From 1996 to 2006, President Richard Freeland led an institutional change; average SAT scores increased more than 200 points, retention rates rose dramatically, and applications doubled.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> President Freeland oversaw Northeastern's largest expansion ever, opening $485 million in new facilities, including residence halls, academic and research facilities, and athletic centers. The institution also became substantially more selective, leading to a more academically talented student body.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>During the transition, students experienced a re-organization of the co-operative education system to better integrate classroom learning with workplace experience.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The university also switched its full-time undergraduate and graduate programs to a new academic calendar comprising two traditional semesters and two summer &quot;minimesters&quot;, replacing the four-quarter system. This new calendar allowed students to delve more deeply into their academic courses and to experience longer, more substantive co-op placements.</p> 
     <p>Throughout the transformation, President Freeland's oft-repeated goal was to crack the Top 100 of the U.S. News rankings,<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which was accomplished in 2005. With this goal accomplished and the transformation from commuting school to national research university complete, he stepped down from the presidency on August 15, 2006. His successor is Dr. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Aoun" title="Joseph Aoun">Joseph Aoun</a>, formerly a <a href="/wiki/Dean_(education)" title="Dean (education)">dean</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boston_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Since coming into office in the fall of 2006, President Aoun has implemented a decentralized management model, giving the academic deans of the university more control over their own budgets, faculty hiring decisions, and fundraising.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg/220px-NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg/330px-NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg/440px-NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:NEU_Ell_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Northeastern's historic 
        <a href="/wiki/Carl_Stephens_Ell" title="Carl Stephens Ell">Ell Hall</a> on Huntington Avenue
       </div>

     <p>As part of Northeastern's five-year, $75 million Academic Investment Plan,<sup id="cite_ref-neu2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu2-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> the University is concentrating on three areas: undergraduate education, core graduate professional programs, and centers of research excellence. The Plan centers around the addition of 100 tenured and tenure-track professors between 2004 and 2009. This plan was recently expanded to provide for the hiring of an additional 300 tenure and tenure-track faculty in interdisciplinary fields. Aoun has also placed more emphasis on improving town relations by reaching out to leaders of the communities surrounding the university.<sup id="cite_ref-boston3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston3-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, Aoun has created more academic partnerships with other institutions in the Boston area including <a href="/wiki/Tufts" title="Tufts" class="mw-redirect">Tufts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_College" title="Hebrew College">Hebrew College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/School_of_the_Museum_of_Fine_Arts" title="School of the Museum of Fine Arts" class="mw-redirect">School of the Museum of Fine Arts</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Under Aoun's leadership, the university continues to climb in the rankings.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 2013 edition of <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News and World Report's</a> &quot;Best Colleges Guide&quot;, Northeastern increased its ranking to 56th, up 6 spots from 2012.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_campuses">Satellite campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Satellite campuses">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>In addition to Northeastern's main Boston campus, the university operates a number of remote undergraduate locations Massachusetts. The University operates the Kostas Research Institute in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Massachusetts" title="Burlington, Massachusetts">Burlington</a>, a Financial District campus in the Hilton Hotel in downtown Boston, a Dedham Campus in <a href="/wiki/Dedham,_Massachusetts" title="Dedham, Massachusetts">Dedham, Massachusetts</a>, and a Marine Science Center in <a href="/wiki/Nahant,_Massachusetts" title="Nahant, Massachusetts">Nahant, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campus_Map_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campus_Map-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Additionally, the University has launched two full service graduate campuses. The first in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a> opened October 2011 and a second campus in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The University has additional plans to open campuses in <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> (California), <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-CHE1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHE1-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Admissions">Admissions</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Admissions">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Northeastern ranks as the top private American university in the number of applications it receives annually.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Barrons College Guides rates admission to Northeastern University as &quot;highly competitive&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-barrons_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barrons-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_and_World_Report" title="U.S. News and World Report" class="mw-redirect">U.S. News and World Report</a> rates Northeastern as &quot;more selective.&quot; The middle 50% of admitted students for Fall 2011 had weighted GPA's of 3.7 - 4.2, SAT scores of 1890–2140 and ACT scores of 29 - 32.<sup id="cite_ref-Northeastern_Alumni_Admissions_FA10_FAQ_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northeastern_Alumni_Admissions_FA10_FAQ-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A report by The Huntington News, NU's newspaper, claimed that for the 114th entering class, Northeastern University received 42,948 applications from prospective freshmen for the fall term.<sup id="cite_ref-NU_hits_record_for_applicants_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NU_hits_record_for_applicants-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> This represented a 15% surge from last year's record of 38,000 applications. For this particular year, Northeastern University beat Boston University and New York University in terms of &quot;the most applied-to private university in the United States&quot;.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>For the same year, the admissions rate dropped to a record-low 34.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-College_Admissions_Rates_Drop_For_The_Class_Of_2015_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_Admissions_Rates_Drop_For_The_Class_Of_2015-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Approximately 3,000 students enrolled, one-sixth of them international students.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Northeastern offers undergraduate majors in 65 departments. At the graduate level, there are more than 125 programs. Academics at Northeastern is grounded in the integration of classroom studies with <a href="/wiki/Experiential_learning" title="Experiential learning">experiential learning</a> opportunities, including <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_education" title="Cooperative education">cooperative education</a>, student research, <a href="/wiki/Service_learning" title="Service learning" class="mw-redirect">service learning</a>, and global experience. The university's cooperative education program places about 5,000 students annually with more than 2,500 co-op employers in Boston, across the United States, and around the globe. In 2010, <a href="/wiki/College_Prowler" title="College Prowler">College Prowler</a> gave Northeastern an &quot;A-&quot; rating for the quality of classes, professors, and overall academic environment.<sup id="cite_ref-collegeprowler_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegeprowler-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_Program">Honors Program</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honors Program">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University Honors Program offers selected students an enhanced curriculum. Starting with the First Year Reading Project and moving on to participating in a wide range of courses during the undergraduate years, the program gives students a variety of academic choices. The culminating experience is advanced specialty work in a major field through college-specific choices including specialized advanced honors seminars and an independent research project.<sup id="cite_ref-neu5_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu5-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, students in the Honors Program exclusively can live in a Living-Learning Community housed in West Villages C<sup id="cite_ref-neu6_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu6-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> and F. Since Fall 2009, first-year Honors students are housed in the North and West Towers of the newly constructed International Village residence hall.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senior_Capstone">Senior Capstone</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Senior Capstone">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Senior Capstone is an advanced level course related to the student's major. The course requires the student to integrate what they have learned through their academic coursework and their experiential learning experience (co-op, research, study abroad, and service).<sup id="cite_ref-Senior_Capstone_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Senior_Capstone-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-neu7_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu7-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-neu8_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu8-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-neu9_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu9-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The university recently partnered with <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Tufts University School of Medicine">Tufts University School of Medicine</a> to create an early acceptance BA/MD Program.<sup id="cite_ref-neu10_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu10-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Northeastern's campus is just a few blocks from the <a href="/wiki/Longwood_Medical_and_Academic_Area" title="Longwood Medical and Academic Area">Longwood Medical and Academic Area</a> where <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine" title="Harvard School of Dental Medicine">Harvard School of Dental Medicine</a> are located along with their associated world class teaching hospitals.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Study_abroad">Study abroad</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Study abroad">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Northeastern has semester-long study abroad programs with placements in Europe, Asia, Africa, Australia, and South America. Some participating schools include: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, Scotland; <a href="/wiki/Reims_Management_School" title="Reims Management School">Reims Management School</a>, France; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cape_Town" title="University of Cape Town">University of Cape Town</a>, South Africa; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a>, England; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Auckland" title="University of Auckland">University of Auckland</a>, New Zealand; <a href="/wiki/Swinburne_University_of_Technology" title="Swinburne University of Technology">Swinburne University of Technology</a>, Australia; <a href="/wiki/Obirin_University" title="Obirin University" class="mw-redirect">Obirin University</a>, Japan; <a href="/wiki/American_College_of_Thessaloniki" title="American College of Thessaloniki" class="mw-redirect">American College of Thessaloniki</a>, Greece and <a href="/wiki/Pontificia_Universidad_Cat%C3%B3lica_de_Chile" title="Pontificia Universidad Cat&oacute;lica de Chile" class="mw-redirect">Pontificia Universidad Cat&oacute;lica de Chile</a>, Chile and also Antarctica<sup id="cite_ref-neu11_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu11-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Northeastern's International Business program is a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Partnership_of_Business_Schools" title="International Partnership of Business Schools">International Partnership of Business Schools</a>. Through this program International Business students have the opportunity to be awarded a dual-degree from Northeastern as well as from a sister school in Europe, Mexico or Hong Kong.</p> 
     <p>Northeastern also has the notable Dialogues of Civilizations program, which features dozens of one month-long programs (usually taking place in the summer) where a faculty member will lead a group of students in the country of their choice. A sort of &quot;mini&quot; study abroad, each program has an area of focus - for example, the Geneva program focuses on small arms and multilateral negotiations while the South Africa program is based in non-governmental organizations. This program is meant to be a communicative experience and an exchange of ideas and cultures. It is open to all majors and all years, and is the most popular study abroad option at Northeastern.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Since the arrival of President Aoun in 2006, the school has also been emphasizing co-op abroad, in an effort to make the school more global and internationally engaged. There are many programs being offered including social entrepreneurship in the Dominican Republic, Belize, and South Africa.</p> 
     <p>Northeastern also offers a program called NUin for first-year students who choose to spend their first semester studying abroad. In 2012, 500 students enrolled in the NUin program in destinations of England, Ireland, Greece, Australia, and Costa Rica.</p>

     <p>Many of Northeastern's 973 full-time and part-time faculty members have garnered national and international acclaim for their achievements in teaching and research, with particular strength in <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect">interdisciplinary</a> scholarship.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-op.2Finternship_program">Co-op/internship program</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Co-op/internship program">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Northeastern has one of the largest co-op/internship programs in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-northeastern23_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northeastern23-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Started in 1909, NU's co-op program is one of the oldest in the nation. In the co-op program, students alternate periods of academic study with periods of paid professional employment related to their major. Most majors offer a four-year graduation option with fewer co-op placements, but the five-year program is more popular with students. The co-op program typically spring of the second year or fall of the third year (after a more traditional program for the first semesters on campus).</p> 
     <p>Co-op placements range from small dynamic start-up companies to large multinational companies with thousands of employees, including many <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> corporations. The program also places students with government agencies, branches of government, nonprofits, and <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" title="Non-governmental organizations" class="mw-redirect">non-governmental organizations</a>. Northeastern students can be found interning in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>. Student placements usually last six months, and are mostly paid. Students may live in the university residence halls on campus during co-op employment, and the university currently leases housing for students co-oping in New York City and Washington, D.C. and assists elsewhere.</p> 
     <p>Northeastern University is also a partner with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Youth_Fund" title="Boston Youth Fund">Boston Youth Fund</a>, which is run by the Boston Youth council and provides summer job and enrichment placement for the City of Boston.</p>

     <p>The NU mascot is <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_University_Paws" title="Northeastern University Paws" class="mw-redirect">Paws</a>. The school colors are red and black with white trim. The fight song, &quot;All Hail, Northeastern&quot;, was composed by Charles A. Pethybridge, Class of 1932.</p> 
     <p>Some notable athletes have played for Northeastern's sports teams. <a href="/wiki/Dan_Ross_(American_football)" title="Dan Ross (American football)">Dan Ross</a> played football at Northeastern long before setting the Super Bowl record for receptions in a game. <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Lewis" title="Reggie Lewis">Reggie Lewis</a> still holds the men's basketball career scoring record. <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Carlos Pe&ntilde;a">Carlos Pe&ntilde;a</a> was named Major League Baseball's American League Comeback Player of the Year in 2007 and an AL Gold Glove winner in 2008. The U.S. Olympic women's ice hockey teams have included Northeastern alumni <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Looney" title="Shelley Looney">Shelley Looney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chanda_Gunn" title="Chanda Gunn">Chanda Gunn</a>.</p> 
     <p>The baseball team has competed in one <a href="/wiki/College_World_Series" title="College World Series">College World Series</a> and played in the NCAA regionals seven times.<sup id="cite_ref-sportsinfo_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sportsinfo-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In its first year in the CAA, the men's basketball team finished in 6th place (out of 12 teams) and advanced to the semifinals of the conference tournament. The CAA proved to be a competitive conference in the 2006 <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Basketball Tournament</a>, as <a href="/wiki/2005-06_George_Mason_Patriots_men%27s_basketball_team" title="2005-06 George Mason Patriots men's basketball team" class="mw-redirect">George Mason University</a> advanced all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Final_Four" title="Final Four" class="mw-redirect">Final Four</a>. In 2007, its second year in the CAA, the women's track team captured the conference championship, while the volleyball team finished second. The women's basketball team won 10 more games in 2008 than the previous year, the biggest one-year turnaround in the CAA, and advanced to the tournament quarterfinals.</p> 
     <p>Northeastern's men's and women's hockey teams compete in the <a href="/wiki/Hockey_East" title="Hockey East">Hockey East</a> Conference. During the 2007–08 season, the men's team ranked as high at #7 in the country and held the top spot in the conference before finishing the season in sixth place in Hockey East. Both teams also participate in the annual <a href="/wiki/Beanpot_(Ice_Hockey)" title="Beanpot (Ice Hockey)" class="mw-redirect">Beanpot</a> tournament between the four major Boston-area colleges. Northeastern's men's team has won the annual event 4 times in its 54-year history, while the women's team has captured the Beanpot 14 times. During the 2008–2009 season, the men's team ranked as high as 3rd in the nation and held the top spot in Hockey East until the last weekend of the season; the team made the NCAA tournament for the first time since 1994, the Beanpot championship game for the first time since 2004, and goalie Brad Thiessen made the Hobey Hat Trick, only the second Northeastern player to do so.</p> 
     <p>The Northeastern Crew team consistently ranks as one of the top 10 teams in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-gonu_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gonu-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 2008 National Championship, the team made the Grand Finals and placed fourth behind <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, while beating <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gonu24_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gonu24-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Northeastern offers 40 club sports, including <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse" title="Lacrosse">lacrosse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_frisbee" title="Ultimate frisbee" class="mw-redirect">ultimate frisbee</a>. In 2005 the women's rugby team finished third in the nation in Division II, while in the same year the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_University_Rugby_Club" title="Northeastern University Rugby Club">men's rugby team</a> won the largest <a href="/wiki/Beast_of_the_East" title="Beast of the East">annual tournament</a> in the United States. The men's lacrosse team began the 2008 season ranked in the Top 10 nationally. The men's and women's squash team finished the 2008 season ranked in the Top 20 nationally. In the 2008–2009 academic year the Northeastern Club Field Hockey and Women's Basketball teams won their respective National Championships. From 2007 to 2009, the Northeastern Club Baseball team won three straight New England Club Baseball Association championships.<sup id="cite_ref-championships_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-championships-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> On May 25, 2010 the club baseball team defeated Penn State to win the National Club Baseball Association Division II World Series and national championship.<sup id="cite_ref-NCBA_Division_II_World_Series_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCBA_Division_II_World_Series-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Citing sparse attendance, numerous losing seasons and the expense to renovate Parsons Field to an acceptable standard, the university Board of Trustees voted on November 20, 2009, to end the <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> program. According to president Joseph Aoun, &quot;Leadership requires that we make these choices. This decision allows us to focus on our existing athletic programs.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-Northeastern_cuts_74-year-old_football_program_-_ESPN_Boston_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northeastern_cuts_74-year-old_football_program_-_ESPN_Boston-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Northeastern is located in Boston's <a href="/wiki/Fenway-Kenmore" title="Fenway-Kenmore" class="mw-redirect">Fenway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Back_Bay" title="Back Bay" class="mw-redirect">Back Bay</a> neighborhoods adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue" title="Huntington Avenue">Huntington Avenue</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_Hall,_Boston" title="Symphony Hall, Boston">Symphony Hall</a>.      <p>Although located in the heart of Boston, the NU campus is still filled with trees, flowers, and grassy quads. Since the late 1990s, Northeastern has been considered a model of design for urban universities and has twice won the &quot;most beautiful new or renovated exterior space&quot; award (presented by the American Institute of Architects) in 2001 and 2004.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matthews_Arena">Matthews Arena</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Matthews Arena">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Matthews_Arena" title="Matthews Arena">Matthews Arena</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Opened in 1910 and widely known as the Boston Arena, Matthews Arena is the world's oldest surviving indoor ice hockey arena.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Located on the east edge of Northeastern University's campus, it is home to the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Huskies" title="Northeastern Huskies">Northeastern Huskies</a> men's and women's hockey teams, and men's basketball team as well as the <a href="/wiki/Wentworth_Institute_of_Technology" title="Wentworth Institute of Technology">Wentworth Institute of Technology</a>'s men's hockey team. The arena is named after former Chair of the Board of Trustees George J. Matthews and his wife, the late Hope M. Matthews. The arena is the original home of the NHL <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a> and the WHA New England Whalers (now the NHL <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a>). It was also the secondary home to the NBA <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Boston Celtics</a> in the 1940s. It has hosted all or part of the <a href="/wiki/America_East_Conference" title="America East Conference">America East Conference</a> men's basketball tournament a total of seven times and hosted the 1960 <a href="/wiki/Frozen_Four" title="Frozen Four" class="mw-redirect">Frozen Four</a>. The arena also served as the original home to the annual <a href="/wiki/Beanpot_(ice_hockey)" title="Beanpot (ice hockey)">Beanpot</a> tournament between Boston's four major college hockey programs.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marino_Recreation_Center">Marino Recreation Center</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Marino Recreation Center">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Marino Center features on its first floor an atrium with two caf&eacute;s and a food market. The second floor includes a student exercise area, a multipurpose room is used for aerobics classes and martial arts clubs. The gymnasium consists of three basketball courts. On the third floor, a state-of-the-art resistance training area and a fully equipped free weight room. A three-lane suspended track is available for either walking or jogging, and rowing ergometers are available.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Library_facilities">Library facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Library facilities">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The NU Libraries include the Snell Library, the John D. O'Bryant African-American Institute Library, and the library at the NU Marine Science Center in <a href="/wiki/Nahant,_Massachusetts" title="Nahant, Massachusetts">Nahant, Massachusetts</a>. The NU School of Law Library is separately administered by the NU School of Law.</p> 
     <p>Snell Library opened in 1990 at a cost of $35 million and contains 1.3 million volumes. The Digital Media Design Studio within the library is a collaborative and interdisciplinary learning environment for creating course-related multimedia presentations, projects and portfolios. The library is home to the Favat Collection, a current collection of children's literature and K-12 curriculum resources, instructional materials, and related information to support courses offered by the School of Education. It contains three computer labs operated by NU Information Services. Two are available to all NU students, faculty, and staff; the other is a teaching lab. .</p> 
     <p>The NU Libraries received federal depository designation in 1962. As a selective depository, the Libraries receive forty-five percent of the federal publication series available to depository libraries.</p> 
     <p>The Snell Library is also home to the Northeastern University Archives and Special Collections department, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_LaGuer" title="Benjamin LaGuer">Benjamin LaGuer</a> papers collection. The Special Collections focus on records of Boston-area community-based organizations that are concerned with social justice issues.<sup id="cite_ref-neu25_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu25-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Snell Library is also open 24 hours a day, allowing students to study at any given time.<sup id="cite_ref-neu26_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu26-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spiritual_Life_Center_and_Sacred_Space">Spiritual Life Center and Sacred Space</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Spiritual Life Center and Sacred Space">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Within the urban environment that characterizes the campus as a whole, NU has carved out a quiet, peaceful space in the centrally located Ell Building for the Spiritual Life Center's Sacred Space. The nondenominational Sacred Space, the Center's main assembly hall, can be configured with carpets, mats or chairs. It has a distinctive ceiling consisting of 3 hanging domes made of overlapping aluminum tiles with an origami-like effect, warm wood floors and accents, and glass-panelled walls that lean outward slightly, their shape and material giving a sense of openness and volume to the space. Faucets for ablution are available in a flanking antechamber, and the Center also contains a smaller meeting space and library.<sup id="cite_ref-Sacred_Space--Practices_and_Potentials_.28The_Pluralism_Project.29_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sacred_Space--Practices_and_Potentials_.28The_Pluralism_Project.29-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> The Sacred Space opened in 1998.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dodge_Hall">Dodge Hall</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Dodge Hall">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Dodge Hall is mainly used for Northeastern's business programs (Before Snell Library opened in 1990, it served as the university's main library). Dodge Hall has five floors. The basement houses a computer lab and is connected to the university's large network of underground tunnels which connects many buildings.</p> 
     <p>Classrooms and a lounge area occupy the first floor. The D'Amore-McKin School of Business undergraduate office is on the second floor, and the graduate<sup id="cite_ref-neu28_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neu28-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> office is on the third floor. The School of Professional Accounting office is on the fourth floor.</p> 
     <p>Directly behind Dodge Hall is the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Young_Men%27s_Christian_Association" title="Boston Young Men's Christian Association">YMCA</a> where Northeastern was founded. There is currently a 17-story high-rise residence hall being built behind the YMCA, scheduled for completion by the Fall 2014 semester.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In order to reduce the campus’ environmental impact, Northeastern has formed a Sustainability Committee that meets several times a year to create proposals to the President's Office.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainability_.40_Northeastern-_Sustainability_Committee_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainability_.40_Northeastern-_Sustainability_Committee-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The 22-story residence Hall (International Village) will be the campus’ second LEED certified building, with the renovation of Dockser Hall the first building on campus to achieve LEED certification.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainability_.40_Northeastern-_Initiatives_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainability_.40_Northeastern-_Initiatives-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> The Clean Plates initiative, which encourages students to separate their leftover food in dining halls, has allowed dining services to compost seven to eight tons per week. Consequently, initiatives, the University was awarded an overall grade of &quot;B&quot; on the Sustainable Endowments Institute's College Sustainability Report Card 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Endowments_Institute_Report_Card_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Endowments_Institute_Report_Card-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, in 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Review" title="Princeton Review" class="mw-redirect">Princeton Review</a> rated Northeastern as one of the top 15 &quot;Green Colleges&quot; in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-greenworld365_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenworld365-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011, the GreenMetric World University ranking put Northeastern as the second greenest university in the world, and first in the US.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_safety">Public safety</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Public safety">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University Police Department is a full-service law enforcement agency with full powers of arrest on university property or property used by Northeastern students and faculty. The campus is adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Police_Department" title="Boston Police Department">Boston Police Department</a>'s Headquarters. A 2008 Reader's Digest survey ranked NU as the second safest school in the United States after Johns Hopkins University in Maryland.<sup id="cite_ref-Reader.27s_Digest_College_Safety_Survey_Results_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader.27s_Digest_College_Safety_Survey_Results-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-www_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transportation">Public transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Public transportation">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Northeastern is bracketed by the <a href="/wiki/MBTA" title="MBTA" class="mw-redirect">MBTA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Orange_Line_(MBTA)" title="Orange Line (MBTA)">Orange Line</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_%22E%22_Branch" title="Green Line &quot;E&quot; Branch">Green Line &quot;E&quot; Branch</a>. Six stations serve the campus: <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Avenue_(MBTA_Orange_Line_station)" title="Massachusetts Avenue (MBTA Orange Line station)">Massachusetts Avenue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruggles_(MBTA_station)" title="Ruggles (MBTA station)">Ruggles</a> on the Orange Line; and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_(MBTA_station)" title="Symphony (MBTA station)">Symphony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_(MBTA_station)" title="Northeastern (MBTA station)" class="mw-redirect">Northeastern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts_(MBTA_station)" title="Museum of Fine Arts (MBTA station)">Museum of Fine Arts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Longwood_Medical_Area_(MBTA_station)" title="Longwood Medical Area (MBTA station)">Longwood Medical Area</a> on the Green Line. The Green Line is paralleled by the <a href="/wiki/39_(MBTA_bus)" title="39 (MBTA bus)" class="mw-redirect">#39 bus</a>. Ruggles also serves several of the southside lines of the <a href="/wiki/MBTA_Commuter_Rail" title="MBTA Commuter Rail">MBTA Commuter Rail</a> system.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_development_background">Campus development background</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northeastern_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Campus development background">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Northeastern's campus is mostly located along Huntington Avenue in an area known as the &quot;<a href="/wiki/Fenway-Kenmore" title="Fenway-Kenmore" class="mw-redirect">Fenway Cultural District</a>&quot; which is part of Boston's <a href="/wiki/Fenway" title="Fenway" class="mw-redirect">Fenway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Back_Bay" title="Back Bay" class="mw-redirect">Back Bay</a> neighborhoods. Other notable institutions in the district include: the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Stewart_Gardner_Museum" title="Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum">Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symphony_Hall,_Boston" title="Symphony Hall, Boston">Symphony Hall</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntington_Theater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Huntington Theater (page does not exist)">Huntington Theater</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_England_Conservatory" title="New England Conservatory">New England Conservatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Conservatory" title="Boston Conservatory">Boston Conservatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berklee_College_of_Music" title="Berklee College of Music">Berklee College of Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Science_Center" title="Christian Science Center" class="mw-redirect">Christian Science Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Baker_Eddy_Library" title="Mary Baker Eddy Library">Mary Baker Eddy Library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Public_Health" title="Harvard School of Public Health">Harvard School of Public Health</a>.</p> 
     <p>Northeastern's campus is something of an urban oddity; despite its location in central Boston, Northeastern is home to a remarkable amount of green open space and quads.<sup id="cite_ref-northeastern29_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northeastern29-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A site master planning competition awarded a contract to revive and rejuvenate the campus; the process was started in 1988 with the creation of the new Northeastern Quad and Mt Ryder. A small oval of land centrally located at the campus main entrance was refurbished by the donations of the graduating class of 1989.</p> 
     <p>What was once a concrete square, outside of the library and student center, was transformed with brick pavers and granite curb stones, in a scalloped design that would eliminate all square corners, a concept developed by the outgoing class of 1989 in a &quot;Northeastern News&quot; poll and suggestion to the President Box that was presented to the board of Trustees in March 1988. The &quot;No Corners&quot; campaign kicked off with a fund raiser at the Ell Student Center on Parents weekend in October 1988. The later selection of a nationally recognized green space landscape architect<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>in 1990 started a renewal plan that continues today. Since the late 1990s Northeastern has twice won the &quot;most beautiful new or renovated exterior space&quot; award presented by the American Institute of Architects in 2001 and again in 2004. In 2008, West Village Building F was recognized in American Institute of Architects New England 2008 Merit Awards for Design Excellence.<sup id="cite_ref-Design_Awards_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Design_Awards-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2003, Northeastern was awarded the prestigious gold medal by the Massachusetts Horticultural Society for its Dedham Campus.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
