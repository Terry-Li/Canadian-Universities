     <p><b>North Carolina State University at Raleigh</b><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> (<b>NCSU</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coeducation" title="Coeducation" class="mw-redirect">coeducational</a>, research university located in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, United States.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Commonly known as <b>N.C. State</b> or simply <b>State</b>, the university is part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> system and is a <a href="/wiki/Land-grant_university" title="Land-grant university">land</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sea_grant_colleges" title="Sea grant colleges" class="mw-redirect">sea</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Space_grant_colleges" title="Space grant colleges" class="mw-redirect">space grant</a> institution.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The university forms one of the corners of the <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Research Triangle</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill" class="mw-redirect">The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a>.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_General_Assembly" title="North Carolina General Assembly">North Carolina General Assembly</a> founded the North Carolina College of Agriculture and Mechanic Arts, now NC State, on March 7, 1887, as a <a href="/wiki/Land-grant_university" title="Land-grant university">land-grant college</a>. Today, NC State has an enrollment of more than 34,000 students, making it the largest university in The Carolinas. NC State has historical strengths in engineering, agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a>, textiles and design and now offers 106 <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degrees</a>. The graduate school offers 104 <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degrees" title="Master's degrees" class="mw-redirect">master's</a> degrees, 61 <a href="/wiki/PhDs" title="PhDs" class="mw-redirect">doctoral</a> degrees, and a Doctor of Veterinary Medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_North_Carolina_State_University" title="History of North Carolina State University">History of North Carolina State University</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_General_Assembly" title="North Carolina General Assembly">North Carolina General Assembly</a> founded NC State on March 7, 1887 as a <a href="/wiki/Land-grant_university" title="Land-grant university">land-grant college</a> under the name &quot;North Carolina College of Agriculture and Mechanic Arts.&quot; As a land-grant college, NC State would provide a liberal and practical education while focusing on military tactics, agriculture and the mechanical arts without excluding <a href="/wiki/Classical_education_movement" title="Classical education movement">classical studies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BriefHistory_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BriefHistory-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Since its founding, the university has maintained these objectives while building on them.<sup id="cite_ref-libraries_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libraries-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG/220px-NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG/330px-NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG/440px-NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:NCSU-Freshmen_1889.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> First freshman class at North Carolina College of Agriculture and Mechanic Arts in 1889.

     <p>After opening in 1889, NC State saw its enrollment fluctuate and its mandate expand. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the North Carolina state government, under <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_North_Carolina" title="Governor of North Carolina">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/O._Max_Gardner" title="O. Max Gardner" class="mw-redirect">O. Max Gardner</a>, administratively combined the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Greensboro" title="University of North Carolina at Greensboro">Woman's College (at Greensboro)</a>, and NC State. This conglomeration became the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Following World War II, the university grew and developed. The G.I. Bill brought thousands of ex-servicemen to campus, and enrollment shot past the 5,000 mark in 1947. State College created new academic programs, including the School of Architecture and Landscape Design in 1947 (renamed as the School of Design in 1948), the School of Education in 1948, and the School of Forestry in 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-BriefHistory_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BriefHistory-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In the summer of 1956, North Carolina State College enrolled its first African-American undergraduates, Ed Carson, Manuel Crockett, Irwin Holmes, and Walter Holmes.<sup id="cite_ref-libraries_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libraries-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg/220px-Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg" width="220" height="64" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg/330px-Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg/440px-Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Raleigh_AM_College_1909_View_LOC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> Panoramic photo of campus taken around 1909

     <p>In 1962, State College officials desired to change the institution’s name to North Carolina State University. Consolidated University administrators approved a change to the University of North Carolina at Raleigh, frustrating many student and alumni who protested the change with letter writing campaigns. In 1963, State College officially became the University of North Carolina at Raleigh. Students, faculty, and alumni continued to express dissatisfaction with this name, however, and after two additional years of protest, the name was once again changed, this time to the current North Carolina State University at Raleigh.<sup id="cite_ref-BriefHistory_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BriefHistory-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The &quot;at Raleigh&quot; part is almost never used, but is still part of the school's official name.</p> 
     <p>In 1966, single year enrollment reached 10,000.<sup id="cite_ref-libraries_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libraries-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The 1970s saw enrollment surpass 19,000 and the addition of the School of Humanities and Social Sciences. NC State celebrated its centennial in 1987 and reorganized its internal structure renaming all its schools to colleges (e.g. School of Engineering to the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_College_of_Engineering" title="North Carolina State University College of Engineering">College of Engineering</a>). Also in this year, it gained 700 acres (2.8&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) of land that would later become <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Campus_of_North_Carolina_State_University" title="Centennial Campus of North Carolina State University">Centennial Campus</a>. During the next decade and a half and continuing today, NC State has focused on developing its new Centennial Campus. Over $620 million has been invested in facilities and infrastructure at the new campus with 62 acres (0.3&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) of space being constructed. There are also 61 private and government agency partners located on Centennial Campus.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Currently, NC State has almost 8,000 employees, nearly 35,000 students, a $1.01&nbsp;billion annual budget, and a $535&nbsp;million endowment. It is the largest university in the state and one of the anchors of North Carolina's <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Research Triangle</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-planninganalysis_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planninganalysis-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nacubo_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nacubo-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>NCSU Libraries Special Collections Research Center, located in <a href="/wiki/D.H._Hill_Library" title="D.H. Hill Library" class="mw-redirect">D.H. Hill Library</a>, maintains a website devoted to NC State history entitled <i>Historical State</i>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campuses">Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campuses">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_Campus">Main Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>NC State's Main Campus has three sub-campuses: North Campus, Central Campus, and South Campus. North Campus is the oldest part of NC State and is home to most academic departments and a few residence halls. Central Campus is primarily composed of residence halls, cafeterias, gymnasiums and student support facilities. Finally, Greek Court, the McKimmon Conference and Training Center, and student park-and-ride areas are found on South Campus. North and Central Campus are separated by the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Railroad" title="North Carolina Railroad">North Carolina Railroad</a>. Pedestrian tunnels allow students to commute between campuses. Central and South Campuses are separated by Western Boulevard, a major downtown artery. University Housing divides Main Campus into West, Central, and East Campus for residence hall purposes. West and Central campuses are divided by Dan Allen Drive, while Central and East are divided by Morill Drive and <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_Coliseum" title="Reynolds Coliseum">Reynolds Coliseum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Architecturally, Main Campus is known for its distinctive red brick buildings. Because of oversupply, odd brick statues dot the landscape, the <a href="/wiki/The_Brickyard_(NC_State)" title="The Brickyard (NC State)">University Plaza</a>, colloquially named &quot;The Brickyard&quot;, in Central Campus is nicknamed for its paving material, and most sidewalks are also made from brick. The Brickyard and sidewalks also sporadically contain white brick mosaics and other patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_Memorial_Bell_Tower" title="North Carolina State University Memorial Bell Tower" class="mw-redirect">Memorial Tower</a>, located in the Northeast corner of North Campus, serves as the signature of NC State and appears in the NC State Chancellor's Seal. It was constructed as a monument to alumni killed in World War I. The granite tower, completed in 1937, is 115&nbsp;feet (35 m) tall. As a tradition, the Belltower is lit in red at night immediately following athletic victories and certain academic achievements.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Court_of_North_Carolina" title="Court of North Carolina" class="mw-redirect">Court of North Carolina</a>, just West of the Memorial Tower, is surrounded by the 1911 Building; the College of Humanities and Social Sciences in Tompkins, Caldwell, Winston Halls and The College of Education in Poe Hall; Page Hall, home to College of Engineering offices; and Leazar Hall, former location of the Computer Science Teaching Labs. It was once home to 100 trees (one for every <a href="/wiki/County_(US)" title="County (US)" class="mw-redirect">county</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>), but damage caused by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fran" title="Hurricane Fran">Hurricane Fran</a> in 1996 reduced the number significantly, including the destruction of a particularly old and large tree which was some 12 feet (3.7&nbsp;m) in diameter. Some replanting has occurred, but the Court's former appearance is far from being restored.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Southwest of the Court of North Carolina is another landmark, the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_Free_Expression_Tunnel" title="North Carolina State University Free Expression Tunnel" class="mw-redirect">Free Expression Tunnel</a>. The Tunnel functions as one of three pedestrian tunnels underneath the railroad tracks separating North Campus and Central Campus. This particular tunnel is the site of sanctioned <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a>; anyone may paint there, and it is often the place for political statements, personal messages, and art.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2008 racist and threatening graffiti directed at (then) President-elect <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> prompted an investigation from the United States <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a>. Students held a &quot;Unity Rally&quot; in response to denounce the acts of racism.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Centennial">Centennial</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Centennial">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Campus_of_North_Carolina_State_University" title="Centennial Campus of North Carolina State University">Centennial Campus of North Carolina State University</a>
     </div> 
     <p>NC State's main campus is augmented by the 1,334-acre (5.4&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) mixed-use Centennial Campus. Located 1&nbsp;mi (1.6&nbsp;km) south of the Memorial Tower, this campus houses university, corporate, and government research, in addition to classrooms and non-student residences. The College of Textiles has been based on this campus since 1991. Beginning in 2002, the College of Engineering began to relocate to Centennial Campus. Currently, six of the nine College of Engineering departments are housed in three Engineering Buildings in an area known as the Engineering Oval. The <a href="/wiki/Golden_LEAF_Biomanufacturing_Training_and_Education_Center" title="Golden LEAF Biomanufacturing Training and Education Center">Golden LEAF Biomanufacturing Training and Education Center</a> (BTEC), part of the College of Engineering, is located adjacent to the Engineering Oval. The offices of <a href="/wiki/Red_Hat" title="Red Hat">Red Hat</a>, <a href="/wiki/ABB_Group" title="ABB Group">ABB</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> are also on the Centennial Campus, as well as <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Campus_Magnet_Middle_School_(Raleigh,_NC)" title="Centennial Campus Magnet Middle School (Raleigh, NC)" class="mw-redirect">Centennial Campus Magnet Middle School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Centennial_Biomedical_Campus">Centennial Biomedical Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Centennial Biomedical Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_Centennial_Biomedical_Campus" title="North Carolina State University Centennial Biomedical Campus" class="mw-redirect">North Carolina State University Centennial Biomedical Campus</a> is located 2.5&nbsp;mi (4.0&nbsp;km) west of the NCSU Memorial Tower. <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_College_of_Veterinary_Medicine" title="North Carolina State University College of Veterinary Medicine">North Carolina State University College of Veterinary Medicine</a>, NC State's professional college and North Carolina's only <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary medicine</a> program, serves as the anchor of the Biomedical Campus. The 180-acre (0.73&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) campus consists of 20 buildings including the 100,000-square-foot (9,300&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) CVM Research Building where biomedical investigations in genomic sciences; vaccine development; cancer immuno-therapy; emerging and infectious zoonotic diseases; and diseases of the lung, respiratory track, skin, and digestive system are undertaken. The Center for Comparative Medicine and Translational Research, the Veterinary Teaching Hospital with its clinical research and clinical trials, and the Teaching Animal Unit (TAU), which is a working farm, are also campus elements. The 110,000-square-foot Randall B. Terry, Jr. Companion Animal Veterinary Medical Center, completed in 2011, doubled the size of the veterinary hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/J._C._Raulston_Arboretum" title="J. C. Raulston Arboretum">J. C. Raulston Arboretum</a> is an 8-acre (0.03&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) <a href="/wiki/Arboretum" title="Arboretum">arboretum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> operated by NC State, and located just south of West Campus. It is open daily without charge.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="N.C._Research_Campus">N.C. Research Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: N.C. Research Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>NC State is one of eight universities with a presence at the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Research_Campus" title="North Carolina Research Campus">North Carolina Research Campus</a>, a 340-acre biotechnology hub in <a href="/wiki/Kannapolis" title="Kannapolis" class="mw-redirect">Kannapolis</a>, N.C.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The university operates the <a href="/wiki/Plants_for_Human_Health_Institute" title="Plants for Human Health Institute">Plants for Human Health Institute</a> (PHHI), a research and education organization devoted to research involving food crops, like fruits and vegetables, and the potential health-promoting properties they convey when consumed.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> NC State's <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_College_of_Agriculture_and_Life_Sciences" title="North Carolina State University College of Agriculture and Life Sciences">College of Agriculture and Life Sciences</a> staffs the institute with faculty from the departments of horticultural science; food, bioprocessing and nutrition sciences; plant biology; genetics; and agricultural and resource economics.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>PHHI has both research and <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Extension" title="Cooperative Extension" class="mw-redirect">Cooperative Extension</a> components. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ann_Lila" title="Mary Ann Lila">Dr. Mary Ann Lila</a> is director of the Plants for Human Health Institute.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> As of September 2012, PHHI is home to around 50 faculty and staff at the N.C. Research Campus.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In March 2008, NC State launched the University Sustainability Office to address environmental concerns on campus and includes a Director of Sustainability, a Sustainability Outreach Coordinator, a Sustainability Program Coordinator, and a Sustainability Student intern.<sup id="cite_ref-NCSU_Sustainability:_History_of_Campus_Sustainability_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCSU_Sustainability:_History_of_Campus_Sustainability-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> NC State has established a commitment to reduce its annual energy consumption per square foot by at least 4% over a 10-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-NCSU_Sustainability:_Energy_.26_Climate_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCSU_Sustainability:_Energy_.26_Climate-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, the university has surpassed the Executive Order 156 goal of diverting 40 percent of their solid waste stream from the landfill through a variety of campus reuse and recycling programs.<sup id="cite_ref-NCSU_Sustainability:_Energy_.26_Climate_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCSU_Sustainability:_Energy_.26_Climate-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> NC State incorporates locally grown, organic, and free range foods into dining service meals at several events such as Earth Week’s EarthFest and the annual All Carolinas Meal with foods local to the Carolinas.<sup id="cite_ref-NCSU_Sustainability:_Dining_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCSU_Sustainability:_Dining-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Examinations of the campus’ sustainability practices by the Sustainable Endowments Institute resulted in a “C+” grade for NC State on the College Sustainability Report Card 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Endowments_Institute_Report_Card_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Endowments_Institute_Report_Card-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a>
     </div> 
     <p>NC State is one of 16 campuses that constitute the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> system. Each campus has a high degree of independence, but each submits to the policies of the UNC system Board of Governors. The 32 voting members of the Board of Governors are elected by the North Carolina General Assembly for four-year terms. President <a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Ross" title="Thomas W. Ross">Thomas W. Ross</a> heads the system.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Board of Trustees of NC State has thirteen members and sets all policies for the University. The UNC system Board of Governors elects eight of the trustees and the Governor of North Carolina appoints four. The student body president serves on the Board of Trustees as a voting member. The UNC system also elects the Chancellor of NC State, currently Randy Woodson.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Board of Trustees administers NC State's eleven academic <a href="/wiki/Colleges" title="Colleges" class="mw-redirect">colleges</a>. Each college grants its own degrees with the exception of the First Year College which provides incoming freshmen the opportunity to experience several disciplines before selecting a major. The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_College_of_Agriculture_and_Life_Sciences" title="North Carolina State University College of Agriculture and Life Sciences">College of Agriculture and Life Sciences</a> is the only college to offer <a href="/wiki/Associate%27s_degrees" title="Associate's degrees" class="mw-redirect">associate's degrees</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University_College_of_Veterinary_Medicine" title="North Carolina State University College of Veterinary Medicine">College of Veterinary Medicine</a> does not grant undergraduate degrees. Each college is composed of numerous <a href="/wiki/Academic_department" title="Academic department">departments</a> that focus on a particular discipline or degree program, for example <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" title="Computer Science" class="mw-redirect">Computer Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">Genetics</a> or Accounting. There are a total of 66 departments administered by all eleven NC State colleges.</p> 
     <p>In total, NC State offers nine <a href="/wiki/Associate%27s_degree" title="Associate's degree" class="mw-redirect">associate's degrees</a> in agriculture,<sup id="cite_ref-ncsu1_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu1-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degrees</a> in 102 areas of study, master's degrees in 108 areas and <a href="/wiki/Doctorate_degree" title="Doctorate degree" class="mw-redirect">doctorate degrees</a> in 60 areas. NC State is known for its programs in agriculture, engineering, <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary medicine</a>, and science. The textile and <a href="/wiki/Paper_engineering" title="Paper engineering">paper engineering</a> programs are notable, given the uniqueness of the subject area.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Entrepreneurship_Initiative">The Entrepreneurship Initiative</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Entrepreneurship Initiative">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Entrepreneurship Initiative<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> in 2006. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Albright" title="Andy Albright">Andy Albright</a> was named to the Triad's 2011 Movers &amp; Shakers list by Business Leader magazine<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> (EI) at NC State distinguishes itself by encouraging multidisciplinary participation on every level – faculty, staff, industry and outreach partners, and most importantly, students. With Dr. Tom Miller as its first executive director, the initiative was formed In July 2008 in response to the Chancellor’s call to &quot;develop an educated an entrepreneurial work force.&quot; NC State has long been widely recognized as a strong presence in the <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurial" title="Entrepreneurial" class="mw-redirect">entrepreneurial</a> community of the region. By organizing and energizing its efforts through the Initiative, NC State will provide a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Springboard" title="Springboard">springboard</a> for ideas, projects, and partnerships. By <a href="/wiki/Leveraging" title="Leveraging" class="mw-redirect">leveraging</a> its core strengths in science, engineering, and technology, NC State will develop entrepreneurial solutions to society’s current problems and future needs. Through the Initiative, individual entrepreneurial projects and programs throughout the university unite to provide students with top-tier resources and opportunities.</p> 
     <p>The EI program encourages students to grow the entrepreneurial spirit by encouraging <a href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary" title="Multidisciplinary" class="mw-redirect">multidisciplinary</a> participation with faculty, staff, industry and students, in hopes of building a community within and beyond N.C. State that thrives on a creative, immerse entrepreneurial experience for all.</p> 
     <p>The ELS is designed to showcase outstanding <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> and entrepreneurship among N.C. State <a href="/wiki/Alumni" title="Alumni" class="mw-redirect">alumni</a> and partners. The lecture series is made possible by a grant from the Albright's. The goal of the EI program is to build a culture of innovation and entrepreneurship for N.C. State students through programs and opportunities that help prepare them to be North Carolina's job creators and leaders of tomorrow.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Considered a <i>more selective</i> university, NC State accepts about half of those who apply for undergraduate admission. For the class of 2014, 19,148 applied and 10,372, or 54%, were accepted. For the class of 2015, 19,634 applied and 10,372, or 53%, were accepted. The fall of 2012 saw 20,298 students apply for the class of 2016, the highest pool of applicants in the University's history. Only 10,124, or half, were accepted, an all-time-low; 4,225, or 42 percent, enrolled.<sup id="cite_ref-upa.ncsu.edu_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upa.ncsu.edu-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Members of the class of 2016 had average SAT verbal, math and writing scores of 591, 627 and 572, respectively, for a two-part total (verbal and math) of 1218 (1600-point scale) or a three-part total of 1790 (2400-point scale). The 4,225 students who enrolled had an average high school GPA of 4.37; 40%, or 1,678, ranked in the top 10% of their graduating classes. There were 110 valedictorians and 93 salutatorians in the class.<sup id="cite_ref-upa.ncsu.edu_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upa.ncsu.edu-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Transfer admission is also very competitive. The mean transfer GPA was a 3.30 fall of 2008. In the fall of 2012, 3,747 students applied to the transfer class; 1,503, or 40%, were admitted.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>NC State does not requires undergraduate admission candidates to choose a preferred college of study. After determining that an applicant meets the overall university requirements, the individual college must also agree to accept the student. Because of this process, some colleges have significantly higher admission requirements than others.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Graduate School reviews all postgraduate education applications. For fall 2011, 14,923 prospective students applied to the Graduate School; 4,381 (29.4%) were admitted. Of these, 2,982 (68.0%) enrolled. Total fall 2011 graduate enrollment was 8,416.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarships">Scholarships</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Scholarships">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>There are several notable scholarships of North Carolina State University which include: <b>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ncsu.edu/park_scholarships/">Park Scholarships</a> program</b> – Made possible by generous financial support from the <a href="/wiki/Park_Foundation" title="Park Foundation" class="mw-redirect">Park Foundation</a> of Ithaca, New York, The Park Scholarship Program was established in 1996. Among the most prestigious undergraduate merit scholarships, the Park program pays expenses for 4 years of study at NC State, a one time computer stipend, and costs associated with enrichment activities. Approximately 35 in-state and 15 out-of-state scholarships are awarded each year to incoming freshmen at North Carolina State University. Selection is based upon scholarship, service, leadership, and character. <b>The Centennial Scholarship</b> – A scholarship to the College of Textiles. Introduced by the North Carolina Textile Foundation in 1999 as a part of the College of Textiles' Centennial Celebration, Centennial Scholarships carry the greatest value among all scholarships at the College of Textiles. Furthermore, the Centennial Scholarship is among the highest valued scholarships offered at North Carolina State University. This scholarship covers approximately 80% of expenses to attend NC State, and on-campus housing (or a stipend for an equal amount) and a meal plan (or predetermined allowance), with any remaining funds to be used as a stipend for books and travel expenses (for up to eight semesters). In addition, each Centennial Scholar will have access to a $7,500 enrichment fund that will allow him/her to participate in a variety of leadership, language immersion and international studies programs, attend international textile shows and benefit from other approved textile enrichment opportunities. Selection is based upon demonstrated high academic achievement throughout high school with proven and potential leadership qualities, extracurricular activities and unique life experiences.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> <b>The Caldwell Fellows Program</b> – Created by the NC State Alumni Association to honor the legacy of former Chancellor <a href="/wiki/John_T._Caldwell" title="John T. Caldwell" class="mw-redirect">John T. Caldwell</a>, the Caldwell Fellows Program is a leadership development scholarship program with a strong focus on <a href="/wiki/Servant_leadership" title="Servant leadership">servant leadership</a>. To be eligible, an applicant must be a full-time, first-year student at NC State University with a GPA of 3.25 or greater and meet the program's selection criteria. Caldwell Fellows are selected on the basis of strong academic performance, leadership potential, orientation toward community and service, commitment to personal growth and a demonstrated interest in and commitment to maximizing program opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> Students that go through the program are challenged by Dr. Caldwell's charge to <b>&quot;THINK BIG&quot;</b> in all their pursuits.</p>

     <p>North Carolina State (NC State) teams are known as the Wolfpack. They compete as a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA) <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a> level (Football Bowl Subdivision (FBS) sub-level for football), primarily competing in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a> (ACC) for all sports since the 1953-54 season. Men's sports include baseball, basketball, cheerleading, cross country, football, golf, soccer, swimming &amp; diving, tennis, track &amp; field and wrestling; while women's sports include basketball, cheerleading, cross country, golf, gymnastics, soccer, softball, swimming and diving, tennis, track &amp; field and volleyball.</p> 
     <p>NC State has won eight national championships: two NCAA championships, two <a href="/wiki/AIAW" title="AIAW" class="mw-redirect">AIAW</a> championships, and four titles under other sanctioning bodies. Most NC State fans and athletes recognize the <a href="/wiki/Carolina-NC_State_rivalry" title="Carolina-NC State rivalry" class="mw-redirect">rivalry with the North Carolina Tar Heels</a> as their biggest.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> NC State was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Conference" title="Southern Conference">Southern Conference</a> and is also one of the four teams on <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_Road" title="Tobacco Road">Tobacco Road</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The primary logo for NC State athletics is a red block 'S' with an inscribed 'N' and 'C'. The block S has been in use since 1890 but has seen many alterations through the years. It became the sole logo for all NC State athletic teams in 2000 and was modernized to its current design in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-ncsu2_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu2-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>NC State athletic teams are nicknamed the 'Wolfpack' (most women's teams are also called the &quot;Wolfpack,&quot; except for the women's basketball team who go by the &quot;Wolfpack Women&quot;). The name was adopted in 1922 when a disgruntled fan described the behavior of the student body at athletic events as being &quot;like a wolf pack.&quot; Prior to the adoption of the current nickname, NC State athletic teams went by such names as the Aggies, the Techs, and the Red Terrors. Since the 1960s the Wolfpack has been represented at athletic events by its mascots, Mr. and Ms. Wuf. In print, the 'Strutting Wolf' is used and is known by the name 'Tuffy.'<sup id="cite_ref-ncsu2_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu2-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

Aside from the two stadiums, the property is mainly open space used for event parking. The property borders the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_Fair" title="North Carolina State Fair">North Carolina State Fair</a> to the North and hosts <a href="/wiki/Tailgate_party" title="Tailgate party">tailgating parties</a> before NC State <a href="/wiki/American_Football" title="American Football" class="mw-redirect">football</a> games.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residence_life">Residence life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Residence life">edit</a>]</span></h3>

Many residence halls house villages, such as Honors village in Quad, Global village in Alexander and Carroll halls, Scholars village in Sullivan hall, Impact Leadership Village in Bowen hall, WISE in Lee Hall, Arts village in Turlington hall and First Year Commons in Owen and Tucker hall. The residence hall or residence hall area has an elected council to provide for local event programming and an outlet for student concerns. Collectively, representatives from the from each hall make up the Inter-Residence Council which represents the on-campus residence-life community as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_Government">Student Government</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Student Government">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Founded in 1921, NC State <a href="/wiki/Student_Government" title="Student Government" class="mw-redirect">Student Government</a> is a student-run organization that serves as the official voice of the student body and attempts to better the student experience at the University. Both a governing body and an advocacy group, Student Government is involved in policy-making, adjudication, programming, advocating, community service, and countless other activities. The organization is a clearinghouse for concerns from students and answers from administrators. The organization also is responsible for distributing a portion of the fees it receives to student organizations in a process known as appropriations.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Student Government at NC State is composed of three branches, and headed by four Student Body Officers (SBOs). The Student Body President heads the executive branch. The President executes policy passed by the senate and is a member of the NC State Board of Trustees. The Student Senate is a 64-member body that considers legislative policy on behalf of the students of the University. It is headed by the Student Senate President who is also the Student Body Vice President and assumes the Presidency if that office becomes vacant. The Senate is also responsible for approving the annual Student Government budget. The budget is drafted each year by the Student Body Treasurer who oversees the monetary status of the entirety of Student Government and as such is a member of both the Legislative and Executive branches. The Student Chief Justice heads the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_branch" title="Judicial branch" class="mw-redirect">judicial branch</a> and is the only member of that branch elected by the student body.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_traditions">Student traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Student traditions">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_NC_State">Service NC State</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Service NC State">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Service NC State (SNCS) is sponsored by the Center for Student Leadership, Ethics &amp; Public Service as the definitive service event to welcome new students to NC State's campus, and as a way to impact the community. This event is open to all students, faculty, staff and the Raleigh community. The event role models the university's mission to impact and support its local and global community.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Homecoming_Week">Homecoming Week</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Homecoming Week">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Homecoming week, an event run by the AASAP (Alumni Association Student Ambassador Program), is one of the only homecoming weeks entirely run by students. Usually at the end of October, starts with a Kickoff event and ends with the Pack Howl pep rally and concert. Featured performers have included <a href="/wiki/Lonestar" title="Lonestar">Lonestar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Daughtry" title="Chris Daughtry">Chris Daughtry</a>, and most recently <a href="/wiki/Cartel_(band)" title="Cartel (band)">Cartel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guster" title="Guster">Guster</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Avett_Brothers" title="The Avett Brothers">The Avett Brothers</a>.</p> 
     <p>During the week, events such as Wear Red-Get Fed, a parade down <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_Street" title="Hillsborough Street">Hillsborough Street</a>, and a campus-wide Spirit Competition take place. The week also includes a Leader of the Pack competition honoring NC State's brightest and most passionate female and male student leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shack-A-Thon">Shack-A-Thon</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Shack-A-Thon">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The Shack-A-Thon, an NCSU tradition since 1991, is NC State's <a href="/wiki/Habitat_for_Humanity" title="Habitat for Humanity">Habitat for Humanity</a>'s annual fall fundraiser. Habitat for Humanity at NCSU Campus Chapter partners with many other student organizations to take over the Brickyard by building shacks that the students live, sleep, and study in for an entire week. Each group raises money by collecting from people walking through the brickyard. This money goes toward funding a house through Habitat for Humanity of Wake County.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Krispy_Kreme_Challenge">Krispy Kreme Challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Krispy Kreme Challenge">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>A more recent tradition of NC State is the <a href="/wiki/Krispy_Kreme_Challenge" title="Krispy Kreme Challenge">Krispy Kreme Challenge</a>. In this race, students meet at the University's Memorial Bell Tower, then run to a <a href="/wiki/Krispy_Kreme" title="Krispy Kreme">Krispy Kreme</a> shop 2&nbsp;mi (3.2&nbsp;km) away (increased to 2.5&nbsp;mi (4.0&nbsp;km) in 2012). Each student must eat twelve glazed <a href="/wiki/Doughnuts" title="Doughnuts" class="mw-redirect">doughnuts</a>, then run back to the Bell Tower within one hour. The Challenge was listed as one of the &quot;102 Things You Gotta Do Before You Graduate&quot; by <i>Sports Illustrated</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> Proceeds from the race go to the NC Children's Hospital. In 2011, proceeds reached $100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_media">Student media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Carolina_State_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Student media">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p><i><a href="/wiki/Technician_(newspaper)" title="Technician (newspaper)">Technician</a></i> has been NC State's student-run newspaper since 1920. It employs students throughout the year and reports on campus news, sports, entertainment, and state and national news. <i>Technician</i> is published Monday through Friday when school is in session with a circulation of about 20,000. The paper is funded by advertisement and a student media fee; it is distributed for free at numerous locations on campus and at area merchants.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <i>Nubian Message</i> is NC State's African American student newspaper. The <i>Nubian Message</i> was first published in 1992 following protests from many African American students denouncing the <i>Technician's</i> alleged racial bias.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i>Nubian Message</i> is currently published weekly.</p> 
     <p>NC State's oldest student publication, the <i>Agromeck</i> <a href="/wiki/Yearbook" title="Yearbook">yearbook</a>, celebrated its 100th birthday with the 2002 edition. It acts as a compendium of student life on campus including sporting events, social activities, and day-to-day living. The yearbook serves as a historian of campus. Each year, nearly 1,000 copies are printed and sold.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University has its own student-run radio station, <a href="/wiki/WKNC" title="WKNC" class="mw-redirect">WKNC</a>. The radio station broadcasts at 25,000 watts and reaches all corners of &quot;the Triangle&quot; (Raleigh, Durham and Chapel Hill). In 2010, WKNC was voted &quot;Best College Radio Station of the Triangle&quot; by readers of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Weekly" title="Independent Weekly" class="mw-redirect">Independent Weekly</a>. The station hosts several formats run by student disc jockeys.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> Prior to its designation as WKNC in 1958, the station's call letters were WVWP.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>NC State has 156,297 living alumni with 61% of alumni living in North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-planninganalysis_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planninganalysis-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> There are 100 alumni clubs in North Carolina and another 42 states also have active clubs. In addition to alumni, the university employs 2,040 faculty and 5,843 staff. A number of NC State alumni and faculty have made significant contributions in the fields of government, military, science, academia, business, arts, and athletics, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ncsu3_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu3-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/John_Edwards" title="John Edwards">John Edwards</a>, former U.S. Senator and two time presidential candidate, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hunt" title="Jim Hunt">James B. Hunt Jr.</a>, 4-term <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_North_Carolina" title="Governor of North Carolina">Governor of North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gibbs" title="Robert Gibbs">Robert Gibbs</a>, Former <a href="/wiki/White_House_Press_Secretary" title="White House Press Secretary">White House Press Secretary</a>, are among the most notable alumni with involvement in politics.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup> Combining science and politics, <a href="/wiki/Rajendra_K._Pachauri" title="Rajendra K. Pachauri">Rajendra Kumar Pachauri</a> is the elected chief of <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, an organization that shared the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> with <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abdurrahim_El-Keib" title="Abdurrahim El-Keib">Abdurrahim El-Keib</a> is the interim prime minister of <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup> Alumnus <a href="/wiki/Munir_Ahmad_Khan" title="Munir Ahmad Khan">Munir Ahmad Khan</a> also would gain an international recognition for his work in reactor quantum physics and later guided the scientific research in nuclear weapons for Pakistan's atomic bomb programs.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Several alumni hold or have held top positions at large companies. These include: <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_Inc." title="Caterpillar Inc.">Caterpillar Inc.</a> (<a href="/wiki/James_W._Owens" title="James W. Owens">James W. Owens</a>), <a href="/wiki/Thomasville_Furniture_Industries" title="Thomasville Furniture Industries">Thomasville Furniture Industries</a> (Nancy Webster), <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> (Ed Woolard), <a href="/wiki/SAS_Institute" title="SAS Institute">SAS Institute</a> (<a href="/wiki/James_Goodnight" title="James Goodnight">James Goodnight</a>), <a href="/wiki/Andy_Albright" title="Andy Albright">Andy Albright</a> businessman, entrepreneur and President and CEO of <a href="/wiki/National_Agents_Alliance" title="National Agents Alliance">National Agents Alliance</a>, Citrix Systems (Mark Templeton) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=MurFam_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MurFam Enterprises (page does not exist)">MurFam Enterprises</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wendell_Murphy" title="Wendell Murphy" class="mw-redirect">Wendell Murphy</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ncsu3_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu3-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Over 50 NC State alumni have achieved the rank of Brigadier General or higher in the <a href="/wiki/Military_of_the_United_States" title="Military of the United States" class="mw-redirect">United States Military</a>. The first four star officer at NCSU, General <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_R._Thurman" title="Maxwell R. Thurman">Maxwell R. Thurman</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Vice_Chief_of_Staff_of_the_U.S._Army" title="Vice Chief of Staff of the U.S. Army" class="mw-redirect">Vice Chief of Staff of the U.S. Army</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Shelton" title="Hugh Shelton">Hugh Shelton</a>, now retired, was a former <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> and a four-star general.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup> General <a href="/wiki/Dan_McNeill" title="Dan McNeill" class="mw-redirect">Dan McNeill</a> currently commands the International Security Assistance Force in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup> General <a href="/wiki/William_C._Lee" title="William C. Lee">William C. Lee</a> is often referred to as the &quot;Father of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Airborne_forces" title="Airborne forces">Airborne</a>.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some alumni have become national academic leaders. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Carnesale" title="Albert Carnesale">Albert Carnesale</a> was the chancellor of <a href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA" class="mw-redirect">UCLA</a> from 1997 to 2006 after a 23-year tenure at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_C._Friday" title="William C. Friday">Bill Friday</a> served as president of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_system" title="University of North Carolina system" class="mw-redirect">University of North Carolina system</a> for 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Brantley_Aycock" title="William Brantley Aycock">William Brantley Aycock</a> served as chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a> from 1957 until 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Notable contributors to the Entertainment Industry are associated with NC State. Actor and Comedian <a href="/wiki/Zach_Galifianakis" title="Zach Galifianakis">Zach Galifianakis</a> attended NC State.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup> Country Singer and <a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a> winner, <a href="/wiki/Scotty_McCreery" title="Scotty McCreery">Scotty McCreery</a> currently attends NC State University.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Bitzer" title="Donald Bitzer">Donald Bitzer</a>, the father of <a href="/wiki/Plasma_display" title="Plasma display">Plasma Television</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> winner currently sits on the faculty of NC State.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Tesh" title="John Tesh">John Tesh</a> also studied at NC State, but was expelled his junior year.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Several NC State students later became professional athletes. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cowher" title="Bill Cowher">Bill Cowher</a> coached 15 seasons with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Steelers" title="Pittsburgh Steelers">Steelers</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XL" title="Super Bowl XL">Super Bowl XL</a> championship team and he currently is a studio analyst for <i><a href="/wiki/The_NFL_Today" title="The NFL Today">The NFL Today</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup> Over 130 NC State alumni play or have played in the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>. Graham</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hill" title="Anthony Hill">Anthony Hill</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Texans" title="Houston Texans">Houston Texans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tank_Tyler" title="Tank Tyler">Tank Tyler</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Panthers" title="Carolina Panthers">Carolina Panthers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Tulloch" title="Stephen Tulloch">Stephen Tulloch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Lions" title="Detroit Lions">Detroit Lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Rivers" title="Philip Rivers">Philip Rivers</a> who plays for the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Chargers" title="San Diego Chargers">San Diego Chargers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russell_Wilson" title="Russell Wilson">Russell Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Hauschka" title="Steven Hauschka">Steven Hauschka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J._R._Sweezy" title="J.
