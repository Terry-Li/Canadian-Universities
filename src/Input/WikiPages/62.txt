     <p>The <b>University of Minnesota, Twin Cities</b> (<b>U of M</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public research university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" title="St. Paul, Minnesota" class="mw-redirect">St. Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, United States. It is the oldest and largest campus within the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_system" title="University of Minnesota system">University of Minnesota system</a> and has the <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_university_campuses_by_enrollment" title="List of United States university campuses by enrollment">sixth-largest</a> main campus student body in the United States, with 51,853 students in 2012?2013.<sup id="cite_ref-www.oir.umn.edu_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.oir.umn.edu-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USDeptofEd_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDeptofEd-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It has since grown into the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_United_States_university_campuses_by_enrollment" title="List of largest United States university campuses by enrollment" class="mw-redirect">sixth largest university campus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p> 
     <p><br /> In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a> placed the University of Minnesota at 29th out of more than 1000 world-class universities and international research institutions surveyed.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 2012, the University of Minnesota was ranked 47th out of 400 top research universities in the world by the <i>Times Higher Education</i> world university rankings,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and was ranked ninth among U.S. public research universities and 26th in the world in <i>Newsweek'</i>s most recent &quot;Top 100 Global Universities&quot; report.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It is considered to be one of America's <a href="/wiki/Public_Ivy" title="Public Ivy">Public Ivy</a> universities, which recognizes top public research universities in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-publicivy_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publicivy-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><br /></p> 
     <p>Minnesota's athletic teams are known collectively as the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers">Minnesota Golden Gophers</a> and compete in the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a>, as members of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campuses">Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Campuses">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minneapolis_campus">Minneapolis campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Minneapolis campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The original Minneapolis campus overlooked the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_Falls" title="Saint Anthony Falls">Saint Anthony Falls</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, but it was later moved about a mile downstream to its current location. The original site is now marked by a small park known as Chute Square at the intersection of University and Central Avenues. The school shut down following a financial crisis during the American Civil War, but reopened in 1867 with considerable financial help from Pillsbury. It was upgraded from a <a href="/wiki/University-preparatory_school" title="University-preparatory school">preparatory school</a> to a college in 1869.</p> 
     <p>The campus now has buildings on both river banks. The &quot;<i>East Bank</i>&quot;, the main portion of the campus, covers 307 acres (124&nbsp;ha). The West Bank is home to Carlson School of Management and the performing arts center. St. Paul campus is home to the College of Biological Sciences (CBS), the College of Design (CDes), the College of Food, Agriculture and Natural Resource Sciences (CFANS), and the veterinary program.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Bank">East Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: East Bank">edit</a>]</span></h4>

     <p>To help simplify the size of campus, the University has broken down the <b>East Bank</b> into several areas: <b>the Knoll area</b>, <b>the Mall area</b>, <b>the Health area</b>, <b>the Athletic area</b>, and <b>the Gateway area</b>.</p> 
     <p><b>The Knoll area</b>, the oldest part of the University's current location, is located in the northwestern part of the campus.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Discipline" title="Discipline">disciplines</a> in this area relate to the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>. Burton Hall is home to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_College_of_Education_and_Human_Development" title="University of Minnesota College of Education and Human Development">College of Education and Human Development</a>. Many buildings in this area are well over one hundred years old and it includes a 13-building group comprising the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Old_Campus_Historic_District" title="University of Minnesota Old Campus Historic District">Old Campus Historic District</a> that is on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> One residence hall, Sanford Hall, and one student-apartment complex, Roy Wilkins Hall, are located in this area. The Institute for Advanced Study<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> is located in the Nolte Center.</p> 
     <p><b>Northrop Mall</b> or <b>the Mall area</b>, is arguably the center of the Minneapolis Campus. It was based on a design by <a href="/wiki/Cass_Gilbert" title="Cass Gilbert">Cass Gilbert</a>, although his plans were too extravagant to be fully implemented. Several of the campus's primary buildings surround the Mall area. The <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Auditorium" title="Northrop Auditorium">Cyrus Northrop Memorial Auditorium</a> provides a northern anchor, with <a href="/wiki/Coffman_Memorial_Union" title="Coffman Memorial Union">Coffman Memorial Union</a> (CMU) to the south. Four of the larger buildings on the sides of it are the primary <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> buildings, and Walter Library. The Mall area is home to both the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_College_of_Liberal_Arts" title="University of Minnesota College of Liberal Arts">College of Liberal Arts</a>, which is Minnesota's largest public or private college, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Institute_of_Technology" title="University of Minnesota Institute of Technology" class="mw-redirect">College of Science and Engineering</a>. Behind CMU is another residence hall, Comstock Hall, and another student-apartment complex, Yudof Hall.</p> 
     <p><b>The Health area</b> is to the southeast of the Mall area and focuses on undergraduate buildings for <a href="/wiki/Biological_science" title="Biological science" class="mw-redirect">biological-science</a> students, as well as homes to the College of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">Pharmacy</a>, the School of <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">Nursing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_School_of_Dentistry" title="University of Minnesota School of Dentistry">School of Dentistry</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Medical_School" title="University of Minnesota Medical School">Medical School</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_School_of_Public_Health" title="University of Minnesota School of Public Health">School of Public Health</a>, and Fairview Hospitals and Clinics. This complex of buildings forms what is known as the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Medical_Center" title="University of Minnesota Medical Center">University of Minnesota Medical Center</a>. Part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_College_of_Biological_Sciences" title="University of Minnesota College of Biological Sciences">College of Biological Sciences</a> is housed in this area.</p> 
     <p>Across the street from Fairview Hospital is an area known as the &quot;<a href="/wiki/City_block#Superblock" title="City block">Superblock</a>&quot;. The Superblock is a four-city-block space housing four residence halls (Pioneer, Frontier, Centennial and Territorial Halls). The Superblock is one of the most popular locations for on-campus housing because it has the largest concentration of students living on campus and has a multitude of social activities between the four residence halls.</p> 
     <p><b>The Athletic area</b> is directly north of the Superblock and includes four recreation/athletic facilities: the University Recreation Center, Cooke Hall, the University Fieldhouse, and the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Stadium_(University_of_Minnesota)#University_Aquatic_center" title="Memorial Stadium (University of Minnesota)">University Aquatic Center</a>. These facilities are all connected by tunnels and skyways allowing students to use one locker-room facility. North of this complex is the <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Williams_Arena" title="Williams Arena">Williams Arena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariucci_Arena" title="Mariucci Arena">Mariucci Arena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ridder_Arena" title="Ridder Arena">Ridder Arena</a>, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baseline_Tennis_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Baseline Tennis Center (page does not exist)">Baseline Tennis Center</a>.</p> 
     <p><b>The Gateway area</b>, an easternmost section, is primarily office buildings rather than classrooms and lecture halls. The most prominent building is <a href="/wiki/McNamara_Alumni_Center" title="McNamara Alumni Center">McNamara Alumni Center</a>. The University is also heavily invested in a <a href="/wiki/Biomedical" title="Biomedical" class="mw-redirect">biomedical-research</a> <a href="/wiki/Initiative_(enterprise)" title="Initiative (enterprise)">initiative</a> and is striving to build five more biomedical-research buildings in ten years that will form a biomedical complex directly north of TCF Bank Stadium.</p>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Armory" title="University of Minnesota Armory">Armory</a>, northeast of the Northrop Mall, is built like a <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Sally_port" title="Sally port">sally-port</a> entrance facing Church Street, and a tower originally intended to be the Professor of <a href="/wiki/Military_Science" title="Military Science" class="mw-redirect">Military Science</a>'s residence, until it was found to be too cold. It originally held the athletics department as well as the military-science classes that it now holds.</p> 
     <p>One of the oldest buildings on campus is <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Old_Campus_Historic_District#Pillsbury_Hall.2C_1889" title="University of Minnesota Old Campus Historic District">Pillsbury Hall</a>, designed in the <a href="/wiki/Richardsonian_Romanesque" title="Richardsonian Romanesque">Richardsonian Romanesque</a> style and built using varieties of sandstone available in Minnesota. It has a unique color that is hard to capture in a photograph.</p> 
     <p>In more recent times, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Gehry" title="Frank Gehry">Frank Gehry</a> designed the <a href="/wiki/Weisman_Art_Museum" title="Weisman Art Museum">Frederick R. Weisman Art Museum</a>. It is a typical example of his work with curving metallic structures.</p> 
     <p>Another new building is the addition to the Architecture building designed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Holl" title="Steven Holl">Steven Holl</a> and completed in 2002. It won an <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">American Institute of Architects</a> award for its innovative design. The Architecture building was then renamed Rapson Hall after the local <a href="/wiki/Modernist_architecture" title="Modernist architecture" class="mw-redirect">modernist</a> architect and School of Architecture Dean <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Rapson" title="Ralph Rapson">Ralph Rapson</a>.</p> 
     <p>The University also has historic <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities" class="mw-redirect">fraternities and sororities</a> buildings (a &quot;Greek row&quot;) north of Northrop Mall on University Avenue SE.</p>

     <p>The <b>West Bank</b> covers 53 acres (21&nbsp;ha) .</p> 
     <p>The West Bank Arts Quarter includes:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Rarig Center (Theatre Arts &amp; Dance)</li> 
      <li>The Barbara Barker Center for Dance</li> 
      <li>Ferguson Hall (School of Music)</li> 
      <li>Ted Mann Concert Hall</li> 
      <li>Regis Center for Art</li>

     <p>The Quarter is home to several annual interdisciplinary arts festivals.</p> 
     <p>The Social Sciences are also on the West Bank and include the <a href="/wiki/Carlson_School_of_Management" title="Carlson School of Management">Carlson School of Management</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Law_School" title="University of Minnesota Law School">Law School</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Institute_of_Public_Affairs" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Institute of Public Affairs" class="mw-redirect">Hubert H. Humphrey Institute of Public Affairs</a>.</p> 
     <p>Wilson Library, the largest library in the University system, is also located on the West Bank as is Middlebrook Hall, the largest residence hall on campus. Approximately 900 students reside in the building named in honor of William T. Middlebrook.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Getting_around">Getting around</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Getting around">edit</a>]</span></h4>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Washington_Avenue_Bridge_(Minneapolis)" title="Washington Avenue Bridge (Minneapolis)">Washington Avenue Bridge</a> crossing the Mississippi River provides access between the East and West Banks, either on foot, designated bike lanes, or via free shuttle service. The bridge has two separate <a href="/wiki/Deck_(bridge)" title="Deck (bridge)">decks</a>: the lower deck for vehicles and the upper deck for pedestrians. An unheated enclosed walkway runs the length of the bridge and shelters students from the weather. Walking and riding bicycles are the most common mode of transportation among students. University Police occasionally cite individuals for <a href="/wiki/Jaywalking" title="Jaywalking">jaywalking</a> in areas surrounding the University resulting in fines as high as $250. This is often done at the beginning of a school year or after pedestrians interfere with traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are some pedestrian tunnels to get from building to building during harsh weather. The tunnels are marked with signs reading, &quot;<a href="/wiki/The_Gopher_Way" title="The Gopher Way">The Gopher Way</a>&quot;.</p> 
     <p>The Minneapolis campus is located near <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstates</a> <a href="/wiki/Interstate_94" title="Interstate 94">94</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstate_35" title="Interstate 35">35W</a> and is bordered by the Minneapolis neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/Dinkytown" title="Dinkytown">Dinkytown</a> (on the north), <a href="/wiki/Cedar-Riverside,_Minneapolis" title="Cedar-Riverside, Minneapolis">Cedar-Riverside</a> (on the west), <a href="/wiki/Stadium_Village" title="Stadium Village" class="mw-redirect">Stadium Village</a> (on the southeast), and <a href="/wiki/Prospect_Park,_Minneapolis" title="Prospect Park, Minneapolis">Prospect Park</a> (on the east).</p> 
     <p>Two <a href="/wiki/Light_rail_in_Minnesota" title="Light rail in Minnesota" class="mw-redirect">light-rail stations</a> are under construction to serve the University along the <a href="/wiki/Central_Corridor_(Minnesota)" title="Central Corridor (Minnesota)" class="mw-redirect">Central Corridor</a> light-rail line. Stations are being constructed on the <a href="/wiki/East_Bank_Station" title="East Bank Station" class="mw-redirect">East Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Bank_Station" title="West Bank Station" class="mw-redirect">West Bank</a>.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_safety">Campus safety</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Campus safety">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>Minneapolis was named the safest metropolitan area in the United States by Forbes in October 2009. Despite this distinction, shootings have occurred near and on campus.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The St. Paul campus is in the city of <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heights,_Minnesota" title="Falcon Heights, Minnesota">Falcon Heights</a>. Despite this, all campus buildings have St. Paul street addresses. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_College_of_Food,_Agricultural_and_Natural_Resource_Sciences" title="University of Minnesota College of Food, Agricultural and Natural Resource Sciences">College of Food, Agricultural and Natural Resource Sciences</a>, many other disciplines from social sciences to <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational education</a> are located on this campus. This also includes the College of Continuing Education,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> College of Veterinary Medicine<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and the College of Biological Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The extensive lawns, flowers, trees, wood lots and the surrounding University research farm plots creates a greener and quieter campus. It has a grassy mall of its own and can be a bit of a retreat from the more-urban Minneapolis campus. Prominent on this campus is Bailey Hall, the St. Paul campus's only residence hall. There are campus connectors running every 5 minutes on the weekdays when school is in session, allowing students easy access to both campuses.</p> 
     <p>The Continuing Education and Conference Center,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> which serves over 20,000 conference attendees per year, is also located on the St. Paul campus.</p> 
     <p>The St. Paul campus is home to the College of Design's department of Design, Housing, and Apparel (DHA). Located in McNeal Hall, DHA includes the departmental disciplines of Apparel Design, Graphic Design, Housing Studies, Interior Design, and Retail Merchandising.</p> 
     <p>The St. Paul campus is known to University students and staff for the Dairy Salesroom,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> which sells food (including ice cream) produced in the University's state-certified dairy plant by students, faculty and staff, and the similar Meat Sales Room,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The St. Paul campus borders the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Fair" title="Minnesota State Fair">Minnesota State Fairgrounds</a>. The State Fair is one of the largest in the United States, usually lasting twelve days, from late August through Labor Day in early September. Since classes do not start on either campus until after Labor Day, the Fair uses the campus parking facilities.</p> 
     <p>Although the Falcon Heights <a href="/wiki/North_American_Numbering_Plan" title="North American Numbering Plan">area code</a> is 651, the University telephone system trunk lines use Minneapolis <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">exchanges</a> and its 612 area code.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commuting_between_Minneapolis_and_St._Paul_campuses">Commuting between Minneapolis and St. Paul campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Commuting between Minneapolis and St. Paul campuses">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>During the school year on regular weekdays, the shuttles operate with schedule-less service as often as every five minutes. In 2008, the system carried 3.55 million riders. Even <a href="/wiki/Metro_Transit_(Minnesota)" title="Metro Transit (Minnesota)">Metro Transit</a>'s busy <a href="/wiki/Hiawatha_Line" title="Hiawatha Line" class="mw-redirect">Hiawatha Line</a> light rail required a subsidy of $1.44 that year, and that was with many riders paying $1.75 or more for a ride.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Ten_Committee_on_Institutional_Cooperation">Big Ten Committee on Institutional Cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Big Ten Committee on Institutional Cooperation">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University of Minnesota is a participant in the <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Institutional_Cooperation" title="Committee on Institutional Cooperation">Committee on Institutional Cooperation</a>. The Committee on Institutional Cooperation (CIC) is the academic consortium of the universities in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> plus former conference member, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>. The initiative also allows students at participating institutions to take distance courses at other participating institutions. The initiative also forms a partnership of research. Engaging in $8 billion in research in 2010, CIC universities are providing powerful insight into important issues in medicine, technology, agriculture, and communities.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Students at participating schools are also allowed &quot;in-house&quot; viewing privileges at other participating schools' libraries.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> They also employ collective purchasing, which has saved member institutions $19 million to date.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Print">Print</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Print">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p><i><a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Daily" title="Minnesota Daily">The Minnesota Daily</a></i>, is published in print Monday through Thursday during the normal school season, and only online on Fridays. It is printed once each week during the summer. The <i>Daily</i> is operated by an autonomous organization run entirely by students. It was first published on May 1, 1900. Outside of every day news coverage the paper has also published special issues such as the Grapevine Awards, Ski-U-Mah, the Bar &amp; Beer Guide, Sex-U-Mah, and others.</p> 
     <p>A long-defunct but fondly remembered humor magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/Ski-U-Mah_(magazine)" title="Ski-U-Mah (magazine)">Ski-U-Mah</a></i>, was published from about 1930 to 1950. It launched the career of novelist and scriptwriter <a href="/wiki/Max_Shulman" title="Max Shulman">Max Shulman</a>.</p> 
     <p>A relative newcomer to the University's print-media community is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wake_Student_Magazine" title="The Wake Student Magazine" class="mw-redirect">The Wake Student Magazine</a></i>, a weekly magazine that covers University-related stories and provides a forum for student expression. It was founded in November 2001 in an effort to diversify campus media and achieved student group status in February 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-fund_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fund-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Students from many disciplines do all of the reporting, writing, editing, illustration, photography, layout and business management for the publication. The magazine was founded by James DeLong and Chris Ruen.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The Wake was named the nation's best campus publication (2006) by the Independent Press Association.<sup id="cite_ref-fund_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fund-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Additionally, the <i>Wake</i> publishes <i>Liminal</i>, a literary journal that began in 2005. <i>Liminal</i> was created in the absence of an undergraduate literary journal and continues to bring poetry and prose to the University community.</p> 
     <p><i>The Wake</i> has faced a number of challenges during its existence, due in part to the reliance on student fees funding. In April 2004, the needed $60,000 in funding was restored, which allowed for the magazine's continued existence after the Student Services Fees Committee had initially declined to fund it.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> They faced further challenges in 2005 when their request for additional funding to publish weekly was denied<sup id="cite_ref-dead2_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead2-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and then partially restored.<sup id="cite_ref-dead4_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead4-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2005 conservatives on campus began formulating a new monthly magazine named the <i>Minnesota Republic</i>. The first issue was released in February 2006, and funding by student service fees started in September 2006.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The campus radio station, <a href="/wiki/KUOM" title="KUOM" class="mw-redirect">KUOM</a> &quot;Radio K&quot;, broadcasts an eclectic variety of <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">independent music</a> during the day on 770 <a href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz" class="mw-redirect">kHz</a> <a href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio" class="mw-redirect">AM</a>. Its 5,000-watt signal has a range of 80 miles (130&nbsp;km), but shuts down at dusk because of <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> regulations. In 2003, the station added a low-power (8-watt) signal on 106.5&nbsp;MHz <a href="/wiki/FM_radio" title="FM radio" class="mw-redirect">FM</a> overnight and on weekends. In 2005, a 10-watt translator began broadcasting from Falcon Heights on 100.7 FM at all times. Radio K also <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio" title="Internet radio">streams</a> its content at www.radiok.org. With roots in experimental transmissions that began before World War I, the station received the first AM broadcast license in the state on January 13, 1922, and began broadcasting as <a href="/wiki/Radio_K" title="Radio K">WLB</a>, changing to the KUOM <a href="/wiki/Radio_call_sign" title="Radio call sign" class="mw-redirect">call sign</a> about two decades later. The station had an educational format until 1993 when it merged with a smaller campus-only music station to become what is now known as Radio K. A small group of full-time employees are joined by over 20 part-time student employees who oversee the station. Most of the on-air talent consists of student volunteers.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Some television programs made on campus have been broadcast on local <a href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service" class="mw-redirect">PBS</a> station <a href="/wiki/KTCI" title="KTCI" class="mw-redirect">KTCI</a> channel 17. Several episodes of <i>Great Conversations</i> have been made since 2002, featuring one-on-one discussions between University faculty and experts brought in from around the world. <i>Tech Talk</i> is a show meant to help people who feel intimidated by modern technology, including cellular phones and computers.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer">Computer</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Computer">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University developed <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(protocol)" title="Gopher (protocol)">Gopher</a>, a precursor to the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> which used <a href="/wiki/Hyperlinks" title="Hyperlinks" class="mw-redirect">hyperlinks</a> to connect documents across computers on the internet. However, the version produced by <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> was favored by the public since it was freely distributed and could more easily handle multimedia webpages.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The University also houses the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage_Institute" title="Charles Babbage Institute">Charles Babbage Institute</a>, a research and archive center specializing in computer history.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile">Mobile</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mobile">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>There are multiple ways in which students can connect with the University via their smartphones. Free wireless Internet is abundantly available throughout the campus to enrolled students, and there are applications that can be downloaded that relate to the University and surrounding areas. There are deals offered for university-area restaurants on popular coupon applications such as Groupon, foursquare, and CollegeByMe, which is an application exclusive to the University students offering special deals to local restaurants and businesses.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers">Minnesota Golden Gophers</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The University's intercollegiate sports teams are called the &quot;<a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers">Golden Gophers</a>&quot; and are members of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Collegiate_Hockey_Association" title="Western Collegiate Hockey Association">Western Collegiate Hockey Association</a> in the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA). Beginning with the 2013-2014 season, Minnesota will be leaving the WCHA and along with Michigan, Ohio State, Penn State, Michigan State and Wisconsin will form the new Big Ten Hockey Conference. The current athletic director is <a href="/wiki/Norwood_Teague" title="Norwood Teague">Norwood Teague</a> who was introduced on April 23, 2012 replacing <a href="/wiki/Joel_Maturi" title="Joel Maturi">Joel Maturi</a>.</p> 
     <p>The Golden Gophers' most notable rivalry is the annual college-football game between them and the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Badgers" title="Wisconsin Badgers">Wisconsin Badgers</a> (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin?Madison">University of Wisconsin?Madison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison</a>, Wisconsin) for <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bunyan%27s_Axe" title="Paul Bunyan's Axe">Paul Bunyan's Axe</a>, the longest continuous rivalry in <a href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA" class="mw-redirect">NCAA</a> Division I football. The two universities also compete in the Border Battle, a year-long athletic competition in which each sport season is worth 40 points divided by the number of times the teams play each other (i.e. football is worth 40 points because they play each other only once, while women's ice hockey is worth 10 points per game because they play four times a year). Conference and post-season <a href="/wiki/Playoffs" title="Playoffs">playoffs</a> do not count in the point standings.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Goldy_Gopher" title="Goldy Gopher">Goldy Gopher</a> is the mascot for the Twin Cities campus and the associated sports teams. The <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(animal)" title="Gopher (animal)">gopher</a> mascot is a tradition as old as the state which was tabbed the ?Gopher State? in 1857 after a political cartoon ridiculing the US$5-million railroad loan which helped open up the West. The cartoon portrayed shifty railroad barons as striped gophers pulling a railroad car carrying the Territorial Legislature. Later, the University picked up the nickname with the first University <a href="/wiki/Yearbook" title="Yearbook">yearbook</a> bearing the name &quot;Gopher Annual&quot; appearing in 1887.</p> 
     <p>The &quot;<a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Rouser" title="Minnesota Rouser">Minnesota Rouser</a>&quot; is the University of Minnesota's <a href="/wiki/Fight_song" title="Fight song">fight song</a>. It is commonly played and sung at various events such as <a href="/wiki/Graduation" title="Graduation">commencement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convocation" title="Convocation">convocation</a>, and athletic games. It is among a number of songs associated with the University, including the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_March" title="Minnesota March">Minnesota March</a>, which was composed for the University by <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Sousa" title="John Philip Sousa">John Philip Sousa</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>The Minnesota Golden Gophers are one of the oldest programs in college-football history. They have won 7 <a href="/wiki/National_Championship" title="National Championship" class="mw-redirect">National Championships</a> and 18 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Big_Ten_National_Championships" title="List of Big Ten National Championships" class="mw-redirect">Big Ten Conference Championships</a>. The Golden Gophers played their first game on September 29, 1882, a 4?0 victory over <a href="/wiki/Hamline_University" title="Hamline University">Hamline University</a>, St. Paul. In 1890, the Golden Gophers played host to the Wisconsin Badgers in a 63?0 victory. With the exception of 1906, the Golden Gophers and the Badgers have played each other every year since. The 117 games played against each other is the most-played rivalry in NCAA Division I-A college football .</p> 
     <p>In 1981, the Golden Gophers played their last game in <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Stadium_(University_of_Minnesota)" title="Memorial Stadium (University of Minnesota)">Memorial Stadium</a>. Between 1982 and 2008, the school hosted their home games in the <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Metrodome" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome">Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome</a> in downtown Minneapolis until they moved back to campus on September 12, 2009, when their new home, <a href="/wiki/TCF_Bank_Stadium" title="TCF Bank Stadium">TCF Bank Stadium</a>, opened with a game against the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_Academy#Intercollegiate_athletics" title="United States Air Force Academy">U.S. Air Force Academy</a> Falcons, which Minnesota won 20?13.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basketball">Basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Basketball">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>The Golden Gophers men's basketball team has won two <a href="/wiki/Helms_Athletic_Foundation#NCAA_Basketball_National_Champions" title="Helms Athletic Foundation">National Championships</a>, one <a href="/wiki/National_Invitation_Tournament" title="National Invitation Tournament">National Invitation Tournament</a> (NIT) Championship and eight Big Ten Regular Season Championships. They also have six <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Tournament</a> appearances not including the 1997 appearance in which they reached the <a href="/wiki/Final_Four" title="Final Four" class="mw-redirect">Final Four</a> that was voided because of academic fraud, and three <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">Sweet 16</a> appearances.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gophers" title="Golden Gophers" class="mw-redirect">Golden Gophers</a> women's basketball team has enjoyed success in recent years under <a href="/wiki/Pam_Borton" title="Pam Borton">Pam Borton</a>, including a <a href="/wiki/Final_Four" title="Final Four" class="mw-redirect">Final Four</a> appearance in 2004. Overall, they have six <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Women%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Women's Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Tournament</a> appearances and three <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Women%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Women's Division I Basketball Championship">Sweet 16</a> appearances.</p>

They have won 14 <a href="/wiki/Western_Collegiate_Hockey_Association_men%27s_champions" title="Western Collegiate Hockey Association men's champions">WCHA Tournament Championships</a> and have 20 NCAA <a href="/wiki/Frozen_Four" title="Frozen Four" class="mw-redirect">Frozen Four</a> appearances. A Golden Gophers hockey tradition is to stock the roster almost exclusively (sometimes completely) with Minnesota natives. Home games are played at Mariucci Arena. The Golden Gophers' big rivals are the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin?Madison">University of Wisconsin?Madison</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Dakota" title="University of North Dakota">University of North Dakota</a>.</p> 
     <p>The Golden Gophers women's hockey team has also won five National Championships, most recently in 2013, and five WCHA Regular Season Championships. They have also won four WCHA Tournament Championships and have seven NCAA Frozen Four appearances. They play their home games in <a href="/wiki/Ridder_Arena" title="Ridder Arena">Ridder Arena</a>. They were the first collegiate women's hockey team to play in an arena dedicated solely to women's ice hockey. In the 2012-2013 season they finished undefeated at 41-0, they are the first and only NCAA Women's Hockey team to do so. After winning the NCAA tournament their winning streak stands at 49 games, dating back to February 17, 2012 when they lost to North Dakota.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wrestling">Wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Wrestling">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/College_Board" title="College Board">College Board</a>, as of July 2012 there are 34,812 undergraduates at the University of Minnesota Twin Cities campus. Out of that number, 5,368 are first-time degree seeking freshmen. There are 17,745 graduate students.</p> 
     <p>The racial/ethnic breakdown of the student population is as follows:</p> 
     <p>75% White, 3% Black, 9% Asian, 3% Hispanic/Latino, 3% Two or more races, 1% Other, and 6% Non-resident alien</p> 
     <p>The average age of all students is 21.</p> 
     <p>The gender breakdown of the student population is 52% women and 48% men.</p> 
     <p>67% of matriculants to the university are considered Minnesota residents, and 33% of matriculants are considered out-of-state residents.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_government">Student government</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Student government">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minnesota_Student_Association">Minnesota Student Association</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Minnesota Student Association">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Minnesota Student Association, MSA for short, is the undergraduate student government at the University of Minnesota.<br /> Although it has maintained a consistent history of being a voice for student advocacy, it has more recently turned toward direct efforts of benefiting the student population at the University of Minnesota.</p> 
     <p><b>The MSA Express</b>: A student-operated late night bus service established by Max Page, Monica Heth, and Nathan Olson during the 2006?2007 school year.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Piloted by MSA, the 2007?2008 administration of Emma Olson and Ross Skattum began the process of transitioning the service to the University's Boynton Health Services.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> This was done to ensure its longevity. Student response was overwhelmingly positive,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> and its continued use has caused MSA leaders to consider expanding the service.</p> 
     <p><b>Lend a Hand, Hear The Band</b>: A concert established during the Page-Heth administration, students volunteering 10 hours of community service in the Minneapolis?St. Paul area receive a free ticket to a concert at Northrop Auditorium.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Past artists have included Minnesota natives <a href="/wiki/The_Hold_Steady" title="The Hold Steady">The Hold Steady</a> and Radio On, as well as the nationally-recognized bands <a href="/wiki/Guster" title="Guster">Guster</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Format" title="The Format">The Format</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduate_and_Professional_Student_Assembly">Graduate and Professional Student Assembly</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Minnesota&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Graduate and Professional Student Assembly">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <b>Graduate and Professional Student Assembly</b> (GAPSA) is responsible for graduate and professional student governance at the University of Minnesota. It is the largest and most comprehensive graduate/professional student governance organization in the United States.</p> 
     <p>The University of Minnesota has the second largest number of graduate and professional students in the United States at over 16,000. All registered <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student" title="Graduate student" class="mw-redirect">graduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professional_students" title="Professional students" class="mw-redirect">professional students</a> at the University of Minnesota are members of GAPSA. It was established in 1990 as a non-profit (IRS 501 (c)(3)) confederation of independent college councils representing all graduate and professional students at the University of Minnesota to the Board of Regents, the President of the University, the University Senate, the University at large and wider community.</p> 
     <p>GAPSA serves as a resource for member councils, as the primary contact point for administrative units, as a graduate and professional student policy-making and policy-influencing body, and as a center of intercollegiate and intra-collegiate interaction among students.</p> 
     <p>GAPSA serves students in the <a href="/wiki/Carlson_School_of_Management" title="Carlson School of Management">Carlson School of Management</a>, the Dental School, the Graduate School, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Law_School" title="University of Minnesota Law School">Law School</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Medical_School" title="University of Minnesota Medical School">Medical School</a>, the School of Nursing, the College of Pharmacy, the School of Public Health, the College of Veterinary Medicine, and the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Education_and_Human_Development" title="College of Education and Human Development" class="mw-redirect">College of Education and Human Development</a>. GAPSA is also a member of the National Association of Graduate-Professional Students.</p>
