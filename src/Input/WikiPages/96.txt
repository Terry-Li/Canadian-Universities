     <p>The <b>University of Iowa</b> (also known as <b>UI</b>, or simply <b>Iowa</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> research university located in <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City,_Iowa" title="Iowa City, Iowa">Iowa City, Iowa</a>. It is the oldest public university in the state. The university is organized into eleven colleges granting undergraduate, graduate, and professional degrees; and it is the home of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa_Hospitals_and_Clinics" title="University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics">University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics</a>, one of the largest university-owned teaching hospitals in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Iowa was the first American institution of higher learning to accept creative work for academic credit, and developed the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Fine_Arts" title="Master of Fine Arts">Master of Fine Arts</a> degree.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Writers%E2%80%99_Workshop" title="Iowa Writers’ Workshop" class="mw-redirect">Iowa Writers’ Workshop</a> program is widely regarded as the best-known, most-established writing program in the country.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole sentence (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Iowa's athletic teams compete in <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> and are collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes" title="Iowa Hawkeyes">Hawkeyes</a>. They compete in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg/220px-University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg" width="220" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg/330px-University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg/440px-University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg 2x" /></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:University_of_Iowa_mosaic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>
        </div> University seal mosaic, c. 1908, Iowa Hall.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The University of Iowa was founded on February 25, 1847, as the first public institution of higher learning in Iowa, just 59 days after Iowa was admitted to the Union. The legal name for this university is the <b>State University of Iowa</b>, but this became confused with <a href="/wiki/Iowa_State_University" title="Iowa State University">Iowa State University</a>, and the Board of Regents approved using the &quot;University of Iowa&quot; for everyday usage in October 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The first faculty offered instruction at the university beginning in March 1855 to students in the Old Mechanics Building, located where Seashore Hall is now. In September 1855, there were 124 students, of whom forty-one were women. The 1856–57 catalogue listed nine departments offering <a href="/wiki/Classical_language" title="Classical language">ancient languages</a>, modern languages, intellectual <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moral_philosophy" title="Moral philosophy" class="mw-redirect">moral philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. The first president of the university was <a href="/wiki/Amos_Dean" title="Amos Dean">Amos Dean</a>.</p> 
     <p>The original campus consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Old_Capitol_Building" title="Iowa Old Capitol Building">Iowa Old Capitol Building</a> and the 10 acres (40,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) (4.05 hectares) of land on which it stood. Following the placing of the cornerstone July 4, 1840, the building housed the Fifth Legislative Assembly of the <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Iowa" title="Territory of Iowa" class="mw-redirect">Territory of Iowa</a> (December 5, 1842) and then became the first capitol building of the State of Iowa on December 28, 1846. Until that date, it had been the third capitol of the Territory of Iowa. When the capitol of Iowa was moved to <a href="/wiki/Des_Moines" title="Des Moines" class="mw-redirect">Des Moines</a> in 1857, the Old Capitol became the first permanent &quot;home&quot; of the University.</p>

       <a href="/wiki/File:OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg/220px-OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg/330px-OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg/440px-OldcapitolIowaCity.jpg 2x" /></a>

     <p>In 1855, Iowa became the first public university in the United States to <a href="/wiki/List_of_mixed-sex_colleges_and_universities_in_the_United_States" title="List of mixed-sex colleges and universities in the United States">admit men and women on an equal basis</a>. In addition, this university was the world's first university to accept creative work in theater, writing, music, and art on an equal basis with academic research.</p> 
     <p>The university was one of the first institutions in America to grant a law degree to a woman (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_B._Hickey_Wilkinson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mary B. Hickey Wilkinson (page does not exist)">Mary B. Hickey Wilkinson</a>, 1873), to grant a law degree to an African American (<a href="/w/index.php?title=G._Alexander_Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="G. Alexander Clark (page does not exist)">G. Alexander Clark</a> in 1879), and to put an African American on a varsity athletic squad (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Holbrook" title="Frank Holbrook" class="mw-redirect">Frank Holbrook</a> in 1895). The university offered its first <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> in 1898.</p> 
     <p>The university was the first state university to recognize the Gay, Lesbian, Bisexual, Transgender, and Allied Union (in 1970).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Iowa established the first law school west of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, and it was also the first university to use television in education, in 1932, and it pioneered in the field of <a href="/wiki/Standardized_testing" title="Standardized testing" class="mw-redirect">standardized testing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Also, the University of Iowa was the first Big Ten institution to promote an African American to the position of administrative vice president. (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Phillip_Hubbard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phillip Hubbard (page does not exist)">Phillip Hubbard</a>, promoted in 1966)</p> 
     <p>On November 1, 1991, five employees of the university were killed and one student was critically injured when <a href="/wiki/Gang_Lu" title="Gang Lu" class="mw-redirect">Gang Lu</a>, a former graduate student of physics, went on a shooting rampage before committing suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Officials received letters written by Lu that discussed his grievances and plans; apparently Lu was set off because he felt that his dissertation should have been received more eagerly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_21st_century">The 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The 21st century">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>On April 13, 2006, a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> struck the university and adjacent <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City" title="Iowa City" class="mw-redirect">Iowa City</a>, causing extensive damage throughout the campus and town. The tornado was the most intense and destructive of five tornadoes that touched down in <a href="/wiki/Johnson_County,_Iowa" title="Johnson County, Iowa">Johnson County, Iowa</a> that evening. It was given the rating of F2 on the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_Scale" title="Fujita Scale" class="mw-redirect">Fujita Scale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday2006_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday2006-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> &quot;Damage on the campus was limited to a parking garage for university vehicles and some downed trees.&quot; <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Several houses and businesses in Iowa City suffered extensive damage.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday2006_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday2006-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the wreckage, injuries were relatively light in the area, although one person in a neighboring county was killed.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday2006_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday2006-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Two years later, in the summer of 2008, another natural disaster struck the campus. Flood waters breached the Coralville Reservoir spillway, damaging more than 20 major campus buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Several weeks after the flood waters receded university officials placed a preliminary estimate on flood damage at $231.75 million. Later, the university estimated that repairs would cost about $743 million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Later that year, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> responded to a proposal from the UI Writing University committee by designating Iowa City the world's third <a href="/wiki/City_of_Literature" title="City of Literature">City of Literature</a>, making it part of the UNESCO <a href="/wiki/Creative_Cities_Network" title="Creative Cities Network">Creative Cities Network</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>The University of Iowa's main campus, located in <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City" title="Iowa City" class="mw-redirect">Iowa City</a>, was originally designed by architect D. Elwood Cook. The campus is roughly bordered by Park Road and U.S. Highway 6 to the north and Dubuque and Gilbert Streets to the east. The <a href="/wiki/Iowa_River" title="Iowa River">Iowa River</a> flows through the campus, dividing it into west and east sides.</p> 
     <p>Of architectural note is the <a href="/wiki/Pentacrest" title="Pentacrest">Pentacrest</a> at the center of The University of Iowa campus. The Pentacrest comprises five major campus buildings: <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Old_Capitol_Building" title="Iowa Old Capitol Building">Old Capitol</a>, Schaeffer Hall, MacLean Hall, Macbride Hall, and Jessup Hall. The Old Capitol was once the primary government building for the state of Iowa, but it is now a museum of Iowa history.</p> 
     <p>Also on the eastern side of campus are five residence halls (Burge, Daum, Stanley, Currier, and Mayflower), the Iowa Memorial Union, the Pappajohn Business Building, Seamans Center for the Engineering Arts and Sciences, the Lindquist Center (home of the College of Education), Phillips Hall (the foreign language building), Van Allen Hall (home to physics and astronomy), the English-Philosophy Building, and the buildings for biology, chemistry, geology &amp; environmental sciences, psychology, communications, and journalism. The Main Library can also be found on the east side.</p> 
     <p>The Colleges of Law, Medicine, Nursing, Dentistry, Pharmacy, and <a href="/wiki/Public_Health" title="Public Health" class="mw-redirect">Public Health</a> are on the western side of the Iowa River, along with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa_Hospitals_and_Clinics" title="University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics">University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics</a>, the Theatre Building, and <a href="/wiki/Himie_Voxman" title="Himie Voxman">Voxman</a> Music Building. Additionally, five residence halls (Hillcrest, Slater, Reinow, Quadrangle, and Parklawn), <a href="/wiki/Kinnick_Stadium" title="Kinnick Stadium">Kinnick Stadium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carver-Hawkeye_Arena" title="Carver-Hawkeye Arena" class="mw-redirect">Carver-Hawkeye Arena</a> are located on the west campus.</p>

     <p>The Oakdale Campus, which is home to some of the university's research facilities and the driving simulator, is located north of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_80" title="Interstate 80">Interstate 80</a> in adjacent <a href="/wiki/Coralville,_Iowa" title="Coralville, Iowa">Coralville</a>.</p> 
     <p>The flood of 2008 had a major impact on a number of campus buildings, forcing many buildings to temporarily close. The Iowa Memorial Union was closed for a period of time, and the ground floor of this building is still closed as it undergoes renovation. The arts campus, which includes Hancher, Voxman, Clapp Recital Hall, and the Theatre Building, was hit especially hard. The theatre building has since reopened, but the music facilities have not. Music classes were for a short time held in temporary trailers, and now music classrooms are spread throughout campus. Recently, a University task force suggested to state regents that Hancher be rebuilt near its current site on the West bank of the Iowa River and Voxman and Clapp be built nearer to the main campus on South Clinton Street.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pentacrest">The Pentacrest</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Pentacrest">edit</a>]</span></h3>

      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Pentacrest" title="Pentacrest">Pentacrest</a>

     <p>The Pentacrest is sometimes referred to as the center of academic life at the University, especially for Liberal Arts students. It comprises five buildings: the Old Capitol and the four lecture halls Schaeffer, Macbride, MacLean, and Jessup. A variety of classes are held in these four buildings, mostly relating to the Liberal Arts and Mathematics. Macbride Hall and the Old Capitol also contain museums of natural history and Iowa state history, respectively.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University of Iowa is one of the EPA's Green Power Partners,<sup id="cite_ref-Green_Power_Partners_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Power_Partners-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> burning oat hulls instead of coal and reducing coal consumption by 20%.<sup id="cite_ref-UI_President_Mason_announces_strengthened_sustainability_focus_for_university_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UI_President_Mason_announces_strengthened_sustainability_focus_for_university-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In May 2004 the university joined the Chicago Climate Exchange<sup id="cite_ref-Examples_of_Sustainability_Practices_and_Initiatives_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Examples_of_Sustainability_Practices_and_Initiatives-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and in April 2009 a student garden was opened.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Student_Garden_opens_on_UI_west_campus_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Student_Garden_opens_on_UI_west_campus-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University also offers a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.uiowa.edu/certificate/">Certificate in Sustainability</a> through the Office of Sustainability (OS). The OS recently coordinated the University's first sustainability plan: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.uiowa.edu/assets/Uploads/2020-Vision-UIowa-Sustainability-Targets.pdf">2020 Vision UIowa Sustainability Targets</a> proposed by UI President Sally Mason on Oct. 29, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Iowa is one of 61 elected members to the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_American_Universities" title="Association of American Universities">Association of American Universities</a>. In 2001, Iowa was listed as a <a href="/wiki/Public_Ivy#Greenes.27_Guides" title="Public Ivy">Public Ivy</a> by Greene Guides. The university is currently home to <a href="/wiki/ISCABBS" title="ISCABBS">ISCABBS</a>, an aging public <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_board_system" title="Bulletin board system">bulletin board system</a> that was the largest Internet community in the world prior to the commercialization of the <a href="/wiki/World_wide_web" title="World wide web" class="mw-redirect">world wide web</a>.</p> 
     <p>The University of Iowa is also the home to the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www-nrd.nhtsa.dot.gov/departments/nrd-12/NationalAdvancedDriverSimulator.html">National Advanced Driving Simulator</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> <a href="/wiki/Driving_simulator" title="Driving simulator">driving simulator</a>.) The <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Writers%27_Workshop" title="Iowa Writers' Workshop">Iowa Writers' Workshop</a> was founded in 1936. Since 1947 it has produced thirteen <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> winners. In total, twenty-five people affiliated with the Writers' Workshop have won a Pulitzer Prize. The <b>Hanson Center for Technical Communication</b> was founded at The University of Iowa and is named after a 1960 graduate, Thomas R. Hanson who funded the institution with $800,000.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>For 2012, out of 2,774 4-year institutions in the U.S., University of Iowa was nationally ranked #72 by <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a></i>, #113 by <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Monthly" title="Washington Monthly">Washington Monthly</a></i>, and #269 by <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>. Globally for 2012, the university was ranked #169 by <i><a href="/wiki/Times" title="Times" class="mw-redirect">Times</a></i> and #199 by both <i>QS</i> and <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i>.</p> 
     <p>In departmental rankings for 2012, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> ranked Iowa's Carver College of Medicine #12 in the country for primary care and #29 in the country for research, its College of Law #29, its College of Pharmacy #17, and its School of Art &amp; Art History's Fine Arts #22.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Iowa Institute of Gerontology: The Iowa Institute of Gerontology existed from 1953 until 1971. It was closed in the latter year due to a budget shortfall.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Institute of Agricultural Medicine</b>. The Institute of Agricultural Medicine was established in 1955 with a grant from the <a href="/wiki/W._K._Kellogg_Foundation" title="W. K. Kellogg Foundation">W. K. Kellogg Foundation</a> as an institutte to study rural public health issues.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> It was later renamed to the <b>Institute of Agricultural Medicine and Occupational Health</b>.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>IIHR — Hydroscience &amp; Engineering (Iowa Institute of Hydraulic Research)</b>. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.iihr.uiowa.edu/">IHR—Hydroscience &amp; Engineering</a> is a world-renowned center for education, research, and public service focusing on hydraulic engineering and fluid mechanics. Based in the C. Maxwell Stanley Hydraulics Laboratory, a five-story red brick building on the banks of the Iowa River, IIHR is a unit of the University of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.engineering.uiowa.edu/">Iowa’s College of Engineering</a>. Because of its contributions to water’s study and use, the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a> recognized the Stanley Hydraulics Lab as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Historic_Civil_Engineering_Landmarks" title="List of Historic Civil Engineering Landmarks">National Historic Civil Engineering Landmark</a>. The ASCE distinguishes the lab as the &quot;oldest university-based hydraulics laboratory in the nation continuously focusing on research and education in hydraulic engineering&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Public Policy Center</b>. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://ppc.uiowa.edu">Public Policy Center (PPC</a>) is an interdisciplinary academic research center investigating six policy areas: Environmental, Health, Housing &amp; Land Use, Human Factors &amp; Vehicle Safety, Social Science, and Transportation. The University of Iowa Public Policy Center (PPC) was founded in 1987 by David Forkenbrock, Professor of Urban and Regional Planning, as a freestanding unit in the Office of the Vice President for Research. The PPC's original focus was Transportation Policy research, followed by Health Policy research (1990), and Human Factors and Vehicle Safety research (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Much of the student night-life in Iowa City is centered around the pedestrian mall (&quot;ped mall&quot;), which contains numerous restaurants, local shops/boutiques, and over thirty bars. A popular university event that draws both students and also residents from the entire midwest is home football games. A related activity that many students engage in is tailgating, often beginning early in the morning. The University of Iowa is well known for its party and social scene: it was given the rank of 10th-best party school in the United States by Playboy magazine in 2010, and in 2011 The Princeton Review ranked Iowa in the #4 spot on its annual Top Party Schools survey.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The university has appeared on similar top ten lists of several other publications over the years. In addition, there are hundreds of student organizations, including groups focused on politics, sports, games, lifestyles, dance, song, and theater, and a variety of other activities. The University also tries to sponsor events that give students an alternative to the typical drinking scene.<sup id="cite_ref-latenight_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latenight-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2004 the University established an annual $25,000 contract with the newly reopened <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City_Englert_Theatre" title="Iowa City Englert Theatre" class="mw-redirect">Iowa City Englert Theatre</a> to host concerts and performances for as many as 40 nights a year.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Students also participate in a variety of student media organizations. For example, students edit and manage <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Iowan" title="The Daily Iowan">The Daily Iowan newspaper</a> (often called the DI), which is printed every Monday through Friday while classes are in session. An early editor of the DI was noted pollster <a href="/wiki/George_Gallup" title="George Gallup">George Gallup</a>. Daily Iowan TV, <a href="/wiki/KRUI-FM" title="KRUI-FM">KRUI Radio</a>, Student Video Productions, Off Deadline magazine and Earthwords magazine are other examples of student-run media.</p>

     <p>The school's sports teams, the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes" title="Iowa Hawkeyes">Hawkeyes</a>, participate in the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>'s Division I-A and in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a>. The athletics department is headed by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Barta" title="Gary Barta">Gary Barta</a>, who was previously athletic director at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wyoming" title="University of Wyoming">University of Wyoming</a>. <a href="/wiki/Herky_the_Hawk" title="Herky the Hawk">Herky the Hawk</a> (pictured right) has been Iowa's mascot since 1948.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_football" title="Iowa Hawkeyes football">Iowa Hawkeyes football</a>
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_Football" title="Iowa Hawkeyes Football" class="mw-redirect">Iowa's football</a> team plays its home games at <a href="/wiki/Kinnick_Stadium" title="Kinnick Stadium">Kinnick Stadium</a>, named after former Iowa football player <a href="/wiki/Nile_Kinnick" title="Nile Kinnick">Nile Kinnick</a> who won the <a href="/wiki/Heisman_Trophy" title="Heisman Trophy">Heisman Trophy</a> in 1939. Kinnick Stadium hosts 70,585 fans. The stadium unveiled a new look in 2006 with the completion of a $90 million renovation. The renovation included new stands in the south endzone, a new press box, and a statue of Kinnick.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Kirk_Ferentz" title="Kirk Ferentz">Kirk Ferentz</a> has been the head coach of the football program since 1999, when he replaced longtime coach <a href="/wiki/Hayden_Fry" title="Hayden Fry">Hayden Fry</a>. Under Ferentz, the Hawkeyes have enjoyed national success, making eight bowl games in the last nine years, including a <a href="/wiki/Bowl_Championship_Series" title="Bowl Championship Series">Bowl Championship Series</a> victory in the <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Orange_Bowl" title="FedEx Orange Bowl" class="mw-redirect">FedEx Orange Bowl</a> over Georgia Tech in 2010. The program has also finished in the Top 10 four times and shared two Big Ten championships in the Ferentz era.</p> 
     <p>Iowa battles <a href="/wiki/Iowa_State_University" title="Iowa State University">Iowa State University</a> annually for the <a href="/wiki/Cy-Hawk_Trophy" title="Cy-Hawk Trophy" class="mw-redirect">Cy-Hawk Trophy</a>, a traveling award. Iowa also has a traditional rivalry with <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_football" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers football">Minnesota</a>. The two schools' football teams meet yearly to battle for <a href="/wiki/Floyd_of_Rosedale" title="Floyd of Rosedale" class="mw-redirect">Floyd of Rosedale</a>, a traveling trophy in the shape of a bronzed pig. In 2004, Iowa and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">Wisconsin</a> unveiled the <a href="/wiki/Heartland_Trophy" title="Heartland Trophy" class="mw-redirect">Heartland Trophy</a>, a bronze bull, to be played for in their rivalry. However, since the Big Ten has expanded to 12 teams, Iowa will only play Wisconsin 6 times every 10 years while the Floyd of Rosedale series remains an annual matchup. A new rivalry is in the making with Nebraska, with the teams competing for the Heroes Trophy.</p> 
     <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal" class="mw-redirect">Wall Street Journal</a>, Iowa has produced more Division I college-football coaches than any other school in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wrestling">Wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Wrestling">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_wrestling" title="Iowa Hawkeyes wrestling">Iowa Hawkeyes wrestling</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Iowa is famous for its extremely successful <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_wrestling" title="Collegiate wrestling">collegiate wrestling</a> program. Through 2010, the Hawkeyes wrestling team has won 23 national titles and 34 Big Ten titles. Coach <a href="/wiki/Dan_Gable" title="Dan Gable">Dan Gable</a>'s Gang won nine straight <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Wrestling_Team_Championship" title="NCAA Wrestling Team Championship">NCAA team championships</a> (1978 to 1986) and three times won three in a row (1991 to 1993, 1995 to 1997, and 2008 to 2010). Iowa's 51 NCAA Champions have won a total of 77 NCAA individual titles, crowning six three-time and 15 two-time champions. Furthermore, Iowa's 132 all-Americans have earned <a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">all-America</a> status 269 times, including 16 four-time, 29 three-time and 34 two-time honorees. <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> named the Iowa program one of the top sports dynasties of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Brands" title="Tom Brands">Tom Brands</a>, a four-time All-American and Olympic gold medalist, is the current head wrestling coach.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Men.27s_basketball">Men's basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Men's basketball">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_men%27s_basketball" title="Iowa Hawkeyes men's basketball">Iowa Hawkeyes men's basketball</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_men%27s_basketball" title="Iowa Hawkeyes men's basketball">Iowa Hawkeyes men's basketball</a> team currently plays in 15,500-seat <a href="/wiki/Carver-Hawkeye_Arena" title="Carver-Hawkeye Arena" class="mw-redirect">Carver-Hawkeye Arena</a>, along with the school's women's basketball, <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Hawkeyes_wrestling" title="Iowa Hawkeyes wrestling">wrestling</a>, and volleyball teams.</p> 
     <p>Throughout history, the Hawkeyes have enjoyed the successes of eight Big Ten regular season conference championships, the last coming in 1979. More recently, Iowa has won the Big Ten <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference_Men%27s_Basketball_Tournament" title="Big Ten Conference Men's Basketball Tournament">tournament</a> twice, in 2001 and 2006. Iowa also has 22 <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament" class="mw-redirect">NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament</a> appearances, including three <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship#Final_Four" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">Final Fours</a>, reaching the semifinals in 1955 and 1980 and playing in the championship game against the <a href="/wiki/University_of_San_Francisco" title="University of San Francisco">University San Francisco</a> in 1956.</p> 
     <p>The team experienced success in the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s under <a href="/wiki/Head_coach" title="Head coach">head coaches</a> <a href="/wiki/Lute_Olson" title="Lute Olson">Lute Olson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Davis_(basketball)" title="Tom Davis (basketball)" class="mw-redirect">Tom Davis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fran_McCaffery" title="Fran McCaffery">Fran McCaffery</a> is the current head men's basketball coach.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_basketball">Women's basketball</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Women's basketball">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The Iowa Hawkeye women's basketball team experienced great success in the 1980s and early 1990s under coach <a href="/wiki/C._Vivian_Stringer" title="C. Vivian Stringer">C. Vivian Stringer</a>. In 1985, the Hawkeyes became the first women's basketball team in history to sellout in advance.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> During Stringer's tenure, the Hawkeyes appeared in 10 Women's NCAA Tournaments, including 9 consecutive berths from 1985–86 through 1993–94. Additionally, the Hawkeyes appeared in the 1993 Women's Final Four, losing to the Ohio State Buckeyes in the semifinals. Stringer's successor at Iowa, Angie Lee, took Iowa to an additional 3 NCAA tournaments, including a Sweet Sixteen appearance during the 1995–96 season. Current coach Lisa Bluder has taken the Hawkeyes to five NCAA tournaments and two <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Invitation_Tournament" title="Women's National Invitation Tournament">WNIT</a> berths, including an appearance in the WNIT semifinals during the 2004–2005 season. Most recently, the women were Big 10 Champions (tying with Ohio State) in 2008.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sports">Other sports</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Iowa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other sports">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Other sports at the university include field hockey, soccer, baseball, softball, gymnastics, golf, swimming and diving, tennis, track and field, volleyball, cross country, and rowing. In addition, UI has many club teams that compete in fencing, water polo, baseball, rugby, lacrosse, volleyball, crew, soccer, ultimate, ice hockey, cricket and other sports and there is also a women's flag football team.</p>
