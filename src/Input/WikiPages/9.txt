     <p>The <b>University of Saskatchewan</b> (<b>U of S</b>) is a Canadian <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university" class="mw-redirect">research university</a>, founded in 1907, and located on the east side of the <a href="/wiki/South_Saskatchewan_River" title="South Saskatchewan River">South Saskatchewan River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, Canada. An &quot;Act to establish and incorporate a University for the Province of Saskatchewan&quot; was passed by the provincial legislature in 1907. It established the provincial university on April 3, 1907 &quot;for the purpose of providing facilities for <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> in all its branches and enabling all persons without regard to race, creed or religion to take the fullest advantage&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-1907act_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1907act-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Saskatchewan is now the largest education institution in the Canadian province of Saskatchewan.</p> 
     <p>The university began as an agricultural college in 1907 and established the first Canadian university-based department of extension in 1910. 300 acres (121&nbsp;ha) were set aside for university buildings and 1,000 acres (405&nbsp;ha) for the U of S farm, and agricultural fields. In total 10.32&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (3.985&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) was annexed for the university.<sup id="cite_ref-bare_url_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Currently, the main University campus is situated upon 2,425 acres (981&nbsp;ha), with another 500 acres (202&nbsp;ha) allocated for Innovation Place Research Park.<sup id="cite_ref-bare_url_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Saskatchewan agriculture college still has access to neighbouring urban research lands.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Saskatchewan's Vaccine and Infectious Disease Organization (VIDO) facility, (2003) develops DNA-enhanced immunization vaccines for both humans and animals.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Since its origins as an agricultural college, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Graduate_Studies_and_Research" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">research</a> has played an important role at the university. Discoveries made at the U of S include sulphate-resistant cement and the <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">cobalt-60</a> cancer therapy unit. The university currently offers over <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">200 academic programs</a>. Duncan P. McColl was appointed as the first registrar, establishing the first convocation from which Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/Edward_Ludlow_Wetmore" title="Edward Ludlow Wetmore">Edward L. Wetmore</a> was elected as the first chancellor. <a href="/wiki/Walter_Charles_Murray" title="Walter Charles Murray">Walter Charles Murray</a> became the first president of the university's board of governors.<sup id="cite_ref-SK-history_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SK-history-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Health_Sciences_Building.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Health_Sciences_Building.JPG/220px-Health_Sciences_Building.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Health_Sciences_Building.JPG/330px-Health_Sciences_Building.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Health_Sciences_Building.JPG/440px-Health_Sciences_Building.JPG 2x" /></a>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Health_Sciences_Building.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>

     <p>The institution was modelled on the American state university, with an emphasis on extension work and applied research.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Saskatchewan, at Saskatoon, was granted a provincial charter on April 3, 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-Pound_2005_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pound_2005-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> A provincial statute known as the <i>University Act</i>. It provided for a publicly funded, yet independent institution to be created for the citizens of the whole province.</p> 
     <p>The governance was modelled on the provincial University of Toronto Act of 1906 which established a bicameral system of university government consisting of a senate (faculty), responsible for academic policy, and a board of governors (citizens) exercising exclusive control over financial policy and having formal authority in all other matters. The president, appointed by the board, was to provide a link between the two bodies and to perform institutional leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The scope of the new institution was to include colleges of arts and science, including art, music and commerce, agriculture with forestry, domestic science, education, engineering, law, medicine, pharmacy, veterinary science and dentistry.</p> 
     <p>Saskatoon was chosen as the site for the University on April 7, 1909 by the board of governors. On October 12, 1912 the first building opened its doors for student admission.<sup id="cite_ref-Pound_2005_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pound_2005-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> It awarded its first degrees in 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In the early part of this century, professional education expanded beyond the traditional fields of theology, law and medicine. Graduate training based on the German-inspired American model of specialized course work and the completion of a research thesis was introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Battleford" title="Battleford">Battleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moose_Jaw" title="Moose Jaw">Moose Jaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert,_Saskatchewan" title="Prince Albert, Saskatchewan">Prince Albert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a> all lobbied to be the location of the new university. Walter Murray preferred the provincial capital, Regina. In a politically influenced vote, Saskatoon was chosen on April 7, 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-SK-history_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SK-history-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg/220px-InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg/330px-InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg/440px-InMemoryOfAllRanksUofS.jpg 2x" /></a>

        </div> Plaque commemorating World War I veterans: &quot;1914?1918 In Memory of All Ranks of the 46th Battalion C.E.F. They are too near to be great, but our children shall understand when and how our fate was changed, and by whose hand.&quot;

     <p>Designed by David Roberston Brown (architect), the Memorial Gates were erected in 1927 at the corner of College Drive and Hospital Drive in honour of the University of Saskatchewan alumni who served in the First World War. A stone wall bears inscriptions of the names of the sisty seven university students and faculty who lost their lives while on service during World War I. <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The hallways of the Old Administrative Building (College Building) at the University of Saskatchewan are decorated with memorial scrolls in honour of the University of Saskatchewan alumni who served in the World Wars.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Board_of_Canada" title="National Film Board of Canada">National Film Board of Canada</a> documentary &quot;Prairie University&quot; (1955) directed by John Feeney explores diverse research activities at the University of Saskatchewan on agriculture, medicine, and ice cream.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A college of veterinary medicine opened at the University of Saskatchewan on July 2, 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-Pound_2005_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pound_2005-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Saskatchewan's Arms were registered with the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Heraldic_Authority" title="Canadian Heraldic Authority">Canadian Heraldic Authority</a> on February 15, 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:NobelPlaza.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/NobelPlaza.jpg/200px-NobelPlaza.jpg" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/NobelPlaza.jpg/300px-NobelPlaza.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/NobelPlaza.jpg/400px-NobelPlaza.jpg 2x" /></a>

     <p>A location next to the South Saskatchewan River, across from the <a href="/wiki/Central_Business_District,_Saskatoon" title="Central Business District, Saskatoon">city centre</a> of Saskatoon, was selected for the <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a>. David Roberston Brown of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_%26_Vallance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brown &amp; Vallance (page does not exist)">Brown &amp; Vallance</a> were the initial architects constructing a campus plan and the first university buildings in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture_in_Canada" title="Gothic Revival architecture in Canada">Collegiate Gothic style</a>: The Prime Minister of Canada, Sir <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Laurier" title="Wilfrid Laurier">Wilfrid Laurier</a>, laid the cornerstone of the first building, the <a href="/wiki/College_Building_(Saskatchewan)" title="College Building (Saskatchewan)" class="mw-redirect">College Building</a>, on July 29, 1910. The first building to be started on the new campus, the College Building, built 1910?1912 opened in 1913; in 2001, it was declared a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Site_of_Canada" title="National Historic Site of Canada" class="mw-redirect">National Historic Site of Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Brown &amp; Vallance designed the Administration Building (1910?12); Saskatchewan Hall Student Residence (1910?12). Brown &amp; Vallance designed the Engineering Building (1910?12) as well as additions 1913 in 1920 and rebuilt the building after it burned in 1925. Brown &amp; Vallance designed the Barn and Stock Pavilion (1910?12) and Emmanuel College (1910?12). Brown &amp; Vallance built the Faculty Club (1911?12) and rebuilt it after it burned in 1964. Brown &amp; Vallance constructed the President's Residence (1911?13) Qu'Appelle Hall Student Residence (1914?16) Physics Building (1919?21); Chemistry Building (1922?23); St. Andrew's Presbyterian College (1922?23); Memorial Gates (1927?28) and the Field Husbandry Building (1929).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The original buildings were built using native <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> ? greystone ? which was mined just north of campus. Over the years, this greystone became one of the most recognizable campus signatures. When the local supply of limestone was exhausted, the University turned to <a href="/wiki/Tyndall_stone" title="Tyndall stone">Tyndall stone</a>, which is quarried in Manitoba.</p> 
     <p>Saskatchewan's Provincial University and Agricultural College were officially opened May 1, 1913 by Hon. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Walter_Scott" title="Thomas Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:TheBowl-UofS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/TheBowl-UofS.jpg/220px-TheBowl-UofS.jpg" width="220" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/TheBowl-UofS.jpg/330px-TheBowl-UofS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/TheBowl-UofS.jpg/440px-TheBowl-UofS.jpg 2x" /></a>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:TheBowl-UofS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf8/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>

     <p>The original architectural plan called for the university buildings to be constructed around a green space known as <i>The Bowl</i>. The original university buildings are now connected by skywalks and tunnels. Clockwise, from the north; Thorvaldson Building (August 22, 1924) (Spinks addition); Geology, W.P. Thompson Biology (1960) adjoined to Physics Building (1921); College Building (May 1, 1913) (Administration addition); Saskatchewan cojoined with Athabasca Hall (1964); Qu'Appelle Hall (1916); Marquis Hall adjoined to Place Riel ? Qu'Appelle Addition; Murray Memorial Main Library (1956); Arts (1960) cojoined with Law and adjoined to Commerce building complete the initial circle around the perimeter of the bowl.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Francis Henry Portnall and Frank Martin designed the Dairy &amp; Soils Laboratory (1947).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Roughly adhering to the original plan of 1909, numerous colleges were established: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Arts_and_Science" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Arts &amp; Science</a> (1909); Agriculture, now called <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_College_of_Agriculture_and_Bioresources" title="University of Saskatchewan College of Agriculture and Bioresources">Agriculture and Bioresources</a> (1912); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Engineering" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Engineering</a> (1912); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_College_of_Law" title="University of Saskatchewan College of Law">Law</a> (1913); Pharmacy, now called <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Pharmacy_and_Nutrition" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Pharmacy &amp; Nutrition</a> (1914); Commerce, now the <a href="/wiki/N._Murray_Edwards_School_of_Business" title="N. Murray Edwards School of Business" class="mw-redirect">N. Murray Edwards School of Business</a> (1917); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_College_of_Medicine" title="University of Saskatchewan College of Medicine">Medicine</a> (1926); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Education" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Education</a> (1927); Home Economics (1928); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Nursing" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Nursing</a> (1938); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Graduate_Studies_and_Research" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Graduate Studies and Research</a> (1946); Physical Education, now called <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Kinesiology" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Kinesiology</a> (1958); <a href="/wiki/Western_College_of_Veterinary_Medicine" title="Western College of Veterinary Medicine">Veterinary Medicine</a> (1964); <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Dentistry" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Dentistry</a> (1965); and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Medicine_.28School_of_Physical_Therapy.29" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">School of Physical Therapy</a> (1976).</p> 
     <p>The U of S also has several graduate programs amongst these colleges, which give rise to a masters or doctorate degree.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1966, the University of Saskatchewan introduced a masters program in adult education. Diploma, and certificate post secondary courses are also available to aid in professional development.</p>

     <p>Theological Colleges, affiliated with the university, were also established: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#College_of_Emmanuel_and_St._Chad" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Emmanuel College</a> ? (Anglican denomination) (1909), St. Andrew's College (as <i>Presbyterian College, Saskatoon</i>) then <a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Canada" title="United Church of Canada">United Church of Canada</a> (1913), <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Theological_Seminary,_Saskatoon" title="Lutheran Theological Seminary, Saskatoon">Lutheran Theological Seminary</a> (1920), <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_More_College" title="St. Thomas More College">St. Thomas More College</a> (1936), and <a href="/wiki/Central_Pentecostal_College" title="Central Pentecostal College" class="mw-redirect">Central Pentecostal College</a> (1983).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Regina College was saved from bankruptcy and became part of the university in 1934, and was given degree-granting privileges in 1959, making it a second University of Saskatchewan campus. By another act of legislation in 1974, Regina College was made an independent institution known as the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Regina" title="University of Regina">University of Regina</a>.</p> 
     <p>The policy of university education initiated in the 1960s responded to population pressure and the belief that higher education was a key to social justice and economic productivity for individuals and for society. The single-university policy in the West was changed as existing colleges of the provincial universities gained autonomy as universities.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Correspondence courses were established in 1929.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_development">Later development</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later development">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In the late 1990s, the U of S launched a major revitalisation program, comprising new capital projects such as an expansion to the <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_Medicine_%E2%80%93_University_of_Saskatchewan" title="Veterinary Medicine ? University of Saskatchewan" class="mw-redirect">Western College of Veterinary Medicine</a>, the building of a new parkade, and a revision of its internal road layout (which has already seen the East Road access being realigned). The Thorvaldson Building, which currently is home to the departments of chemistry and computer science, hosts a new expansion known as the Spinks addition. The College of Pharmacy and Nutrition has also seen a number of renovations.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_holdings">Land holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Land holdings">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Up until the late 1980s, the University of Saskatchewan held an extensive area of land in the northeast quadrant of Saskatoon, stretching far beyond the core campus, east of Preston Avenue and north of the <a href="/wiki/Sutherland,_Saskatoon" title="Sutherland, Saskatoon">Sutherland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_Grove,_Saskatoon" title="Forest Grove, Saskatoon">Forest Grove</a> subdivisions. Much of this land was used for farming, though some areas were intended for future campus and facility development. In the late 1980s, most U of S land beyond Circle Drive was earmarked for residential development; <a href="/wiki/Silverspring,_Saskatoon" title="Silverspring, Saskatoon">Silverspring</a> was the first of these neighbourhoods to be developed.</p>

     <p>Another section of land, west of the Preston Avenue/Circle Drive interchange and north of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific Railway</a> line, was zoned for commercial use, and led to &quot;big box&quot; retail development in the early 2000s called <a href="/wiki/Preston_Crossing" title="Preston Crossing">Preston Crossing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Realignment of two major roads in the area around this same time (Preston Avenue and 108th Street) also used up a portion of university land. The U of S subsequently obtained a large tract of land immediately east of the Saskatoon city limits that it obtained after the city annexed the northeastern section of U of S land (this land has since been itself annexed into the city). The U of S leased a site to the <a href="/wiki/Correctional_Service_of_Canada" title="Correctional Service of Canada">Correctional Service of Canada</a> north of Attridge Drive on Central Avenue for the Regional Psychiatric Centre. It has an additional undeveloped parcel of land at Central Avenue and Fedoruk Drive.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the 1970s and again in the 1980s, the U of S considered opening up some of its land holdings south of College Drive and north of 14th Street for residential development, but opposition from nearby neighbourhoods that appreciated the &quot;green belt&quot; offered by the university led to these plans being dropped.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Students_and_alumni">Students and alumni</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Students and alumni">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>The <i>University Act</i> provided that the University should provide &quot;facilities for higher education in all its branches and enabling all persons without regard to race, creed or religion to take the fullest advantage&quot;. It further stated that &quot;no woman shall by reason of her sex be deprived of any advantage or privilege accorded to the male students of the university.&quot; Seventy students began the first classes on September 28, 1909. The first class graduated on May 1, 1912. Of the three students who earned graduation honours, two were women.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>342 students, faculty, and staff enlisted for <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Of these, 67 were killed, 100 were wounded, and 33 were awarded medals of valour.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Between 1907 and 2007 there have been over 132,200 members of the University of Saskatchewan Alumni Association. The alumni feature those who have successfully graduated from a degree, certificate and/or diploma programme at the University of Saskatchewan.<sup id="cite_ref-usask.ca_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usask.ca-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Over the years, some of the most prominent projects at the University have been associated with the Department of Physics. In 1948, the university built the first <a href="/wiki/Betatron" title="Betatron">betatron</a> facility in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Three years later, the world's first non-commercial <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">cobalt-60</a> therapy unit was constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> (The first female Chancellor of the University, <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Fedoruk" title="Sylvia Fedoruk">Sylvia Fedoruk</a>, was a member of the Cobalt-60 research team. She also served as Saskatchewan's <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-Governor_(Canada)" title="Lieutenant-Governor (Canada)" class="mw-redirect">Lieutenant-Governor</a> from 1988?1994.) The success of these facilities led to the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Linear_accelerator" title="Linear accelerator" class="mw-redirect">linear accelerator</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="Saskatchewan Accelerator Laboratory">Saskatchewan Accelerator Laboratory</a> in 1964 and placed university scientists at the forefront of nuclear physics in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Experience gained from years of research and collaboration with global researchers led to the University of Saskatchewan being selected as the site of Canada's national facility for <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_light" title="Synchrotron light" class="mw-redirect">synchrotron light</a> research, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Light_Source" title="Canadian Light Source">Canadian Light Source</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> This facility opened October 22, 2004 and is the size of a football field. The <a href="/wiki/Plasma_Physics_Laboratory_(Saskatchewan)" title="Plasma Physics Laboratory (Saskatchewan)">Plasma Physics Laboratory</a> operates a <a href="/wiki/Tokamak" title="Tokamak">tokamak</a> on campus.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The University used the <a href="/wiki/SCR-270_radar" title="SCR-270 radar">SCR-270 radar</a> in 1949 to image the <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">Aurora</a> for the first time.</p>

     <p>The University of Saskatchewan has numerous royal and vice-regal connections. Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Archibald_McNab" title="Archibald McNab">Archibald McNab</a> is credited with bringing the institution to Saskatoon.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Its campuses have been venues for royal and vice-regal visits, including visits by the Queen and the Duke of Edinburgh, who lodged at the President's Residence in 1978. A handful of University alumni were invited to a reception for Canadians at <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a> ahead of that visit to Saskatoon.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The main campus is home to Saskatoon's only royally designated institution, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_University_Hospital" title="Royal University Hospital">Royal University Hospital</a>. The Diefenbaker Canada Centre, also on campus, houses original correspondence between the Queen and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Diefenbaker" title="John Diefenbaker">John Diefenbaker</a>, and has staged such exhibits as <i>Happy and Glorious: The Royal Presence in Canada</i>, opened by Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Haverstock" title="Lynda Haverstock">Lynda Haverstock</a> in 2004. The campus was the first in Western Canada to host the <a href="/wiki/Vanier_Cup" title="Vanier Cup">Vanier Cup</a>, named for Governor General <a href="/wiki/Georges_Vanier" title="Georges Vanier">Georges Vanier</a>, in 2006. Fifteen fellows of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Canada" title="Royal Society of Canada">Royal Society of Canada</a> are affiliated with the University of Saskatchewan.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Saskatchewan's <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Saskatchewan" title="Lieutenant Governor of Saskatchewan">Lieutenant Governors</a> have filled the office of <b>Visitor to the University of Saskatchewan</b> since its establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-1995act_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1995act-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Former Lieutenant Governor Sir Richard Lake was famously called upon to assume the visitor's role in the so-called <i>Crisis of 1919</i>. Four senior members of the Board of Governors had been dismissed after three among them abstained from a vote of confidence in university president <a href="/wiki/Walter_Charles_Murray" title="Walter Charles Murray">Walter Murray</a>. Murray was under scrutiny for his maintenance of University finances. The public and press clamoured for an explanation, and, in accordance with provincial law, Lake held a series of hearings through the office of the <a href="/wiki/King%E2%80%99s_Bench" title="King?s Bench" class="mw-redirect">King?s Bench</a>. His findings, delivered in April 1920, vindicated the dismissals, saying they were ?regular, proper and in the best interest of the university.? In other words, their acts of disloyalty were enough to cost them their jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Certain vice-regal representatives have held teaching and governance positions on campus. Before becoming Lieutenant Governor, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Barnhart" title="Gordon Barnhart">Gordon Barnhart</a> was university secretary and professor in Canadian politics. <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Fedoruk" title="Sylvia Fedoruk">Sylvia Fedoruk</a> was university chancellor, professor in oncology and associate member in physics. <a href="/wiki/Grant_MacEwan" title="Grant MacEwan">Grant MacEwan</a>, before becoming Lieutenant Governor of Alberta, was director of the School of Agriculture and professor of animal husbandry at the University of Saskatchewan. Honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">Doctor of Laws</a> degrees have been conferred by the university on vice-regal representatives.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Recipient Lieutenant Governors include <a href="/wiki/William_John_Patterson" title="William John Patterson">William Patterson</a> in 1955, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hanbidge" title="Robert Hanbidge">Robert Hanbidge</a> in 1968, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Worobetz" title="Stephen Worobetz">Stephen Worobetz</a> in 1984 and <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Fedoruk" title="Sylvia Fedoruk">Sylvia Fedoruk</a> in 2006. Recipient Governors General include <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Massey" title="Vincent Massey">Vincent Massey</a> in 1955 and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Hnatyshyn" title="Ray Hnatyshyn">Ramon Hnatyshyn</a> in 1990.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="University_administration">University administration</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: University administration">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>There are three separate areas of governance at the University of Saskatchewan. Financial, management, as well as administration affairs are handled by the Board of Governors, which comprises 11 members. The University of Saskatchewan liaison between the public and professional sector is dealt with by the university Senate, a body of 100 representatives. Finally, the General Academic Assembly is the university's advisory body wherein, all the faculty members and elected students combine to determine academic policies and direction. In 1995, the General Academic Assembly is represented by elected members to the University of Saskatchewan Council which is made up of a combination of 116 faculty and students.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2006, faculty and staff total 7,000, and student enrolment comprised 15,005 full-time students as well as 3,552 part-time students.<sup id="cite_ref-usask.ca_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usask.ca-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In October 2008, the University of Saskatchewan was named one of <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan%27s_Top_Employers" title="Saskatchewan's Top Employers" class="mw-redirect">Saskatchewan's Top Employers</a>, which was announced by the Saskatoon <i><a href="/wiki/StarPhoenix" title="StarPhoenix" class="mw-redirect">StarPhoenix</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Regina_Leader-Post" title="Regina Leader-Post" class="mw-redirect">Regina Leader-Post</a></i> newspapers.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Board of Governors administrators comprise Chancellor Vera Pezer, President and Vice-Chancellor Dr. R. Peter MacKinnon <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">Q.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Laws" title="Bachelor of Laws">LL.B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Laws" title="Master of Laws">LL.M.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">LL.D.</a>; Vice-Presidents or Resource Officers: Brett Fairbairn, Provost and Vice-President Academic; Richard Florizone, Vice-President (Finance &amp; Resources); Karen Chad, Vice-President (Research); Heather Magotiaux, Vice-President (University Advancement).<sup id="cite_ref-usask.ca_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usask.ca-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_presidents">University presidents</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: University presidents">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The past 100+ years have seen nine university presidents beginning with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Charles_Murray" title="Walter Charles Murray">Walter Charles Murray</a> (1908?37) who helped establish and set the groundwork for the University of Saskatchewan. In other words,</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>to make a University where no University existed.<sup id="cite_ref-quote_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quote-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/James_Sutherland_Thomson" title="James Sutherland Thomson" class="mw-redirect">James Sutherland Thomson</a> (1937?49) was the second president</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>during some of the most difficult years in the institution's history. His term spanned the final years of the Great Depression, the Second World War, and the hectic, early post-war years.<sup id="cite_ref-quote2_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quote2-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Walter_P._Thompson" title="Walter P. Thompson">Walter Palmer Thompson</a> presidency term (1949?59) spanned the university's 50th anniversary year. Dr. Walter Murray said to him</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>&quot;Do not be appalled at the absence of all you have been accustomed to find in other Universities. We have all had to start with nothing.. .You have virgin soil to break.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>In many fields Biologist Walter P. Thompson leadership brought innovation, insight and research to new areas beginning with rust resistant varieties of wheat which curtailed the 1916 catastrophic outbreak of rust. He also was instrumental in developing in Saskatchewan a comprehensive medicare program. His popularity and qualities of administrator and teacher served the U of S well during his presidency.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> The fourth and youngest Canadian university president, <a href="/wiki/John_William_Tranter_Spinks" title="John William Tranter Spinks" class="mw-redirect">John William Tranter Spinks</a> (1959?74) brought in a whirlwind era for the university.</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>...enrolment sky rocketed, a new campus was opened, new buildings were erected, new colleges and schools were started and course offerings were increased. This included a comprehensive health care complex, establishment of a Crop Development Centre, a Linear Accelerator Laboratory, SED Systems, an Institute for Northern Studies, and an Indian and Northern Education Program.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Robert_William_Begg" title="Robert William Begg" class="mw-redirect">Robert William Begg</a> (1974?80) became the fifth U of S president, who received the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a> during his term in office</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>for his distinguished career in education and for his contributions to cancer research.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The sixth president was <a href="/wiki/Leo_Friman_Kristjanson" title="Leo Friman Kristjanson" class="mw-redirect">Leo Friman Kristjanson</a> (1980?89) who served a foreshortened term due to the onset of Parkinson's disease. However, even during this tenure from which he left early, the University saw the addition of the Innovation Place research park, Geology Building, Kinsmen Children's Centre, The Centre for the Study of Co-operatives, a new Saskatoon Cancer Clinic, expansion of health science facilities, the Rt. Hon. John G. Diefenbaker Centre, and the groundwork was laid for the new agriculture building.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The seventh university president was <a href="/wiki/George_Ivany" title="George Ivany">George Ivany</a> (1989?99).</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>The J.W. George Ivany Internationalization Award, to be presented annually, was established in 1998 to acknowledge Dr. Ivany's &quot;commitment to internationalization and his leadership in fulfillment of that commitment&quot;.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The eighth president of the University of Saskatchewan was <a href="/wiki/R._Peter_MacKinnon" title="R. Peter MacKinnon" class="mw-redirect">R. Peter MacKinnon</a> (1999?2012).</p> 
     <p>The current president of the University of Saskatchewan is <a href="/wiki/Ilene_Busch-Vishniac" title="Ilene Busch-Vishniac">Ilene Busch-Vishniac</a> (2012? ).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_chancellors">University chancellors</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: University chancellors">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The University of Saskatchewan chancellor would have the duties to preside over convocation ceremonies whereupon they would confer degrees, they chair the Senate and become members of the Board of Governors.<sup id="cite_ref-senate_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-senate-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The first University of Saskatchewan chancellor was Justice <a href="/wiki/Edward_Ludlow_Wetmore" title="Edward Ludlow Wetmore">Edward Ludlow Wetmore</a> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> (1909?1917). The University of Saskatchewan Board of Governors honoured him with an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Civil_Law" title="Doctor of Civil Law">D.C.L.</a>, in recognition of the contributions Edward Wetmore gave to</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>both (the province and country) [which] enjoy in rich measure the results of his great common sense, his judiciousness and his high sense of public duty in shaping those fundamental traditions which give character and direction to the activities of two of the most influential institutions of the State, the judiciary and the University.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>Honourable Sir <a href="/wiki/Frederick_W._A._G._Haultain" title="Frederick W. A. G. Haultain">Frederick W. A. G. Haultain</a> <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Bench" title="King's Bench" class="mw-redirect">K.B.</a>, the second chancellor served the university 1917?1940. Sir Frederick W. A. G. Haultain, Chief Justice of Saskatchewan, was instrumental in the establishment of the provinces of Saskatchewan and Alberta from the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_Canada" title="Territorial evolution of Canada">North West Territories</a>, also Commissioner of Education, he also spent time and energy developing the early school system on the rugged frontier. Next, in line was Justice <a href="/wiki/P._E._MacKenzie" title="P. E. MacKenzie">P. E. MacKenzie</a> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Laws" title="Bachelor of Laws">LL.B</a>. between 1940?1946. In the years 1946?1947 <a href="/wiki/Donald_Maclean_(judge)" title="Donald Maclean (judge)">Donald Maclean</a> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">BSc</a>, LL.B and was a valued contributor to the University of Saskatchewan, and was awarded an honorary bachelor of law as well as appointed fourth chancellor. <a href="/wiki/F._H._Auld" title="F. H. Auld">F. H. Auld</a> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">LL.D.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> became the University's fifth chancellor holding several three year terms between 1947?1965. Francis Hedley received great recognition as Deputy Minister of Agriculture in Saskatchewan for his improvements.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/E._M._Culliton" title="E. M. Culliton">E. M. Culliton</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">CC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Order_of_Merit" title="Saskatchewan Order of Merit">S.O.M.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">Q.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Civil_Law" title="Doctor of Civil Law">D.C.L.</a>, served the university as chancellor from 1965 to 1969. He served Saskatchewan in many fields and was regarded as the</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>cornerstone of a sound Saskatchewan judiciary.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/John_Diefenbaker" title="John Diefenbaker">John Diefenbaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Order of the Companions of Honour">CH</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Privy_Council_for_Canada" title="Queen's Privy Council for Canada">PC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">QC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Canada" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada" class="mw-redirect">FRSC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">FRSA</a> became the seventh chancellor between 1969?1979. He served country as Prime Minister and province as Member of Parliament well, and strived toward the</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>ultimate balance for free enterprise, profit-making and economic growth on the one hand, and social justice and respect for the interests of the common man on the other.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Emmett_M._Hall" title="Emmett M. Hall" class="mw-redirect">Emmett M. Hall</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">CC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">QC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Laws" title="Bachelor of Laws">LL.B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">LL.D</a> served as next chancellor for two terms between 1979?1986. Emmett M. Hall was chairman of several committees that helped to shape Saskatchewan. Saskatchewan's public health insurance was the precursor for Canada's national medicare system, as well as reforms to the current issues involving education, court structure and grain handling.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_O._Fedoruk" title="Sylvia O. Fedoruk" class="mw-redirect">Sylvia O. Fedoruk</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">OC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Order_of_Merit" title="Saskatchewan Order of Merit">SOM</a> followed as ninth University Chancellor from 1986?1989. Sylvia Fedoruk is renowned as a famous physics scientist who was implemental in developing the world?s first <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">Cobalt</a> 60 unit and first <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> scanning machines. Sylvia also was a renowned curler, and Lieutenant Governor of Saskatchewan and contributed to Saskatchewan's growth in many areas.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Between 1989?1995, <a href="/wiki/E._K._Turner" title="E. K. Turner">E. K. Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">C.M.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">LL.D.</a>, S.O.M. served as the University of Saskatchewan's ambassador, becoming the university's tenth chancellor. Saskatchewan agriculture benefited from the life work of E. K. Turner both internationally and nationally.<sup id="cite_ref-something_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-something-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peggy_McKercher" title="Peggy McKercher">Peggy McKercher</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">C.M.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Order_of_Merit" title="Saskatchewan Order of Merit">SOM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws" class="mw-redirect">LL.D.</a> elected by acclamation served as university chancellor between 1995?2001. Peggy McKercher has been always involved in civic growth and development. She has been honoured and gain tremendous recognition for her involvement in the fields of heritage, culture, and municipal development.<sup id="cite_ref-honour_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honour-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/W._Thomas_Molloy" title="W. Thomas Molloy">W. Thomas Molloy</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">OC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">QC</a> served between 2001?2007. He received the Order of Canada for</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>his integrity, commitment to a just settlement and personable rapport.<sup id="cite_ref-OC_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OC-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>On July 1, 2007, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Vera_Pezer" title="Vera Pezer">Vera Pezer</a> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">BA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts_(postgraduate)" title="Master of Arts (postgraduate)" class="mw-redirect">MA</a> PhD. was elected thirteenth chancellor of the University of Saskatchewan. Vera Pezer has served the university for a number of years as student counsellor, faculty member and dean. She has achieved success as a champion curler, author, and member of several civic steering committees.<sup id="cite_ref-esVP_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esVP-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums_and_galleries">Museums and galleries</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Museums and galleries">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The Agricultural Displays and Kloppenburg Collection are hosted in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_Agriculture_%26_Bioresources_College" title="University of Saskatchewan Agriculture &amp; Bioresources College" class="mw-redirect">Agriculture &amp; Bioresources College</a>. The agricultural wall displays are located in the walkway connecting the Agriculture Building and the Biology Building. The Kloppenburg Collection is featured on the sixth floor of the College of Agriculture and Bioresources building which opened in 1991. Twenty seven works by famous Saskatchewan artists are featured in this donation to the University of Saskatchewan.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Beamish_Conservatory_and_Leo_Kristjanson_Atrium" title="Beamish Conservatory and Leo Kristjanson Atrium" class="mw-redirect">Beamish Conservatory and Leo Kristjanson Atrium</a> is also located within the Agriculture &amp; Bioresources College. Diefenbaker Centre for the Study of Canada</a>, also known as the Diefenbaker Canada Centre, houses the Diefenbaker paper collection and legacy, changing exhibit, Centre for the Study of Co-operatives and the Native Law Centre. The grave site of Canadian Prime Minister John Diefenbaker is located near this museum.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Gordon Snelgrove Gallery features displays of Master of Fine Arts graduating exhibitions, as well as Bachelor of Fine Arts shows, and is located within the Murray Building on campus.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Kenderdine Art Gallery celebrated its official opening October 25, 1991. Augustus Frederick Lafosse (Gus) Kenderdine began the University Art Camp at Emma Lake in 1936, the precursor to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan_academics#Emma_Lake_Kenderdine_Campus" title="University of Saskatchewan academics">Emma Lake Kenderdine Campus</a>, a bequest was donated to the University of Saskatchewan by his daughter, Mrs. May Beamish, and initialized the formation of the Kenderdine Art Gallery which has a permanent collection started by Dr. Murray, as well as ongoing exhibits.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The Kenderdine collection consists of archival material and 4,000 works, including paintings, sketches, ceramics, porcelain or pottery, glass, textiles or tapestries many by 19th and 20th century Saskatchewan, Canadian and international artists. <sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> The MacAulay Pharmaceutical Collection is located in the Thorvaldson Building, Room 118A. The collection showcases early 20th-century pharmaceutical paraphernalia, as well as early First Nations remedies such as cherry bark syrup and smartweed.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The Victoria School House, known also as the Little Stone School House, was built in 1888 as the first school house of the Temperance Colony. The <a href="/wiki/One_room_school_house" title="One room school house" class="mw-redirect">one room school house</a> was originally constructed in <a href="/wiki/Nutana,_Saskatoon" title="Nutana, Saskatoon">Nutana</a>. The location is now known as five corners at the south or top of the <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Bridge_(Saskatoon)" title="Broadway Bridge (Saskatoon)">Broadway Bridge</a>. The school yard at one time comprised three school houses, as the population grew. The little stone school house was preserved and moved on campus. It was declared a historic site on June 1, 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_life">Campus life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Saskatchewan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Campus life">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Sheaf" title="The Sheaf">The Sheaf</a></i>, a student publication, was first published in 1912, monthly or less frequently. By 1920, it was published weekly with the aim of becoming a more unifying influence on student life. It has continued to publish.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1965, a student-run campus radio station, <a href="/wiki/CJUS-FM" title="CJUS-FM">CJUS-FM</a> began broadcasting on a non-commercial basis. In 1983, the station became a limited commercial station. By 1985, however, funding was no longer provided, and the campus radio presence died. In early 2005, CJUS was revived in an <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio" title="Internet radio">internet radio</a> form and continues to broadcast today.<sup id="cite_ref-scaa.usask.ca_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scaa.usask.ca-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> The university also maintains a relationship with the independent <a href="/wiki/Community_radio" title="Community radio">community radio</a> station <a href="/wiki/CFCR-FM" title="CFCR-FM">CFCR-FM</a>, which actively solicits volunteers on campus.</p> 
     <p><i>Place Riel Theatre</i>, a campus theatre, was opened in 1975, as was <i>Louis</i>, a campus pub. <i>Place Riel</i>, the existing campus student centre, opened in 1980, and now holds retail outlets, arcade, lounge space, student group meeting areas, and a <a href="/wiki/Food_court" title="Food court">food court</a>; it is presently undergoing expansion and renovation, slated for completion in 2012?2013. These facilities were named after <a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel" title="Louis Riel">Louis Riel</a>. In the late 1990s, Place Riel Theatre stopped public showings and it is now used for campus movie features and lectures.<sup id="cite_ref-scaa.usask.ca_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scaa.usask.ca-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Saskatchewan has adopted as its logo the book of knowledge and three wheat sheaves set inside of a green heraldic shield. The wheat sheaves and book of knowledge are yellow. Upon the pages of the book of knowledge is the Latin phrase <i>Deo et Patrie</i> which when translated means <i>For God and Country</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The official motto of the university is <i>Deo et Patriae</i> (Latin) which translates to <i>God and Country</i>.</p>

     <p>Campus sports teams in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a> use the name <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Huskies" title="Saskatchewan Huskies">Saskatchewan Huskies</a>. The U of S Huskies presently compete in eight men's sports: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer" class="mw-redirect">soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_wrestling" title="Amateur wrestling">wrestling</a> and seven women's sports: <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, cross country, hockey, soccer, track and field, volleyball and wrestling. The men's Husky football team has won the <a href="/wiki/Vanier_Cup" title="Vanier Cup">Vanier Cup</a> as national champions on three occasions; in 1990, 1996, and 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Voyageurs" title="Voyageurs">Voyageur</a> Place 'Room and board' residences on the University of Saskatchewan campus and comprises four separate halls.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> 
       <ul> 
        <li>Saskatchewan Hall was the first student residence of the university and was completed in 1912. Originally called University Hall, it was designed to provide residences for 150 students.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> Saskatchewan Hall was named for the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_River" title="Saskatchewan River">Saskatchewan River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-http_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
        <li>Qu'Appelle Hall was originally known as Student?s Residence No. 2 and officially opened in 1916. The design housed 120 students, and in 1963 an addition for 60 additional student residences was completed. The Qu'Appelle Hall Addition is the fourth residence of Voyageur Place and houses male students.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> Qu'Appelle Hall was named for the <a href="/wiki/Qu%27Appelle_River" title="Qu'Appelle River">Qu'Appelle River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-http_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
        <li>Athabasca Hall provides 270 residences and was completed in 1964. It is now a co-ed hall.<sup id="cite_ref-bare_url_a_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url_a-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> Athabasca Hall was named for the <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_River" title="Athabasca River">Athabasca River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-http_92-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
       </ul> </li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Voyageur Place has historically been organized on the <a href="/wiki/House_system" title="House system">house system</a>, with each house named after an explorer associated with Saskatchewan's early history. Thus, traditionally there were three male houses: Hearne House (named after <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hearne" title="Samuel Hearne">Samuel Hearne</a> and consisting of the residents of Saskatchewan Hall); Kelsey (named after <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kelsey" title="Henry Kelsey">Henry Kelsey</a> and consisting of the residents of Qu'Appelle Hall); and Lav (named after <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gaultier_de_Varennes,_sieur_de_La_V%C3%A9rendrye" title="Pierre Gaultier de Varennes, sieur de La V&eacute;rendrye">Pierre Gaultier de Varennes, sieur de La V&eacute;rendrye</a> and consisting of the residents of Qu'Appelle Hall Addition). There were also three female houses (all of which were composed of residents of the all-female Athabasca Hall): Pond (named after <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pond" title="Peter Pond">Peter Pond</a>), Henday (named after <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Henday" title="Anthony Henday">Anthony Henday</a>), and Palliser (named after <a href="/wiki/John_Palliser" title="John Palliser">John Palliser</a>).</p>

      <li>McEown Park ? Residence complex south of the university campus. McEown.<sup id="cite_ref-http_92-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup> 
       <ul> 
        <li>Souris Hall is an apartment complex for married students with families. Souris Hall, named after the <a href="/wiki/Souris_River" title="Souris River">Souris River</a>, is a nine-storey town house, comprising 67 two-bedroom apartments.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

        <li>Wollaston Hall was added to McEown Park complex in 1976, providing 21 two-bedroom and 83 one-bedroom apartments.<sup id="cite_ref-usask_97-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usask-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

Seager Wheeler Hall was named in honour of <a href="/wiki/Seager_Wheeler" title="Seager Wheeler">Seager Wheeler</a>, a notable Saskatchewan pioneer for breeding wheat. This residence was on the original three complexes built at McEown Park.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

     <p>The University of Saskatchewan provides services to Aboriginal people in more remote communities. The University of Saskatchewan Summer University Transition Course brings first-year Aboriginal students to campus before the start of the school year for some campus orientation. Academic counsellors, tutors and Aboriginal elders are present on campus at the University of Saskatchewan to provide academic and social supports. To assist with the transition to a fulfilling career, the University of Saskatchewan is participating in an Aboriginal Lynx Career and Employment Project led by University of Calgary.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p>
