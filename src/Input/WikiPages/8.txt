     <p>The <b>University of Calgary</b> (<b>U of C</b> or <b>UCalgary</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university" class="mw-redirect">research university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Founded in 1966 (after operating as the Calgary branch of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">University of Alberta</a> since 1945) the U of C is composed of 14 faculties and more than 85 research institutes and centres.</p> 
     <p>More than 25,000 undergraduate and 5,500 graduate students are currently enrolled. The U of C has graduated over 145,000 alumni, including the current <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister of Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a>, and Canadian astronaut <a href="/wiki/Robert_Thirsk" title="Robert Thirsk">Robert Thirsk</a>.</p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary is one of Canada?s top research universities (based on the number of <a href="/wiki/Canada_Research_Chairs" title="Canada Research Chairs" class="mw-redirect">Canada Research Chairs</a>) and is a member of the <a href="/wiki/U15_(universities)" title="U15 (universities)" class="mw-redirect">U15</a> (the 15 most research-intensive universities in Canada).</p> 
     <p>The U of C is the birthplace of a number of important inventions, including the <a href="/wiki/Neurochip" title="Neurochip">neurochip</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The university's sponsored research revenue of $352&nbsp;million, with total revenues exceeding $1.1&nbsp;billion, is one of the highest in the country. Being in Calgary, with Canada's highest concentration of engineers and geoscientists, the Faculty of Science, Department of <a href="/wiki/Geosciences" title="Geosciences" class="mw-redirect">Geosciences</a> and the Schulich School of <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">Engineering</a> maintain ties to the petroleum and geoscience industry.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The main campus is located in the north-west quadrant of Calgary, near the <a href="/wiki/Bow_River" title="Bow River">Bow River</a>, and covers more than 200 <a href="/wiki/Hectares" title="Hectares" class="mw-redirect">hectares</a> (500 acres).</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_overview">History and overview</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and overview">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <p>The University of Calgary was established in 1966, yet its roots date back more than half a century earlier to the establishment of the Normal School in <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a> in 1905. The Alberta Normal School was established in Calgary to train primary and secondary school teachers in the new province.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The people of Calgary, however, attempted to establish another publicly funded university in Calgary itself. &quot;An Act to Incorporate the University of Calgary&quot; was presented for first reading as a Bill in the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Legislature" title="Alberta Legislature" class="mw-redirect">Alberta Legislature</a> in an attempt to establish a private university in Calgary. The Legislature allowed the institution to incorporate as 'Calgary College', rather than as the 'University of Calgary'. It withheld from the College the powers to confer degrees and to hold the examinations required for admission to the professions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Calgary strove to have an <a href="/wiki/Institute" title="Institute">institute</a> of higher learning with degree conferring powers which led to the University of Alberta opening a branch in Calgary in 1945. The University of Alberta Calgary Branch eventually gained full autonomy in 1966 and became the University of Calgary.</p> 
     <p>The university was modeled on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> state university (similar to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">University of Alberta</a>), with an emphasis on extension work and applied research. The governance was modeled on the provincial <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> Act of 1906 which established a bicameral system of university government consisting of a senate (faculty), responsible for academic policy, and a board of governors (citizens) exercising exclusive control over financial policy and having formal authority in all other matters. The president, appointed by the board, was a link between the bodies to perform institutional leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the early 20th century, professional education expanded beyond <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>. Graduate training based on the German-inspired American model of specialized course work and the completion of a research thesis was introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The policy of university education initiated in the 1960s responded to population pressure and the belief that higher education was a key to social justice and <a href="/wiki/Economic_productivity" title="Economic productivity" class="mw-redirect">economic productivity</a> for individuals and for society.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Calgary launched its program in <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The University of Calgary <a href="/wiki/Tartan" title="Tartan">tartan</a> is associated with the university and with its pipe band.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The university's first <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a>, Herbert Stoker Armstrong, held a strong belief that <i>&quot;although the university is accountable to the society that supports it, the university must insist on playing a leadership role in intellectual matters if it is to be worthy of the name.&quot;</i><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>During the late 1960s, the University of Calgary saw a massive construction boom. Construction on campus included Blocks F and G of Calgary Hall, Blocks C and D of the Engineering Building, the lab of the Kananaskis Centre, a research, training, and service unit, and the opening of the new University <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">Theatre</a> in Calgary Hall. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Banff_Centre" title="Banff Centre">Banff Centre</a> (originally known as The Banff School of Fine Arts) became affiliated with the University of Calgary. The School of Social Welfare was also established. Total full-time enrolment in 1966 exceeded 4000 students and there were 355 academic members of staff.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The U of C is in the midst of its largest capital expansion ever. Fuelled by <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>?s nation-leading economic growth, the university has embarked upon a plan to add capacity for more students and a host of new teaching and research activities. These major developments, including the Taylor Family Digital Library (TFDL), the U of C downtown campus and the new Energy, Environment and Experiential Learning building, are fulfilling distinct academic strategies and creating progressive learning environments.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutUofC_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutUofC-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary's Child Development Centre is Alberta's first building designed and constructed to LEED (<a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a>) standards, the <a href="/wiki/North_American" title="North American" class="mw-redirect">North American</a> benchmark for environmentally high-performance buildings. The facility, which houses the university's second child-care facility and a full continuum of researchers, clinicians and frontline workers, is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Child_health" title="Child health" class="mw-redirect">child health</a>. The building officially opened in October, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutUofC_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutUofC-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Recently, an addition of rare <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurology</a> books dated over 500 years have been added to the MacKimmie library collection. The collection broadens the neuroscience educational experience and digitizing the collection enables people around the world to share in the resource. The collection includes an original copy of the 1953 Nature paper where Nobel winners <a href="/wiki/James_D._Watson" title="James D. Watson" class="mw-redirect">James D. Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a> first described the discovery of the double helix structure of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. It also includes the rare first neurological text written in the 1600s by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willis" title="Thomas Willis">Thomas Willis</a>, the man who coined the term neurology and is known as the ?father of neurology?. The collection is valued at approximately $600,000.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Canada?s fifth veterinary school, the University of Calgary Faculty of <a href="/wiki/Veterinary" title="Veterinary" class="mw-redirect">Veterinary</a> Medicine, was opened in mid-2008. Research and graduate education programs will be focused on areas of strength and importance in Alberta and will fall in line with the college?s areas of emphasis: production animal health, equine health, <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> and public health and investigative medicine. The first class of Doctor of Veterinary Medicine (DVM) students started classes in the fall of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Profile">Profile</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Profile">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The university offers 150 programs in <a href="/wiki/Post-secondary" title="Post-secondary" class="mw-redirect">post-secondary</a> <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> awarding <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">masters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> (<a href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD" class="mw-redirect">PhD</a>) degrees. The University of Calgary has developed a wide range of undergraduate and graduate programs.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The U of C also encourages multi-disciplinary programs, meaning students can combine their interest areas and create an education that suits them. The campus has an area of 2.13&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (200 <a href="/wiki/Hectares" title="Hectares" class="mw-redirect">hectares</a>)and hosts 15 faculties, 55 departments and 36 research institutes and centers (see <a href="/wiki/Canadian_university_scientific_research_organizations" title="Canadian university scientific research organizations">Canadian university scientific research organizations</a>). The teaching staff is 2,596. The university employs 2,777 management, professional and support staff. This puts the staff at 5,363, making it one of Calgary's largest employers.</p> 
     <p>With the economic boom in Alberta, the government has promised $4.5&nbsp;billion to post-secondary institutions in the province.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_Alliance">Urban Alliance</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Urban Alliance">edit</a>]</span></h4>

(October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its purpose is to deliver quality of life and qualified people to the city, province and county. Early innovations are helping reduce GHG, integrate <a href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant" class="mw-redirect">immigrant</a> newcomers, reshape urban form, reduce youth crime, adapt to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, create alternate energy, support seniors, increase disaster resilience, improve mobility, <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> and other aspects.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculties_and_college">Faculties and college</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Faculties and college">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Library_system">Library system</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Library system">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>MacKimmie library was the first library to open at the University of Calgary in 1966. Since then, 5 different library branches have been opened in order to provide students with a greater amount of literary choice in many subjects. In addition, the U of C will be the first library system in North America to contain a video game library.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Over 3.2&nbsp;million printed volumes combined with online access to more than 300,000 full-text electronic journals and more than 700 electronic databases are available at the U of C. As of 2006, the library system is the sixth largest, by the number of volumes held, in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The university library was rated 42nd in North America by the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Research_Libraries" title="Association of Research Libraries">Association of Research Libraries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialty_libraries">Specialty libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Specialty libraries">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The library system at the U of C consists of seven library branches:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Taylor Family Digital Library - contains the greatest share of the library system's printed volumes, as well as rare special items like a gaming collection. 
       <ul> 
        <li>Data Library</li> 
        <li>Canadian Architectural Archives</li> 
        <li>The Military Museums Library and Archives</li> 
        <li>Fine Arts and Visual Resources</li> 
       </ul> </li> 
      <li>High Density Library - located at the university's Spy Hill Campus, serves as a climate-controlled respository for lesser-used materials, any of which may be called-back for use as required.</li> 
      <li>Business Library 
       <ul> 
        <li>Virtual Business Library</li> 
       </ul> </li> 
      <li>Doucette Education Library</li> 
      <li>Bennett Jones Law Library 
       <ul> 
        <li>Virtual Law Library</li> 
       </ul> </li> 
      <li>Health Sciences Library</li> 
      <li>Gallagher Library of Geology and Geophysics</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The Taylor Family Digital Library replaced, as the university's primary facility, the MacKimmie Library, which is no longer a library.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Taylor_Family_Digital_Library">Taylor Family Digital Library</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Taylor Family Digital Library">edit</a>]</span></h4> 
     <p>The Taylor Family Digital Library (TFDL) is an innovative convergence of libraries, historical archives, arts museum, scholarly publishing, and student academic support services. The TFDL was officially launched on October 20, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The TFDL will allow the full re-engineering of the university's library system, creating more and better space for teaching and learning resources, while moving the majority of U of C's growing collection off campus to a high-density library.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, The TFDL will offer books and online resources, a large Learning Commons with caf&eacute;, workrooms, film and audio rooms, editing and recording suites, multimedia labs, quiet study areas, and seminar and consultation space for academic growth.</p> 
     <p>Don and Ruth Taylor, longtime friends and supporters of the University of Calgary, donated $25&nbsp;million in December, 2006 to help build the new digital library. In recognition of this generous gift, the Board of Governors of the University named the library the Taylor Family Digital Library (TFDL).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In addition, the gift from the Taylor Family will be used to create the Taylor Quadrangle, a green space in the centre of campus adjacent to the TFDL. Such quadrangles often serve as iconic meeting places for the entire university community, for both informal and formal gatherings.</p>

     <p><i>Webometrics University Rankings</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> which ranks universities on their presence on the Internet, ranks the University of Calgary 70th in the USA and Canada category and 93rd in the world. It is ranked 6th in Canada.</p> 
     <p><i>Research Infosource</i> ranks the top 50 research universities in Canada each year. In its 2009 ranking Calgary was 6th.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><i><a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a></i> ranks the university 7-8 in the national category.<sup id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_ARWU_CAN_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_ARWU_CAN-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Calgary's <a href="/wiki/Haskayne_School_of_Business" title="Haskayne School of Business">Haskayne School of Business</a> has for years dominated the <a href="/wiki/Inter-Collegiate_Business_Competition" title="Inter-Collegiate Business Competition">Inter-Collegiate Business Competition</a> (I.C.B.C.), hosted annually by Queen's University. For much of the competition's lifetime, the teams representing Haskayne have come out on top each year by winning first in the majority of the competition's case study categories, including accounting, business policy, debating, ethics, finance, human resources, marketing, and MIS.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> ranked Haskayne's MBA program 3rd within Canada, 43rd in North America, and 82nd worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary ranks 8th in the medical-doctoral category of <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> annual university rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the rankings have been met with criticism. The University of Calgary and other universities have argued that Maclean's Magazine takes data out of context and is an inaccurate reflection of performance. In 2006, 21 Canadian universities along with the University of Calgary, many being part of the leading group of research universities known as the <a href="/wiki/U15_(universities)" title="U15 (universities)" class="mw-redirect">U15</a>, opted out of the rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Other universities opting out in 2006 included <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carleton_University" title="Carleton University">Carleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalhousie_University" title="Dalhousie University">Dalhousie</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lethbridge" title="University of Lethbridge">Lethbridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manitoba" title="University of Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/McMaster_University" title="McMaster University">McMaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Universit&eacute; de Montr&eacute;al">Montr&eacute;al</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ottawa" title="University of Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Fraser_University" title="Simon Fraser University">Simon Fraser</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">Toronto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_university" title="Queen's university" class="mw-redirect">Queen's</a>.</p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary is ranked 17th in QS InfoSource's<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> ranking of the world's universities founded since 1962.</p> 
     <p>In the category, for 2013, of the top 100 universities under the age of 50 years in service, the University of Calgary is ranked 23rd in the world, according to <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education" title="Times Higher Education">Times Higher Education</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

In October 2008, the University of Calgary was named one of &quot;Canada's Top 100 Employers&quot; by Mediacorp Canada Inc., and was featured in <a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean</a>'s newsmagazine. Later that month, the university was also named one of Alberta's Top Employers.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The Globe and Mail's University Report Card reflects the opinions of 25,000 current undergraduates who responded to some 100 questions about their respective universities.      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grounds">Grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Grounds">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_Campus">Main Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Main Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The university grounds lie about 10 kilometres (6.2&nbsp;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Calgary" title="Downtown Calgary">Downtown Calgary</a>, and immediately south of the neighbourhoods of Varsity and Dalhousie. The U of C campus occupies 213 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> (530&nbsp;acres) or 2.13&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (0.82&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi), an area larger than Calgary?s entire downtown. The campus is bounded by Crowchild Trail, 16 Avenue NW, 32 Avenue NW and Shaganappi Trail. 24 Avenue NW provides direct access to the interior of the campus.<sup id="cite_ref-ucalgary.ca_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucalgary.ca-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The architecture is defined by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival" class="mw-redirect">Gothic Revival</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brutalist" title="Brutalist" class="mw-redirect">Brutalist</a> and Postmodern buildings spread across campus, most of them dated between 1905 and 1980. The dramatic effect of this blended design of different architecture has drawn a considerable amount of praise.<sup id="cite_ref-globecampus.ca_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globecampus.ca-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A light rail transit link is provided to the university by the <a href="/wiki/C-Train" title="C-Train">C-Train</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_(C-Train)" title="University (C-Train)">LRT station</a>.</p> 
     <p>A large <a href="/wiki/Park" title="Park">park</a> is built in the center of the main campus, and is home to the infamous &quot;Prairie Chicken&quot; sculpture. In addition, a <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">pond</a> surrounded by benches and an open field is available for students to relax at. The park contains old <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees which were specially transplanted to the location when the university was opened. Together, they form a considerable amount of tree canopies which add a more natural feeling to the campus.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Campus">West Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: West Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In 1995, the Province of Alberta gave the University of Calgary a large tract of land west of the Main Campus with the understanding it would be used in the future to advance the University?s mission, vision, values and priorities. About a third of the size of downtown Calgary, the land overlooks the Bow River valley and covers 80 hectares (198&nbsp;acres) east of Shaganappi Trail between 16th Avenue (TransCanada Highway) and 32 Avenue N.W. The western campus lies on mostly hilly terrain, near the Bow River. It is adjacent to the main campus of the university, and is home to the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Children%27s_Hospital" title="Alberta Children's Hospital">Alberta Children's Hospital</a> and Child Development Centre. With the recent boost in annual funding, the U of C has begun a development plan in order to make better use of the remaining space.</p> 
     <p>Five years ago, part of the West Campus was leased for the new Alberta Children?s Hospital and a new <a href="/wiki/Ronald_McDonald" title="Ronald McDonald">Ronald McDonald</a> House. So far, the campus is only halfway developed and is considered building room for the future. From this location, it is possible to see the entire university and also <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Calgary" title="Downtown Calgary">Downtown Calgary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ucalgary.ca_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucalgary.ca-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Downtown_Campus">Downtown Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Downtown Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>On September 13, 2010, the University of Calgary opened its new downtown campus located at 8th Avenue and 8th Street SW. The first phase is currently open, as well as a second phase containing a bookstore, library, retail space and additional learning spaces. The building houses classes including Continuing Education, and energy &amp; environment, as well as classes offered by the Haskayne School of Business and the new U of C School of Public Policy.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qatar_Campus">Qatar Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Qatar Campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In 2007, the University of Calgary established a campus in <a href="/wiki/Doha" title="Doha">Doha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, the University of Calgary - Qatar, which currently focuses upon nursing education. The University of Calgary-Qatar offers a flexible, innovative, Bachelor of <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">Nursing</a> (BN) degree to prepare students for the opportunities and challenges of a rewarding nursing career. Throughout the program, students are provided with a theoretical base and supervised clinical experience in a variety of nursing practice settings.</p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary offers two routes towards a Bachelor of Nursing (BN) degree:<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A Regular Track program for high school - <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> transfer students (BNRT)</li> 
      <li>A Post-Diploma program for students with an acceptable Nursing diploma (PDBN)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In addition, several graduate programs are expected to be phased in.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Dinos" title="Calgary Dinos">Calgary Dinos</a> are the athletic teams that represent the University of Calgary in the <a href="/wiki/Canada_West_Universities_Athletic_Association" title="Canada West Universities Athletic Association">Canada West</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Interuniversity_Sport" title="Canadian Interuniversity Sport">Canadian Interuniversity Sport</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Colleges_Athletic_Conference" title="Alberta Colleges Athletic Conference" class="mw-redirect">Alberta Colleges Athletic Conference</a>. They were known as the &quot;Dinosaurs&quot; but usually referred to as the &quot;Dinos&quot; until 1999, when the name was officially shortened. Some of its venues are the <a href="/wiki/Jack_Simpson_Gymnasium" title="Jack Simpson Gymnasium">Jack Simpson Gymnasium</a> (basketball, volleyball), <a href="/wiki/McMahon_Stadium" title="McMahon Stadium">McMahon Stadium</a> (football, soccer), <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Oval" title="Olympic Oval">Olympic Oval</a> (speed skating), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawkings_Field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hawkings Field (page does not exist)">Hawkings Field</a> (field hockey), <a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary_Aquatic_Centre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="University of Calgary Aquatic Centre (page does not exist)">University of Calgary Aquatic Centre</a> (swimming, often shortened to Aquatic Centre) and a 200m Running Track (cross-country and track &amp; field practice).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The Dinos compete in 12 varsity sports: <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross-country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer" class="mw-redirect">soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_wrestling" title="Amateur wrestling">wrestling</a>. The Dinos also have 2 club teams: Men's Baseball and Men's Rugby.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The men's hockey team plays at <a href="/wiki/Father_David_Bauer_Olympic_Arena" title="Father David Bauer Olympic Arena">Father David Bauer Olympic Arena</a>, while the women's hockey team's schedule is split between the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Oval" title="Olympic Oval">Olympic Oval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Father_David_Bauer_Olympic_Arena" title="Father David Bauer Olympic Arena">Father David Bauer Olympic Arena</a>. In the rare case of scheduling conflicts, both men's and women's hockey have used the <a href="/wiki/Max_Bell_Centre" title="Max Bell Centre">Max Bell Centre</a> for games.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary has been associated with the <a href="/wiki/Olympics" title="Olympics" class="mw-redirect">Olympics</a> since 1976, when the 10-year-old athletic department sent three athletes to Montreal. Since then, 42 Dinos athletes have competed at both the summer and winter Games, bringing home 11 medals, and UC hosted the athletes' village and speed skating events at the <a href="/wiki/XV_Olympic_Winter_Games" title="XV Olympic Winter Games" class="mw-redirect">XV Olympic Winter Games</a> in 1988.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The football team plays home matches at <a href="/wiki/McMahon_Stadium" title="McMahon Stadium">McMahon Stadium</a>, home of CFL's <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampeders" title="Calgary Stampeders">Calgary Stampeders</a>. It has won the <a href="/wiki/Vanier_Cup" title="Vanier Cup">Vanier Cup</a> on four occasions, 1983, 1985, 1988 and 1995.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Adopt-a-Dino is a university initiative to provide athletes with monetary assistance by allowing participating members to &quot;adopt&quot; an athlete. All Adopt-A-Dino donors will be recognized in the following season's ROAR Magazine, in addition to receiving: Two all-sport season passes valid for all Dinos regular season home games, a plaque-mounted photo of your chosen athlete or team, an invitation to a free event to meet an interact with your chosen team, and a tax receipt for $890.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a>]</span></h2>

UToday is the University of Calgary's E-newsletter while OnCampus is the campus newspaper.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. It currently publishes every Thursday throughout the year.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/CJSW" title="CJSW" class="mw-redirect">CJSW</a> is the university's campus radio station, broadcasting at 90.9&nbsp;MHz FM. CJSW is a member of the National Campus and Community Radio Association<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> and the University of Calgary Tri-Media Alliance in partnership with <a href="/wiki/NUTV_New_University_Television_at_the_University_of_Calgary" title="NUTV New University Television at the University of Calgary" class="mw-redirect">NUTV</a> (the campus television station) and The Gauntlet (the campus newspaper).CJSW is a non-profit society maintained and operated by a group of four staff members and over 200 volunteers drawn from both the University of Calgary student body and the wider city of Calgary population. CJSW broadcasts music, spoken word and multicultural programming.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>In addition to the FM broadcast, the station can be heard at 106.9&nbsp;MHz cable FM, and via Ogg Vorbis stream from its web site. Select shows are also available for <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcast</a> download.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/NUTV_New_University_Television_at_the_University_of_Calgary" title="NUTV New University Television at the University of Calgary" class="mw-redirect">NUTV</a> is one of the oldest university-based television production societies in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Established in 1983 and incorporated in 1991, NUTV is a campus-based non-profit organization. NUTV offers the opportunity to University of Calgary students and community members to explore the medium of television by learning the various stages of production.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> This includes reporting/interviewing; hosting; writing; camera operation; lighting; sound mixing; Final Cut Pro &amp; Adobe Creative Suite editing; producing; and directing.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> NUTV is part of the University of Calgary Tri-Media Alliance, comprising print The Gauntlet, radio <a href="/wiki/CJSW-FM" title="CJSW-FM">CJSW 90.9</a>, and television (NUTV). The University of Calgary is unique in that it is the only Canadian university that houses three media operations on-campus.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_publishing">Book publishing</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Book publishing">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Calgary_Press" title="University of Calgary Press">University of Calgary Press</a> was founded in 1981 and to date has published over 280 titles.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> Special emphasis is placed on three areas: works concerning the geographic regions spanning the <a href="/wiki/Canadian" title="Canadian" class="mw-redirect">Canadian</a> Northwest and the <a href="/wiki/American_West" title="American West" class="mw-redirect">American West</a>; innovative and experimental works that challenge the established canons, subjects and formats, with special interest in art and architecture; and internationally focused manuscripts with particular attention to Latin America, World Heritage Sites, international relations and public policy.</p>

     <p>The University of Calgary offers a wide range of residences on campus as a significant proportion of undergraduate students reside on the campus.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/residence" class="extiw" title="wikt:residence">residence</a> buildings on campus house 2200<sup id="cite_ref-International_House_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-International_House-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> students, situated in eight buildings named after mountains in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Canadian Rockies</a>. The two traditional buildings are Rundle Hall and Kananaskis Hall<sup id="cite_ref-Building_Names_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Building_Names-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> and were built in the early 1960s when the university relocated to its present campus. Five newer buildings named Glacier, Olympus, Norquay, Brewster, and Castle Halls<sup id="cite_ref-Building_Names_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Building_Names-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> were built prior to the <a href="/wiki/1988_Winter_Olympics" title="1988 Winter Olympics">1988 Winter Olympics</a> as the athletes? <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Village" title="Olympic Village">Olympic Village</a>. However, each is smaller than the traditional buildings, being three or four stories tall and housing 10 to 30 students on each floor. One of the newest, Cascade Hall, is five stories and is the third largest residence building, its floors being able to house more students. The newest six buildings are all designed in the style of apartments with a hallway on each floor with sets of rooms that can accommodate up to four people each. This is in contrast to the traditional buildings which have hallways on each floor, each having rooms accommodating two, along with a common area at the centre of the building on each floor.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Yamnuska Hall opens September 2011 to upper year, international, and transfer students. The two and three bedroom suites are designed to ensure that students experience residence life by sharing space, but also ensures that each student has a private room.<sup id="cite_ref-Yamnuska_Hall_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yamnuska_Hall-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A new building, <i>Dr. Fok Ying Tung International House</i> houses 200 <a href="/wiki/International" title="International">international</a> students, instructors and conference attendees. In addition, a new residence for first year undergraduate students is due to be completed in 2011. Construction of the new residence at the University of Calgary broke ground Friday, May 15, 2009. Scheduled to open in spring 2011, the 596-bed residence is part of the university?s ongoing commitment to students? academic success and personal growth. Through innovative <a href="/wiki/Building_design" title="Building design">building design</a> and construction, the residence will meet current expectations for student housing and will help support institutional recruitment and retention objectives. This is part of the university's $1.5&nbsp;billion capital program. With the completion of this new building the number of beds on campus will increase to 3000.<sup id="cite_ref-International_House_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-International_House-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Efforts are being made to preserve green space wherever possible, which includes retaining the green belt fronting 24 Avenue N.W. and transplanting five mature <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees to the front of the Dr. Fok Ying Tung International House this fall. A new site <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a> plan is also being prepared, which involves planting replacement trees and creating additional recreational green space on the grounds of the new residence.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a>]</span></h3>

      <li>Bermuda Shorts Day (BSD): An annual end-of-school student celebration. Organized and run by the Students' Union, BSD occurs on the last day of classes. Students' wear Bermuda style shorts and participate in a variety of informal pre- and post-BSD activities including couch racing (recently banned by the university administration over safety concerns), the beer mile, and breakfast parties. The official event is an outdoor fenced in party with live music and beer garden.</li>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership_on_campus">Leadership on campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Leadership on campus">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In 2009, the University of Calgary's Office of the Student Experience (now the Student Success Centre) launched their own co-curricular record, the first of its kind in western Canadian universities.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> The co-curricular record is an official university document to be coupled with a student's academic transcript, that recognizes out-of-classroom experiences that are still connected to the university. The Student Success Centre (SSC). The SSC offers programs and services to support students in creating: (1) Academic Success, (2) Personal Success, and (3) Career Success. The SSC offers Orientation Programs, Personalized Career Planning Sessions, Career and Life Workshops, Leadership Programs, the First-Year Experience Program, the Senior-Year Experience Program, Arts &amp; Science Program Advising, Learning Support Services, and Writing Support Services.</p> 
     <p>In April 2011, the University of Calgary launched the Scholars Academy Program: a program for bright, dynamic students that excel beyond just mere academics. Students in the Scholars Academy Program are leaders in the community, show enthusiasm toward learning, compassion and integrity toward others, and inspire those around them. In addition to the formation of a community of high-achieving peers and success student perks, a dedicated academic development specialist provides one-on-one support with scholarship and post-degree applications, as well as with each student's overall academic plan. This support enables students to compete and win prestigious awards and scholarships, and leads to even greater opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Calgary also offers the President's Award for Excellence in Student Leadership to five graduating students (undergraduate or graduate) that represent the best in academic achievement in addition to outstanding extracurricular contributions to the university and community.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarships_and_Awards">Scholarships and Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Scholarships and Awards">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The University of Calgary offers many scholarships, awards, and bursaries to students. For detailed information, visit the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ucalgary.ca/studentfinance/">Office of Student Awards and Financial Aid</a>.</p> 
     <p>A notable high school level scholarship is the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Rutherford" title="Alexander Rutherford" class="mw-redirect">Alexander Rutherford</a> Scholarship which was introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Alberta" title="Government of Alberta" class="mw-redirect">Government of Alberta</a> in 1980. The Alexander Rutherford scholarship is to recognize and reward exceptional academic achievement at the senior <a href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> level and to encourage students to continue their studies. To be considered for these scholarships, students must be a <a href="/wiki/Canadian_citizen" title="Canadian citizen" class="mw-redirect">Canadian citizen</a> or permanent resident who plan to enroll or are enrolled in a full-time post-secondary program of at least one <a href="/wiki/Semester" title="Semester" class="mw-redirect">semester</a> in length.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University joined Project Hero, a scholarship program cofounded by General (Ret'd) <a href="/wiki/Rick_Hillier" title="Rick Hillier">Rick Hillier</a>, for the families of fallen <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> members.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> Dependents of Canadian Forces personnel killed while serving in active military missions will have the support of the University of Calgary to complete <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect">undergraduate</a> degrees.</p> 
     <p>The office of the Chancellor and Senate offers many scholarships, awards, and bursaries to University of Calgary students who demonstrate outstanding academic achievement and exceptional service to the internal and external community.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Top students in the Schulich School of Engineering are recognized as Schulich Scholars and are awarded prestigious Schulich Entrance Scholarships.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> The first cohort of Schulich Scholars graduated in 2010-2011.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Students enrolled in the Bachelor of Health Sciences Program are eligible for the O'Brien Centre Continuing Scholarship, which supports full-time students dedicated to extra-curricular involvement and the community on top of their academics.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University also offers student awards for academic and leadership excellence. The two biggest awards a student can receive are the<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> President?s Award for Excellence in Student Leadership and the<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Arch Future Alumni Award.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aboriginal">Aboriginal</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Aboriginal">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The University of Calgary recognizes Aboriginal students, and has instituted an Aboriginal Admissions Policy [section A.13] and Aboriginal Student Access Program (ASAP) [section A.14], as dictated in the Undergraduate Admissions section of the annual calendar. Any student with Aboriginal ancestry (First Nations, Inuit, Metis) and legal status may apply and be considered under the policy. More information can be obtained online at the University of Calgary's website.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_the_University_of_Calgary">Order of the University of Calgary</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Calgary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Order of the University of Calgary">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The Order of the University of Calgary, developed in 1994 resulting from a suggestion brought forward from the University's Senate, honours worthy recipients who have a record of exemplary and <a href="/wiki/Distinguished" title="Distinguished" class="mw-redirect">distinguished</a> service to the University. Individuals who have been admitted into the Order have included faculty, staff, students, alumni and volunteers. Young aboriginal leaders such as Spencer Saurette have caused an increased awareness in aboriginal heritage and traditions on campus.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> It is available to any member of the University community, those currently or formerly attached to the University and to those representing the University in the community. Candidates nominated for membership in the Order may include, but are not limited to, current or former faculty, staff, students (graduate or undergraduate), <a href="/wiki/Alumni" title="Alumni" class="mw-redirect">alumni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">volunteers</a>.</p> 
     <p>The Order of the University of Calgary includes numerous personalities of note including the likes of <a href="/wiki/Eldon_Smith" title="Eldon Smith">Dr. Eldon R. Smith</a>. As of November 2009, a total of 114 individuals had been admitted into the Order.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
