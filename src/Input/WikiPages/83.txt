     <p>The <b>University of California, Santa Cruz</b> (also known as <b>UC Santa Cruz</b> or <b>UCSC</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a>, <a href="/wiki/Residential_college" title="Residential college">collegiate</a> university and one of 10 campuses in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> system. Located 80 miles (130&nbsp;km) south of San Francisco at the edge of the coastal community of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz</a>, the campus lies on 2,001 acres (809.8&nbsp;ha)<sup id="cite_ref-UCSC_Acreage_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCSC_Acreage-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> of rolling, forested hills overlooking the Pacific Ocean and <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay" title="Monterey Bay">Monterey Bay</a>.</p> 
     <p>Founded in 1965,<sup id="cite_ref-overview_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> UC Santa Cruz began as a showcase for progressive, cross-disciplinary undergraduate education, innovative teaching methods and contemporary architecture. Since then, it has evolved into a modern research university with a wide variety of both undergraduate and graduate programs, while retaining its reputation for strong undergraduate support and student political activism. The residential college system, which consists of ten small colleges, is intended to combine the student support of a small college with the resources of a major university.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Although some of the original founders had already outlined plans for an institution like UCSC as early as the 1930s, the opportunity to realize their vision did not present itself until the City of Santa Cruz made a bid to the <a href="/wiki/Regents_of_the_University_of_California" title="Regents of the University of California">University of California Regents</a> in the mid-1950s to build a campus just outside town, in the foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Mountains" title="Santa Cruz Mountains">Santa Cruz Mountains</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-City_council_hasn.27t_told_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-City_council_hasn.27t_told-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The Santa Cruz site was selected over a competing proposal to build the campus closer to the population center of <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>. Santa Cruz was selected for the beauty, rather than the practicality, of its location, however, and its remoteness led to the decision to develop a residential college system that would house most of the students on-campus.<sup id="cite_ref-McHenry_UCSC_origins_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McHenry_UCSC_origins-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The formal design process of the Santa Cruz campus began in the late 1950s, culminating in the Long Range Development Plan of 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-1963_LRDP_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1963_LRDP-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Construction had started by 1964, and the University was able to accommodate its first students (albeit living in trailers on what is now the East Field athletic area) in 1965. The campus was intended to be a showcase for contemporary architecture, progressive teaching methods, and undergraduate research.<sup id="cite_ref-UC_Regents_history_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UC_Regents_history-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stadtman_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtman-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burchyns_45_years_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burchyns_45_years-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> According to founding chancellor <a href="/wiki/Dean_McHenry" title="Dean McHenry">Dean McHenry</a>, the purpose of the distributed college system was to combine the benefits of a major <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university" class="mw-redirect">research university</a> with the intimacy of a smaller college.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burchyns_future_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burchyns_future-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> UC President <a href="/wiki/Clark_Kerr" title="Clark Kerr">Clark Kerr</a> shared a passion with former <a href="/wiki/Stanford" title="Stanford" class="mw-redirect">Stanford</a> roommate McHenry to build a university modeled as &quot;several <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmores</a>&quot; (i.e., small <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_college" title="Liberal arts college">liberal arts colleges</a>) in close proximity to each other.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Roads on campus were named after <a href="/wiki/Regents_of_the_University_of_California" title="Regents of the University of California">UC Regents</a> who voted in favor of building the campus.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_Santa_Cruz">Impact on Santa Cruz</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact on Santa Cruz">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Although the city of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz</a> already exhibited a strong <a href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" title="Conservation ethic" class="mw-redirect">conservation ethic</a> before the founding of the university, the coincidental rise of the <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture of the 1960s</a> with the university's establishment fundamentally altered its subsequent development. Early student and faculty activism at UCSC pioneered an approach to environmentalism that greatly impacted the industrial development of the surrounding area.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The lowering of the voting age to 18 in 1971 led to the emergence of a powerful student-voting bloc.<sup id="cite_ref-Burchins3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burchins3-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> A large and growing population of politically <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">liberal</a> UCSC <a href="/wiki/Alumnus" title="Alumnus">alumni</a> changed the electorate of the town from predominantly <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> to markedly <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-leaning</a>, consistently voting against expansion measures on the part of both <a href="/wiki/Town_and_gown" title="Town and gown">town and gown</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Rotkin" title="Mike Rotkin">Mike Rotkin</a>, UCSC alumnus, lecturer in <a href="/wiki/Community_studies" title="Community studies">Community Studies</a>, and self-described '<a href="/wiki/Socialist_feminism" title="Socialist feminism">socialist-feminist</a>,' has served as Mayor of Santa Cruz several times.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_plans">Expansion plans</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion plans">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Range_Development_Plan_(UCSC)" title="Long Range Development Plan (UCSC)">Long Range Development Plan (UCSC)</a>
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Long_Range_Development_Plan_(UCSC)" title="Long Range Development Plan (UCSC)">Plans</a> for increasing enrollment over the next 14 years to 19,500 students, adding 1,500 faculty and staff, and, secondarily the anticipated environmental impacts of such action encountered opposition from the city, the local community, and the student body.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> City voters in 2006 passed two measures calling on UCSC to pay for the impacts of campus growth. A Santa Cruz Superior Court judge invalidated the measures, ruling they were improperly put on the ballot. In 2008, the university, city, county and neighborhood organizations reached an agreement to set aside numerous lawsuits and allow the expansion to occur. UCSC agreed to local government scrutiny of its north campus expansion plans, to provide housing for 67 percent of the additional students on campus, and to pay municipal development and water fees.<sup id="cite_ref-bookwalter_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bookwalter-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>George Blumenthal, UCSC's 10th Chancellor, intends to mitigate growth constraints in Santa Cruz by developing off-campus sites in <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>. The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Ames_Research_Center" title="NASA Ames Research Center" class="mw-redirect">NASA Ames Research Center</a> campus is planned to ultimately hold 2,000 UCSC students – about 10% of the entire university's future student body as envisioned for 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In April 2010, UC Santa Cruz opened its new $35 million Digital Arts Research Center; a project in planning since 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a>]</span></h2>

     <p>The 6,088-acre (2,464&nbsp;ha) UCSC campus is located 75 miles (121&nbsp;km) south of San Francisco, in the Ben Lomond Mountain ridge of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Mountains" title="Santa Cruz Mountains">Santa Cruz Mountains</a>. Elevation varies from 285 feet (87&nbsp;m) at the campus entrance to 1,195 feet (364&nbsp;m) at the northern boundary, a difference of about 900 feet (270&nbsp;m). The southern portion of the campus primarily consists of a large, open <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadow</a>, locally known as the Great Meadow. To the north of the meadow lie most of the campus' buildings, many of them among <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">redwood</a> groves. The campus is bounded on the south by the city's upper-west-side neighborhoods, on the east by Harvey West Park<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Pogonip_(park)" title="Pogonip (park)" class="mw-redirect">Pogonip open space preserve</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> on the north by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cowell_Redwoods_State_Park" title="Henry Cowell Redwoods State Park">Henry Cowell Redwoods State Park</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> near the town of <a href="/wiki/Felton,_California" title="Felton, California">Felton</a>, and on the west by <a href="/wiki/Gray_Whale_Ranch" title="Gray Whale Ranch" class="mw-redirect">Gray Whale Ranch</a>, a portion of <a href="/wiki/Wilder_Ranch_State_Park" title="Wilder Ranch State Park">Wilder Ranch State Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The campus is built on a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Cowell_Lime_Works" title="Cowell Lime Works">Cowell Family ranch</a>, which was purchased by the University of California in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The northern half of the campus property has remained in its undeveloped, forested state apart from fire roads and hiking and bicycle trails. The heavily-forested area has allowed UC Santa Cruz to operate a <a href="/wiki/RV_park" title="RV park">recreational vehicle park</a> as a form of student housing.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Shrines" title="Shrines" class="mw-redirect">shrines</a>, dens and other student-built curiosities are scattered around the northern campus. These structures, mostly assembled from branches and other forest detritus, were formerly concentrated in the area known as Elfland, a glen the University razed in 1992 to build colleges Nine and Ten. Students were able to relocate and save some of the structures, however.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Creeks traverse the UCSC campus within several ravines. Footbridges span those ravines on pedestrian paths linking various areas of campus. The footbridges make it possible to walk to any part of campus within 20 minutes in spite of the campus being built on a mountainside with varying elevations.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> At night, orange lights illuminate the occasionally fogged-in paths.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are a number of natural points of interest throughout the UCSC grounds. The &quot;Porter Caves&quot; are a popular site among students on the west side of campus. The entrance is located in the forest between the <a href="/wiki/Porter_College" title="Porter College">Porter College</a> meadow and Empire Grade Road. The caves wind through a set of caverns, some of which are challenging, narrow passages. Tree Nine is another popular destination for students. A large <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Fir" title="Douglas Fir" class="mw-redirect">Douglas Fir</a> spanning approximately 103 feet (31&nbsp;m) tall, Tree Nine is located in the upper campus of UCSC behind <a href="/wiki/College_Nine" title="College Nine">College Nine</a>. The tree had been a popular climbing spot for many years but due to environmental corrosion and fear of student injuries, UC ground services sawed off the limbs to make it nearly impossible to climb.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> For specific directions, reference: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.wikihow.com/Find-Tree-Nine-at-the-University-of-California-in-Santa-Cruz">directions to Tree Nine</a> For the less experienced tree-climber, students also frequent Sunset Tree located on the east side of the meadow behind the UCSC Music Center.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The UCSC campus is also one of the few homes to <a href="/wiki/Mima_Mounds" title="Mima Mounds" class="mw-redirect">Mima Mounds</a> in the United States. They are extremely rare in the United States and in the world in general.<br style="clear:both;" /></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>The university offers 63 undergraduate majors and 35 minors, with graduate programs in 33 fields.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Popular undergraduate majors include Art, <a href="/wiki/Business_management" title="Business management" class="mw-redirect">Business Management Economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">Molecular and Cell Biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">Psychology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Interdisciplinary programs, such as Feminist Studies, American Studies, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_studies" title="Environmental studies">Environmental Studies</a>, Visual Studies, Digital Arts and New Media, and the unique <a href="/wiki/History_of_Consciousness" title="History of Consciousness">History of Consciousness</a> Department are also hosted alongside UCSC's more traditional academic departments.</p> 
     <p>In an effort to cut $13 million, as required by the University of California office of the President and Board of Regents in a decision to cut 10% from the budget of each campus, UCSC nearly eliminated its longstanding and sometimes controversial undergraduate major Community Studies in 2009. The interdisciplinary major continues to be offered, though cuts to department staff raised concern by students that the curriculum has already been seriously damaged. Community Studies and other majors within the Social Sciences division, including some graduate programs, remain threatened by more cuts expected in the near future.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Although designed as a liberal arts-oriented university, UCSC quickly acquired a graduate-level <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a> research component with the appointment of plant physiologist <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_V._Thimann" title="Kenneth V. Thimann">Kenneth V. Thimann</a> as the first provost of <a href="/wiki/Crown_College,_University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="Crown College, University of California, Santa Cruz">Crown College</a>. Thimann developed UCSC's early Division of Natural Sciences and recruited other well-known science faculty and graduate students to the fledgling campus.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Immediately upon its founding, UCSC was also granted administrative responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick Observatory</a>, which established the campus as a major center for <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> research.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Founding members of the Social Science and Humanities faculty created the unique <a href="/wiki/History_of_Consciousness" title="History of Consciousness">History of Consciousness</a> graduate program in UCSC's first year of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>UCSC's <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Agroecology_%26_Sustainable_Food_Systems" title="Center for Agroecology &amp; Sustainable Food Systems">organic farm and garden program</a> is the oldest in the country, and pioneered <a href="/wiki/Organic_horticulture" title="Organic horticulture">organic horticulture</a> techniques internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, UCSC's faculty and emeriti include 13 members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences" class="mw-redirect">National Academy of Sciences</a>, 20 members of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, and 28 members of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-overview_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The young <a href="/wiki/Baskin_School_of_Engineering" title="Baskin School of Engineering" class="mw-redirect">Baskin School of Engineering</a>, UCSC's first professional school and home to the <a href="/wiki/Expressive_Intelligence_Studio" title="Expressive Intelligence Studio">Expressive Intelligence Studio</a>, and the Center for Biomolecular Science and Engineering<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> are gaining recognition, as has the work that UCSC researchers <a href="/wiki/David_Haussler" title="David Haussler">David Haussler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Kent" title="Jim Kent">Jim Kent</a> have done on the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> including the widely used <a href="/wiki/UCSC_Genome_Browser" title="UCSC Genome Browser">UCSC Genome Browser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC administers the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>'s Center for Adaptive Optics.<sup id="cite_ref-CAO_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAO-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Off-campus research facilities maintained by UCSC include the <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick</a> and <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">Keck</a> Observatories and the <a href="/wiki/Long_Marine_Laboratory" title="Long Marine Laboratory">Long Marine Laboratory</a>. In September 2003, a ten-year task order contract valued at more than $330 million was awarded by <a href="/wiki/NASA_Ames_Research_Center" title="NASA Ames Research Center" class="mw-redirect">NASA Ames Research Center</a> to the University of California to establish and operate a University Affiliated Research System (UARC). UCSC manages the UARC for the University of California.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In 2012, UC Santa Cruz was ranked 77th in the list of Best National Universities in the United States by the <i><a href="/wiki/US_News_%26_World_Reports" title="US News &amp; World Reports" class="mw-redirect">US News &amp; World Reports</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and 67th best by <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Monthly" title="Washington Monthly">Washington Monthly</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Washington_Monthly_ranking_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Washington_Monthly_ranking-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2013 <i><a href="/wiki/Kiplinger" title="Kiplinger">Kiplinger</a></i> ranked UC Santa Cruz 54th out of the top 100 best-value public colleges and universities in the nation, and 8th in California.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012–2013, UC Santa Cruz was rated 122nd in the world by <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings"><i>Times Higher Education</i> World University Rankings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 it was placed in the #101-150 tier of best universities in the world by the <a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a><sup id="cite_ref-Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities_-_2012_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities_-_2012-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> and #311 worldwide by the <i><a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2009, RePEc, an online database of research economics articles, ranked the UCSC Economics Department sixth in the world in the field of international finance.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2007, <i>High Times</i> magazine placed UCSC as first among US universities as a &quot;counterculture college.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princeton_Review" title="The Princeton Review">The Princeton Review</a></i> (with <i><a href="/wiki/Gamepro" title="Gamepro" class="mw-redirect">Gamepro</a></i> magazine) ranked UC Santa Cruz's Game Design major among the top 50 in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011, <i>The Princeton Review</i> and <i>Gamepro Media</i> ranked UC Santa Cruz's graduate programs in Game Design as seventh in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, UCSC was ranked No. 3 in the Most Beautiful Campus list of <i>Princeton Review</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The undergraduate program, with only the partial exception of those majors run through the University's School of Engineering, is still based on the version of the &quot;<a href="/wiki/Residential_college" title="Residential college">residential college system</a>&quot; outlined by Clark Kerr and Dean McHenry at the inception of their original plans for the campus (see <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz#History" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">History</a>, above). Upon admission, all undergraduate students have the opportunity to choose one of ten colleges, with which they usually stay affiliated for their entire undergraduate careers.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> There are cases where some students switch college affiliations as each college holds a different graduation ceremony. Almost all faculty members are affiliated with a college as well.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> The individual colleges provide housing and dining services, while the university as a whole offers courses and majors to the general student community.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Other universities with similar college systems include <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Diego" title="University of California, San Diego">University of California, San Diego</a>.</p> 
     <p>Each of the colleges has its own, distinctive architectural style and a resident faculty <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">provost</a>, who is the nominal head of his or her college.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> An incoming first-year student will take a mandatory &quot;core course&quot; within his or her respective college, with a curriculum and central theme unique to that college.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> College resident populations vary from about 750 to 1,550 students, with roughly half of undergraduates living on campus within their college community or in smaller, intramural campus communities such as the International Living Center, the Trailer Park, and the Village.<sup id="cite_ref-collegesection_62-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegesection-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Coursework, academic majors and general areas of study are not limited by college membership, although colleges host the offices of many academic departments. Graduate students are not affiliated with a residential college, though a large portion of their offices, too, have historically tended to be based in the colleges.      <p>For the fall 2012 term, UCSC offered admission to 20,142 freshmen out of 33,148 applicants, an acceptance rate of 60.8%.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The Fall 2012 entering freshman class had an average high school GPA of 3.59,<sup id="cite_ref-planning.ucsc.edu_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planning.ucsc.edu-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> an average ACT Composite score of 26,<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and an average combined SAT score of 1639.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC hopes to contain the entering class to about 3,700 students.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> In the school year 2010-11, UCSC enrolled 16,451 undergraduates and 1,452 graduate and postgraduate students, for a student body total of 17,903.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> In general, graduation and retention rates are above national averages but below the mean among UC campuses. Among students who entered in 1999, 70% graduated within six years, ten percentage points below the UC average. Earlier statistics show that the six-year graduation rate is above the mean for both NCAA Division I schools and a sample of major universities throughout the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> About half of graduates pursue further education, and 13 percent proceed to advanced degree programs within six months of graduation.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grading">Grading</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grading">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>For most of its history, UCSC employed a unique <a href="/wiki/Grading_in_the_United_States" title="Grading in the United States" class="mw-redirect">student evaluation</a> system. With the exception of the choice of letter grades in science courses the only <a href="/wiki/Grade_(education)" title="Grade (education)" class="mw-redirect">grades</a> assigned were &quot;pass&quot; and &quot;no record&quot;, supplemented with <a href="/wiki/Narrative_evaluation" title="Narrative evaluation">narrative evaluations</a>. Beginning in 1997, UCSC allowed students the option of selecting letter grade evaluations, but course grades were still optional until 2000, when faculty voted to require students receive letter grades. Students were still given narrative evaluations to complement the letter grades. As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the narrative evaluations were deemed an unnecessary expenditure. Still, some professors write evaluations for all students while some would write evaluations for specific students upon request.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Students can still elect to receive a &quot;pass/no pass&quot; grade, but many academic programs limit or even forbid pass/no pass grading. A grade of C and above would receive a grade of &quot;pass&quot;. Overall, students may now earn no more than 25% of their UCSC credits on a &quot;pass/no pass&quot; basis. Although the default grading option for almost all courses offered is now &quot;graded&quot;, most course grades are still accompanied by written evaluations.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/McHenry_Library" title="McHenry Library">McHenry Library</a> houses UCSC's arts and letters collection, with most of the scientific reading at the newer Science and Engineering Library. The McHenry Library was designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Carl_Warnecke" title="John Carl Warnecke">John Carl Warnecke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, the colleges host smaller libraries, which serve as quiet places to study. The McHenry Special Collections Library includes the archives of <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>, the largest collection of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a> photographs in the country, the <a href="/wiki/Mycology" title="Mycology">mycology</a> book collection of composer <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a>, a large collection of works by <a href="/wiki/Satyajit_Ray" title="Satyajit Ray">Satyajit Ray</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hayden_White" title="Hayden White">Hayden White</a> collection of 16th century Italian printing, a photography collection with nearly half a million items, and the Mary Lea Shane Archive. The latter contains an extensive collection of photographs, letters, and other documents related to <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick Observatory</a> dating back to 1870.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A 82,000-square-foot (7,600&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) new addition to the library opened on March 31, 2008, including a &quot;cyber study&quot; room and a Global Village caf&eacute;. The original 144,000-square-foot (13,400&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) library reopened on June 22, 2011 after seismic upgrades and other renovations.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grateful_Dead_archive">Grateful Dead archive</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Grateful Dead archive">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>In 2008, UCSC agreed to house the <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a> archives at the <a href="/wiki/McHenry_Library" title="McHenry Library">McHenry Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC plans to devote an entire room at the library, to be called &quot;Dead Central,&quot; to display the collection and encourage research.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC beat out petitions from Stanford and UC Berkeley to house the archives. Grateful Dead guitarist <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weir" title="Bob Weir">Bob Weir</a> said that UCSC is &quot;a seat of neo-Bohemian culture that we're a facet of. There could not have been a cozier place for this collection to land.&quot;<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> Silicon Valley entrepreneurs and Dead fans <a href="/wiki/Roger_McNamee" title="Roger McNamee">Roger McNamee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Watkins_(Seagate)" title="Bill Watkins (Seagate)">Bill Watkins</a> are expected to join a committee to oversee and raise funds for the project.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> The archive became open to the public July 29, 2012.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>According to a 2002 study of first year students, most students come from <a href="/wiki/Affluent" title="Affluent" class="mw-redirect">affluent</a> backgrounds and are more likely to identify as <a href="/wiki/Liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Liberalism in the United States">liberal</a> than the <a href="/wiki/Average_Joe" title="Average Joe">national average</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CIRP_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIRP-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Median_household_income" title="Median household income">median household income</a> UCSC students reported for their families of origin was $80,600, roughly 87.5% above the national average in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-CIRP_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIRP-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> In terms of political orientation, the student body was far more liberal than the general U.S. population, but more <a href="/wiki/Centrism" title="Centrism">centrist</a> than the national average for professors.<sup id="cite_ref-CIRP_84-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIRP-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> The majority of respondents, 59%, identified as liberal, 34% as &quot;Middle of the Road&quot; and 8% as <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CIRP_84-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIRP-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> Though UCSC students come from throughout the United States and the world, a large majority are from California.      <p>UCSC students are known for political activism. In 2005, a <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a> surveillance program deemed student opposition to <a href="/wiki/Military_recruiter" title="Military recruiter" class="mw-redirect">military recruiters</a> on campus a &quot;credible threat,&quot; the only campus <a href="/wiki/Antimilitarism" title="Antimilitarism">antiwar</a> action to receive the designation.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> In February 2006, Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Denice_Denton" title="Denice Denton">Denice Denton</a> got the designation removed.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> Military recruiters declined to return to UCSC the following year, but returned in 2008 to a more low-keyed student reception and protests using elements of <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_theatre" title="Guerrilla theatre">guerrilla theatre</a>, rather than vandalism or physical violence.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Thanks to students passing a $3 quarterly tuition increase to support buying renewable energy in 2006, UCSC is the sixth-largest buyer of renewable energy among college campuses nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>UC Santa Cruz is also well known for its <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> culture. On April 20, 2007, approximately 2,000 UCSC students gathered at Porter Meadow to celebrate the annual &quot;<a href="/wiki/420_(cannabis_culture)" title="420 (cannabis culture)">420</a>&quot;. Students and others openly smoked marijuana while campus police stood by.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> The once student-only event has grown since the city of Santa Cruz passed <a href="/wiki/Measure_K_2006" title="Measure K 2006" class="mw-redirect">Measure K</a> in 2006, an ordinance making marijuana use a low-priority crime for police. The 2007 event attracted a total of 5,000 participants. The university does not condone the gathering, but has taken steps to regulate the event and ensure security for all participants. On April 20, 2010, the school administration shut down the west entrance to campus and limited the amount of buses that could drive through campus.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Another well known tradition is what is known as &quot;<a href="/wiki/First_Rain" title="First Rain">First Rain</a>&quot;. Students run around campus naked or nearly naked to celebrate the school year's first night of heavy rain. The run begins at Porter and proceeds to travel through all the other colleges, finishing with skinny dipping at OPERS swimming pool.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_government">Student government</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Student government">edit</a>]</span></h3>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing">Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Housing">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>Most of the UCSC undergraduate housing is affiliated with one of the ten residential colleges. The residence halls, which include both shared and private rooms, typically house fifteen to twenty students per floor and have common bathrooms and lounge areas. Some halls have coed floors where men and women share bathroom facilities, others have separate bathroom facilities for men and women. Single-gender, gender-neutral and substance-free floors are also available.</p> 
     <p>All of the colleges, except for Kresge, have both residence halls and apartments. Kresge is all apartments. Apartments are typically shared by four to eight students, have common living/dining rooms, kitchens and bathrooms, and a combination of shared and private bedrooms. Apartments at colleges other than Kresge are generally reserved for students above the freshman level.</p> 
     <p>In addition to the residential colleges, housing is available at the Village on the lower quarry, populated by continuing, transfer, and graduate students; the University Inn, a remodeled hotel in downtown Santa Cruz that accommodates all students; Redwood Grove, formerly Kresge East, now available to applicants from all colleges; and the University Town Center, also located downtown, that primarily serves international students. Graduate Student Housing is available near Science Hill, and UCSC also offers Family Student Housing units as well as a Camper Park for student-owned trailers and RVs.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_life">Greek life</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Greek life">edit</a>]</span></h3> 
     <p>UCSC is home to few fraternities and sororities. The first Greek organization on campus, <a href="/wiki/Theta_Chi" title="Theta Chi">Theta Chi</a>, was given colony status on January 10, 1987 and chartered on October 14, 1989 (designation: Theta Iota). In the beginning, fraternities like <a href="/wiki/Theta_Chi" title="Theta Chi">Theta Chi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Alpha_Epsilon" title="Sigma Alpha Epsilon">Sigma Alpha Epsilon</a> were met with strong opposition from the student body. Student groups like P.A.C. (People's Alternative Community), S.A.G.E. (Students Against Greek Environments), and M.A.C. (Men's Alternative Community) protested the existence of Greek life at the UCSC campus.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2003, the campus gained national recognition when MTV's reality show <i><a href="/wiki/Fraternity_Life" title="Fraternity Life">Fraternity Life</a></i> documented the lives of students pledging Delta Omega Chi. During the shooting, a few of the students fished a 15-year-old koi named Midas out of a pond at Porter College, barbecued it, and ate it. Since the incident was captured on video, the individuals involved were charged with misdemeanor grand theft and vandalism.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>However, since this time, Greek life has flourished at UCSC, making way for fraternities like <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Lambda_Beta" title="Sigma Lambda Beta">Sigma Lambda Beta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_Kappa_Epsilon" title="Tau Kappa Epsilon">Tau Kappa Epsilon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Pi" title="Sigma Pi">Sigma Pi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Phi_Epsilon" title="Lambda Phi Epsilon">Lambda Phi Epsilon</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma_Phi_Zeta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sigma Phi Zeta (page does not exist)">Sigma Phi Zeta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Epsilon_Pi" title="Alpha Epsilon Pi">Alpha Epsilon Pi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pi_Alpha_Phi" title="Pi Alpha Phi">Pi Alpha Phi</a>. Sororities on campus include <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Alpha_Epsilon_Pi" title="Sigma Alpha Epsilon Pi">Sigma Alpha Epsilon Pi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Sigma_Theta" title="Delta Sigma Theta">Delta Sigma Theta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Kappa_Delta_Phi" title="Alpha Kappa Delta Phi">alpha Kappa Delta Phi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Phi_Beta" title="Gamma Phi Beta">Gamma Phi Beta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Kappa_Gamma" title="Kappa Kappa Gamma">Kappa Kappa Gamma</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma_Pi_Alpha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sigma Pi Alpha (page does not exist)">Sigma Pi Alpha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Lambda_Gamma" title="Sigma Lambda Gamma">Sigma Lambda Gamma</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tri_Chi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tri Chi (page does not exist)">Tri Chi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Omicron_Pi" title="Sigma Omicron Pi">Sigma Omicron Pi</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kappa_Zeta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kappa Zeta (page does not exist)">Kappa Zeta</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Psi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alpha Psi (page does not exist)">Alpha Psi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Theta_Alpha" title="Lambda Theta Alpha">Lambda Theta Alpha</a>.</p> 
     <p>Aside from social fraternities and sororities on campus, there are also a number of professional organizations as well. There are <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Gamma_Delta" title="Kappa Gamma Delta">Kappa Gamma Delta</a>, a prehealth sorority, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Mu_Delta" title="Sigma Mu Delta">Sigma Mu Delta</a>, a prehealth fraternity, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi_Omega" title="Alpha Phi Omega">Alpha Phi Omega</a>, a coed service fraternity, <a href="/wiki/Phi_Alpha_Delta" title="Phi Alpha Delta">Phi Alpha Delta</a>, a pre-law fraternity, and <a href="/wiki/Delta_Sigma_Pi" title="Delta Sigma Pi">Delta Sigma Pi</a>, a co-ed professional business fraternity.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The school has also has progressed to include <a href="/wiki/Delta_Lambda_Psi" title="Delta Lambda Psi">Delta Lambda Psi</a>, the nation's first gender neutral queer Greek organization established in 2005.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <p>Though UCSC has been known for its conservation efforts and environmentally minded students since it was founded, since 2000, enthusiasm for the UCSC sustainability movement has grown steadily among students and administrators alike. Students established the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sec.enviroslug.org/">Student Environmental Center (SEC)</a> in 2001, have held annual <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sec.enviroslug.org/earth-summit.html">Earth Summits</a>, and established a sustainability funding body, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://csc.enviroslug.org/">Campus Sustainability Council</a>. In 2004, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.universityofcalifornia.edu/policy.html">UC Policy on Sustainable Practices</a> was released, stating that the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ucop.edu/">University of California Office of the President</a> was committed to minimizing its impact on the environment and reducing its dependence on non-renewable energy. This set the scene for huge breakthroughs in 2006, when a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.ucsc.edu/governance/committees/css">Committee on Sustainability and Stewardship (CSS)</a> was established and a campus-wide Sustainability Assessment was completed. The following year, the pilot <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.ucsc.edu/">Sustainability Office</a> was created to help institutionalize sustainability, coordinate communication and collaboration between the many entities already engaged in campus sustainability activities at UCSC, support policy implementation, and serve as a resource for the campus.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> Sustainability has become a major part of every aspect of the campus, and students, staff, and faculty campus-wide are working toward sustainability in a variety of different ways.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a>]</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:UCSC_East_Field.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/UCSC_East_Field.jpg/220px-UCSC_East_Field.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/UCSC_East_Field.jpg/330px-UCSC_East_Field.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/UCSC_East_Field.jpg/440px-UCSC_East_Field.jpg 2x" /></a>

     <p>UCSC competes in <a href="/wiki/Division_III_(NCAA)" title="Division III (NCAA)">Division III</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> as an Independent member. There are twelve <a href="/wiki/Varsity_team" title="Varsity team">varsity</a> sports (men's and women's basketball, tennis, soccer, volleyball, swimming and <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving</a>, women's golf, and women's <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross country</a>). UCSC teams are nationally ranked in tennis, soccer, men's volleyball, and swimming. After defeating Emory to win the 2007 National Championship in men's tennis, UCSC has won six men's tennis team championships, and have been defending champions in tennis for two of the past three years.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup> The Banana Slugs were also runners-up in men's soccer in 2004. In the 2006 season, the men's water polo team won the Division III championship, as well as an overall ranking of 19th in the nation. However, both the men and women's water polo teams were cut in 2008 due to budget constraints.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC is one of the largest but one of the least funded NCAA Division III members.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In addition to its NCAA sports, UCSC maintains a number of successful club sides including its women's <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a> team, which won the <a href="/wiki/Division_II_(NCAA)" title="Division II (NCAA)">Division II</a> National Collegiate Championship during its '05–'06 season and has competed at Nationals several times since, most recently in 2010 and 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup> UCSC also fields an often victorious men's lacrosse team, which competes against other western universities in the WCLL. After a highly successful 2008 season, the team traveled to Texas for nationals. Although UCSC never had a track, the residential colleges regularly competed in an improvised &quot;Slug Run&quot; every spring from 1967 to 1982,<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup> though the Run now is a community event and fundraiser event hosted by the cross-country club for much needed fund to pay for entry fees, hotel, and transportation to race.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup> Approximately 25% of the student population participates in intramural athletics, which tend to be better funded than the intercollegiate athletic programs.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascot">Mascot</span><span class="mw-editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Mascot">edit</a>]</span></h3>

         <a href="/wiki/File:Banana_slug_at_UCSC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf9/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a>

     <p>UCSC's mascot is the <a href="/wiki/Banana_slug" title="Banana slug">banana slug</a><sup id="cite_ref-UCSC_Mascot_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCSC_Mascot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> (specifically, <i>Ariolimax dolichophallus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1981, when the university began participating in NCAA intercollegiate sports, the then-chancellor and some student athletes declared the mascot to be the &quot;<a href="/wiki/Sea_Lion" title="Sea Lion" class="mw-redirect">sea lions</a>.&quot; Most students disliked the new mascot and offered an alternative mascot, the banana slug. In 1986, students voted via <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> to declare the banana slug the official mascot of UCSC – a vote the chancellor refused to honor, arguing that only athletes should choose the mascot. When a poll of athletes showed that they, too, wanted to be &quot;Slugs,&quot; the chancellor relented. The June 16, 1986, issue of <i>People</i> magazine featured a full-page spread dedicated to the selection of the Banana Slug as the official mascot of UCSC (see <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://i.imgur.com/GQ6tw.jpg">page 85</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119"><span>[</span>119<span>]</span></a></sup> A sea lion statue can still be seen in front of the Thimann Hall lecture building.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120"><span>[</span>120<span>]</span></a></sup> In February 2008, ESPN Sports Travel named the UCSC Banana Slug as one of the ten best nicknames in college basketball.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121"><span>[</span>121<span>]</span></a></sup> The &quot;Fiat Slug&quot; logo prominently featured on campus is a trademark of UCSC owned by the Regents. It was developed by two students during the mascot controversy, who later incorporated as &quot;Oxford West&quot; and licensed their design from the Regents to produce clothing inspired by the university.</p> 
     <p>The slug also is featured along with the school's logo on <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Vega" title="Vincent Vega" class="mw-redirect">Vincent Vega</a>'s T-shirt during the 1994 film <i><a href="/wiki/Pulp_Fiction" title="Pulp Fiction">Pulp Fiction</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122"><span>[</span>122<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
